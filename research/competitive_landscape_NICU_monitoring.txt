1. NON-CONTACT VITAL SIGN MONITORING SYSTEMS
=====================================================================

A 2025 systematic review in Pediatric Research reviewed 60 observational studies:
- 43 (72%) non-contact technologies
- 17 (28%) wireless wearable sensors
- Most emerging non-contact technologies focus on respiratory rate monitoring
Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41390-025-04469-0

A comprehensive 2024 Frontiers in Pediatrics review categorized sensors into:
- Image-based visible (RGB cameras)
- Image-based infrared (thermal cameras)
- Radar-based sensors
Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11528303/

KEY MODALITIES:

A. Radar-Based Systems
-----------------------
- Ultra-wideband (UWB) radar: Emits signals that penetrate clothing/bedding to detect
  chest wall movements for respiratory and heart rate monitoring
- Impulse-radio UWB (IR-UWB): Demonstrated ability to continuously track respiratory
  rates with minimal error
- FMCW radar: Low-cost proof-of-concept verified in lab (May 2024), implementation
  began December 2024, results expected Q1 2025
  Source: https://www.researchprotocols.org/2025/1/e59532

- RWTH Aachen radar research: CW radar demonstrated >97% accuracy at 10 bpm for
  minimal movements. Average RMSE of 4.3 bpm. Novel random body movement mitigation
  technique developed.
  Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-08836-3

- MEDUSA System (UW-Madison / Georgia Tech): First-of-its-kind contactless multi-radar
  vital-sign sensing system. Uses multiple radar units throughout a room to create a
  multiview system. Published at ACM MobiCom 2025. Announced January 29, 2026.
  Source: https://cdis.wisc.edu/uw-madison-researchers-pioneer-contactless-health-monitoring/

B. Camera-Based RGB / Photoplethysmography (PPG)
--------------------------------------------------
- Imaging photoplethysmography (iPPG): Analyzes subtle skin color changes caused by
  blood flow variations to determine heart rate and SpO2 without physical attachment
- SurePulse VS: Commercial wireless cap using fhPPG for heart rate monitoring in
  delivery room and NICU (see Company Profiles below)
- Continuous camera-based premature-infant monitoring algorithms developed
  Source: https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/11/16/7215

C. Infrared Thermography (IRT)
-------------------------------
- Gauges infant temperature distribution without physical interaction
- Leverages small temperature variations around the nose during breathing to estimate
  respiratory flow and motion
- Can differentiate between different types of apnea
- Abdominal thermal imaging used for NEC detection (>90% classification accuracy)
- Core-peripheral temperature difference (CPTD) serves as key biomarker for hemodynamic
  status and early warning for shock/sepsis
  - Thermal gradient alterations: adjusted OR for late-onset sepsis = 23.60
    (95% CI 6.80-81.88), sensitivity 83%, NPV 94%
  - In 71% of cases, thermal gradient alteration was the FIRST clinical sign of sepsis

D. Multimodal Fusion Systems
------------------------------
- MIT thesis (2025): Combined video camera + radar for neonatal vital signs using
  Video Vision Transformer (ViViT) architecture. Clinical study at Tufts Medical Center
  NICU (16 neonates). Multimodal fusion outperformed unimodal approaches.
  Source: https://dspace.mit.edu/handle/1721.1/163443

- RWTH Aachen camera fusion: Deep learning-based fusion of thermal and RGB cameras for
  real-time temperature monitoring
  Source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11517-022-02561-9

E. Piezoelectric / Under-Mattress Systems
-------------------------------------------
- EarlySense (now under Hillrom/Baxter): Contactless piezoelectric sensor pad placed
  under mattress. Detects ballistic vibrations from respiratory chest movements.
  - 470 hours of data from 109 neonates
  - High HR events: 81% sensitivity, 99% specificity
  - High RR events: 83% sensitivity, 83% specificity
  Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-07189-1

2. COMPANY PROFILES & PRODUCTS
=====================================================================

A. Masimo
----------
Status: Major incumbent player in NICU monitoring
Products:
- RD SET Blue sensor: Continuous noninvasive SpO2 and pulse rate (3% ARMS accuracy)
- Sidestream CO2 monitoring with NomoLine (50 ml/min sampling rate for neonates)
- SofTouch sensors: Minimal adhesive for fragile newborn skin
- NeoPt-500: Non-adhesive sensor for neonates <1 kg
- Rainbow Acoustic Monitoring (RAM): RAS-45 sensor for respiratory rate up to 120 bpm
- Stork: FDA-cleared OTC baby monitoring system (2024) for home use (0-18 months)
- Partnership with March of Dimes for NICU-to-home transition
Website: https://www.masimo.com/solutions/perioperative/NICU/
NOTE: No thermal imaging products. Focus is on pulse oximetry, capnography, acoustic monitoring.

B. Neopenda
------------
Status: Startup (founded 2015, Columbia University)
Product: neoGuard - Wearable monitoring 4 vital signs (HR, respiration, SpO2, temperature)
- Contact-based skin temperature sensor (NOT thermal imaging)
- Designed for resource-constrained settings
- Works up to 36 hours on a charge
- Central tablet displays up to 15 monitors simultaneously
- Target price: ~$50/unit (vs $2,500 for typical US monitors)
- FDA Class II (not yet fully cleared for US clinical use)
Website: https://www.neopenda.com/
NOTE: No thermal imaging. Temperature via skin contact thermistor.

C. Sentec
----------
Status: Established company, transcutaneous monitoring specialist
Product: Sentec Digital Monitor - Transcutaneous CO2 monitoring
- Non-invasive continuous CO2 monitoring
- Site times up to 8 hours
- Low operating temperature to protect fragile skin
- Reduces frequency of blood draws and heel sticks
- Works with all ventilation methods (high frequency, high flow, bubble CPAP)
Website: https://www.sentec.com/
NOTE: No thermal imaging. Focus is on transcutaneous CO2/O2 monitoring.

D. Owlet Baby Care
-------------------
Status: Consumer/prescription baby monitoring
Products:
- Dream Sock: FDA De Novo clearance (Nov 2023), OTC pulse oximeter for home use
- BabySat: FDA-cleared (June 2023), prescription-only, clinical-grade monitoring
  - SpO2 within 2% of Masimo Rad-97
  - Heart rate within 3.5 bpm
- NICU study: Tested on 21 preterm/low-birth-weight infants; strong correlation with
  Masimo wrap probe, but not validated for inpatient use yet
Website: https://owletcare.com/
NOTE: No thermal imaging. Contact-based pulse oximetry (smart sock).

E. EarlySense (now part of Hillrom/Baxter)
--------------------------------------------
Status: Acquired by Hillrom (2021), then Baxter acquired Hillrom
Product: Contactless piezoelectric sensor placed under mattress
- Monitors HR, respiratory rate, and bed motion without skin contact
- Clinically tested in NICU settings (Kenya study, 2020)
- No thermal imaging capability
Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-07189-1

F. Sibel Health
----------------
Status: Startup from Northwestern University (Querrey Simpson Institute for Bioelectronics)
Product: ANNE One Platform
- FDA 510(k) cleared (May 2023) for neonates of any gestational age to 2 years
- Wireless wearable sensors: HR, respiratory rate, skin temp, body temp, SpO2,
  apnea-hypopnea index, sleep time, body position, chest wall movement
- Collaboration with Montreal Children's Hospital / McGill University to create
  "world's first wireless NICU"
- Designed for fragile neonatal skin
Website: https://sibelhealth.com/
NOTE: No thermal imaging. Wireless wearable contact sensors.

G. SurePulse Medical
---------------------
Status: Commercial product for delivery room and NICU
Product: SurePulse VS - Wireless cap-mounted heart rate monitor
- Uses focal head photoplethysmography (fhPPG) - camera-based, contactless
- Median 20 seconds from deployment to HR signal
- R^2 = 0.89 correlation with ECG
- HCP reliability rating: 80% "Always/Mostly" reliable
- 51% deployments at delivery, 47% in neonatal unit
- Promotes parent-infant bonding (no wires)
Website: https://www.surepulsemedical.com/
Source: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11140120/
NOTE: Camera-based heart rate only. No thermal imaging.

H. Bambi Medical
-----------------
Status: European startup (Eindhoven, Netherlands)
Product: Bambi Belt - Wearable silicone belt for neonates
- Monitors ECG heart rate and dEMG for apnea detection
- No adhesives (placed below ribcage)
- CE marked (European market)
- Clinical trials: 40 babies from 26 weeks gestation
- No FDA clearance yet
Website: https://www.bambi-medical.com/
NOTE: No thermal imaging. Contact-based belt sensor.

I. raybaby
-----------
Status: Consumer/clinical startup
Product: raybaby - Non-contact radar-based health monitoring
- Uses radiosomnography (low-power radio waves)
- Works from up to 5 ft distance
- NICU study: 760 hours monitoring, 18 preterm babies, 98% accuracy for respiratory
  rate, 95% of data within +/- 5 units error
- Compared favorably to FDA-approved EarlySense unit
Website: https://raybaby.us/
NOTE: No thermal imaging. Radar-based respiratory monitoring.

- GE HealthCare: AI/IoT integration, Novii+ beltless maternal monitoring
- Draeger: High-quality ventilation and monitoring systems

6. CLINICAL GUIDELINES & POSITION STATEMENTS
=====================================================================

A. WHO Guidelines
------------------
- WHO Thermal Protection of the Newborn (1997): Recommends maintaining normothermia
  36.5-37.5 C with frequent temperature monitoring
- WHO does NOT currently mention or endorse thermal imaging/infrared thermography
  as a temperature monitoring method
  Source: https://www.healthynewbornnetwork.org/hnn-content/uploads/k.-WHO-1997.-Thermal-protection-of-the-newborn.pdf

B. Current Standard Practice
------------------------------
- Axillary, rectal, or skin-probe thermometry remains the clinical standard
- No major professional society (AAP, RCPCH, etc.) has issued guidelines or position
  statements endorsing thermal imaging for routine neonatal monitoring

C. Barriers to Guideline Adoption
------------------------------------
- No large prospective studies examining both benefits and barriers
- Methodological challenges: environmental parameters, incubator effects, radiant
  warmers interfere with readings
- Need for standardized protocols and calibration methods
- Small sample sizes in existing studies
- Risk of bias concerns in current literature
  Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6814124/

D. Emerging Evidence Supporting Future Guidelines
----------------------------------------------------
- 2025 paper on automated CPTD monitoring via thermal imaging shows promise for
  sepsis detection (OR 23.60, sensitivity 83%, NPV 94%)
- IRT calibration in incubators improved to MAE <0.3 C in 93.1% of data
- Japanese study (2024) demonstrated continuous thermographic monitoring of heat
  oscillations in neonates

BOTTOM LINE: Thermal imaging is NOT yet in any clinical guidelines for neonatal care.
This creates both a challenge (regulatory/adoption hurdle) and an opportunity (first-mover
advantage for establishing the evidence base).

STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS FOR NEOTHERM
=====================================================================

1. NO DIRECT COMMERCIAL COMPETITOR exists for dedicated thermal camera
   monitoring in the NICU. This is a clear white space.

2. The market is moving strongly toward contactless/wireless monitoring,
   creating favorable tailwinds.

3. Key differentiation opportunities for NeoTherm:
   - Only thermal imaging-focused NICU monitoring system
   - Multi-application potential (temperature, NEC detection, sepsis/CPTD,
     respiratory monitoring, thermoregulation assessment)
   - Non-contact = zero skin damage risk
   - Continuous monitoring vs. intermittent measurement

4. Competitive threats:
   - Large incumbents (Masimo, GE, Draeger) could add thermal imaging
     to existing platforms
   - Radar-based systems (MEDUSA, raybaby) compete for the "contactless"
     value proposition but don't measure temperature
   - Academic groups (RWTH Aachen, Duke, CHEO, Selcuk) could potentially
     spin out competitors

5. Regulatory pathway is uncertain:
   - No FDA-cleared thermal imaging device for NICU exists
   - Need to establish evidence base for clinical guidelines
   - De Novo classification pathway likely needed

6. Partnership opportunities:
   - Academic centers seeking commercialization partners
   - Incumbent companies seeking to add thermal capabilities
   - NICU network studies (NICHD NRN) for multi-center validation
