THERMAL/INFRARED IMAGING FOR BRAIN-RELATED CONDITIONS IN NEONATES AND INFANTS
Published Research Papers -- Compiled 2026-03-14
================================================================================

This document compiles published research on using thermal/infrared cameras and
non-invasive thermal measurements for detecting and monitoring brain-related
conditions in neonates and infants, including HIE, therapeutic hypothermia
monitoring, cerebral blood flow assessment, and fontanelle-based brain
temperature measurement.

================================================================================
PAPER 1 -- Thermal Index for Brain Injury Assessment (Clinical Study)
================================================================================

Title:    Thermal Index for early non-invasive assessment of brain injury in
          newborns treated with therapeutic hypothermia: preliminary report
Authors:  Walas W, Maczko A, Halaba Z, Bandola D, Ostrowski Z, Rojczyk M,
          Nowak AJ
Year:     2021
Journal:  Scientific Reports, 11, 12578
DOI:      10.1038/s41598-021-92139-6
URL:      https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-92139-6

Key Findings:
- Evaluated the usefulness of a novel "Thermal Index" (TI) for early,
  non-invasive assessment of the degree of brain damage in newborns undergoing
  therapeutic hypothermia due to perinatal asphyxia.
- The TI is derived from the newborn's heat balance calculated using only
  non-invasive thermal measurements (skin surface temperatures, environmental
  temperature, cooling device parameters).
- This preliminary clinical report demonstrates that the Thermal Index shows
  promise as an accessible physiological biomarker and early predictor of brain
  injury severity and mortality in neonates with moderate or severe HIE,
  correlating with MRI-diagnosed injury patterns.

Relevance to NeoTherm: DIRECTLY RELEVANT. Demonstrates that surface thermal
measurements can serve as a biomarker for brain injury severity in neonates --
exactly the type of non-invasive thermal assessment that infrared camera
technology could automate and enhance.

PAPER 2 -- Theoretical Basis for Thermal Brain Injury Assessment
================================================================================

Title:    Theoretical basis for the use of non-invasive thermal measurements
          to assess the brain injury in newborns undergoing therapeutic
          hypothermia
Authors:  Walas W, Bandola D, Ostrowski Z, Rojczyk M, Maczko A, Halaba Z,
          Nowak AJ
Year:     2020
Journal:  Scientific Reports, 10, 22167
DOI:      10.1038/s41598-020-79009-3
URL:      https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-020-79009-3

Key Findings:
- Proposes a new non-invasive methodology to estimate thermogenesis (metabolic
  heat production) in newborns with perinatal asphyxia undergoing therapeutic
  hypothermia.
- Metabolic heat production is calculated from the newborn's heat balance using
  results of ONLY non-invasive thermal measurements -- no invasive probes
  required.
- The measurement system works with standard therapeutic hypothermia equipment
  and includes GSM connectivity for remote monitoring without disturbing medical
  personnel. Demonstrates that the temperature of the active cooling medium
  correlates with the severity of brain injury diagnosed by MRI.

Relevance to NeoTherm: DIRECTLY RELEVANT. Provides the theoretical and
mathematical foundation showing that non-invasive surface thermal measurements
can be used to infer brain metabolic state and injury severity.

PAPER 3 -- Radiometric Thermometry for Brain Temperature in Asphyxiated Newborns
================================================================================

Title:    Non-invasive measurement of brain temperature using radiometric
          thermometry: experimental validation and clinical observations in
          asphyxiated newborns
Authors:  Bass WT, et al.
Year:     2014
Journal:  Journal of Neonatal-Perinatal Medicine, 7(4), 279-286
DOI:      10.3233/NPM-14814041
URL:      https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25468621/

Key Findings:
- Validated radiometric thermometry (RadT) sensors against direct tissue
  measurements in a swine model cooled to 33-34 degrees C, finding RadT
  measurements comparable to invasive fiber optic probes at multiple brain
  depths.
- Applied RadT non-invasively in 30 newborn infants with HIE undergoing
  therapeutic hypothermia. Found approximately 1 degree C increase in brain
  temperature relative to rectal temperature in 60% of infants.
- Critically, an increase in brain temperature measured by the RadT sensors
  was significantly associated with the presence of cerebral white matter
  injury (p=0.01), suggesting brain temperature elevation is a marker for
  injury.

Relevance to NeoTherm: HIGHLY RELEVANT. Demonstrates that non-invasive
thermal measurement of the neonatal brain can detect temperature elevations
associated with white matter injury, supporting the concept that thermal
signatures can indicate brain pathology.

PAPER 4 -- Brain Temperature Discriminates Between Brain Conditions (Landmark)
================================================================================

Title:    Brain temperature discriminates between neonates with damaged,
          hypoperfused, and normal brains
Authors:  Simbruner G, Nanz S, Fleischhacker E, Derganc M
Year:     1994
Journal:  American Journal of Perinatology, 11(2), 137-143
DOI:      10.1055/s-2007-994574
URL:      https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8198656/

Key Findings:
- Landmark study measuring core head temperature noninvasively (zero-gradient
  method) in 16 neonates: 7 healthy, 7 with cerebral damage, and 2 with
  cerebral hypoperfusion from congenital heart disease.
- The difference between core head temperature and esophageal temperature was
  0.72 +/- 0.12 degrees C in normal neonates, only 0.16 +/- 0.4 degrees C in
  infants with cerebral damage, and 0.9-1.2 degrees C in infants with cerebral
  hypoperfusion.
- Brain temperature pattern correctly discriminated between all infants
  according to their brain condition, except one with mild HIE. Demonstrates
  that brain temperature depends on cerebral perfusion and level of brain
  injury.

Relevance to NeoTherm: FOUNDATIONAL. This seminal paper establishes that
non-invasive head temperature measurement can distinguish between normal,
damaged, and hypoperfused neonatal brains -- the core scientific premise
underlying thermal imaging for neonatal brain assessment.

PAPER 5 -- Regional Brain Temperature During Whole-Body Cooling (MRS study)
================================================================================

Title:    An In Vivo Assessment of Regional Brain Temperature during
          Whole-Body Cooling for Neonatal Encephalopathy
Authors:  Wu TW, Wisnowski JL, Geisler RF, Reitman A, Ho E, Tamrazi B,
          Chapman R, Bluml S
Year:     2020
Journal:  The Journal of Pediatrics, 220, 73-79.e3
DOI:      10.1016/j.jpeds.2020.01.019
URL:      https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32089332/

Key Findings:
- Used MR spectroscopy thermometry to assess regional brain temperatures during
  whole-body therapeutic hypothermia in neonates with HIE, measuring basal
  ganglia, thalamus, cortical gray matter, and white matter temperatures.
- Found that whole-body hypothermia was effective in cooling deep brain
  structures, but superficial structures were warmer and significantly higher
  than rectal temperature.
- White matter (superficial) temperature did not correlate well with rectal
  temperature, suggesting that standard rectal monitoring may not accurately
  reflect brain surface temperature -- highlighting the need for direct brain
  temperature monitoring.

Relevance to NeoTherm: HIGHLY RELEVANT. Demonstrates that superficial brain
temperature differs from core body temperature during therapeutic hypothermia,
meaning surface-facing thermal imaging (e.g., through the fontanelle or
scalp) could provide unique, clinically valuable information not captured
by standard monitoring.

PAPER 6 -- Brain Temperature Monitoring Review (Comprehensive)
================================================================================

Title:    Brain temperature monitoring in newborn infants: Current
          methodologies and prospects
Authors:  Verma V, Lange F, Bainbridge A, Harvey-Jones K, Robertson NJ,
          Tachtsidis I, Mitra S
Year:     2022
Journal:  Frontiers in Pediatrics, 10, 1008539
DOI:      10.3389/fped.2022.1008539
URL:      https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pediatrics/articles/10.3389/fped.2022.1008539/full

Key Findings:
- Comprehensive review of all current methods for monitoring neonatal brain
  temperature, including MRS thermometry (accuracy ~0.2 degrees C, precision
  0.3 degrees C), near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) for continuous cot-side
  monitoring, zero-heat-flux thermometry, and radiometric thermometry.
- Emphasizes that perinatal brain injuries (neonatal encephalopathy, seizures)
  induce changes in brain temperature due to altered metabolic and hemodynamic
  states, making brain temperature a clinically important indicator.
- Argues that non-invasive, continuous brain temperature measurement in the
  neonatal neurocritical care setting could enable selection of candidates who
  may benefit from adjusted hypothermia therapy or additional neuroprotective
  therapies.

Relevance to NeoTherm: ESSENTIAL REFERENCE. Provides the definitive review
of the field, validating the clinical need for non-invasive brain temperature
monitoring in neonates and cataloging all existing methodologies -- infrared
thermography-based approaches fit directly into this landscape.

PAPER 7 -- Thermal Imaging in Neonatal Care: Scoping Review
================================================================================

Title:    Thermal imaging applications in neonatal care: a scoping review
Authors:  Topalidou A, Ali N, Sekulic S, Downe S
Year:     2019
Journal:  BMC Pregnancy and Childbirth, 19, 388
DOI:      10.1186/s12884-019-2533-y
URL:      https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12884-019-2533-y

Key Findings:
- Systematic scoping review identifying five broad themes of thermal imaging
  in neonatal care: (1) general thermal physiology, (2) heat loss and
  respiratory monitoring, (3) identification of internal pathologies including
  NEC, (4) other uses, and (5) technical concerns.
- Concludes that thermal imaging can screen newborns for internal abnormalities
  because deep-lying structures in newborns have less insulation than in older
  patients. This is particularly relevant for brain assessment through the
  fontanelle.
- Notes that despite promising results since the 1970s, no large prospective
  studies have been conducted, and methodological challenges (incubator
  environments, radiant warmers) remain barriers to clinical adoption.

Relevance to NeoTherm: ESSENTIAL REFERENCE. The most comprehensive review
of thermal imaging specifically in neonatal care. Validates the feasibility
while identifying the research gaps NeoTherm could address.

PAPER 8 -- Cerebral Blood Flow and Brain Thermal Stability (2026)
================================================================================

Title:    Investigating Roles of Cerebral Blood Flow to Maintain Thermal
          Stability of Neonatal Brain Against Cold Stress Using Non-Invasive
          Probes for Brain Perfusion and Temperature Gradient
Authors:  Iwata S, Tsuda K, Kinoshita M, Saitoh S, Iwata O
Year:     2026
Journal:  Biosensors, 16(2), 127
DOI:      10.3390/bios16020127
URL:      https://www.mdpi.com/2079-6374/16/2/127

Key Findings:
- Used a multimodal non-invasive biosensor approach (infrared thermometry,
  zero-heat-flux thermometry, time-resolved NIRS, and Doppler ultrasound) to
  examine how cerebral blood flow influences neonatal brain temperature during
  mild cold stress (incubator-to-cot transfer) in 43 stable infants.
- Found that while rectal temperature remained stable during environmental
  temperature drops (29.7 to 26.8 degrees C), the relationship between
  cerebral blood flow (SVC flow) and superficial brain temperature shifted
  significantly, returning to baseline after 2 hours.
- Demonstrates that non-invasive cot-side biosensors can successfully monitor
  the relationship between brain perfusion and temperature, offering a
  clinically practical approach to understanding cerebral thermoregulation.

Relevance to NeoTherm: DIRECTLY RELEVANT. Most recent paper (2026) showing
that infrared thermometry combined with other non-invasive sensors can assess
brain thermal dynamics and perfusion in real-time at the cot-side.

PAPER 9 -- Infrared Thermography for Preterm Thermoregulation
================================================================================

Title:    Infrared thermography for detailed registration of thermoregulation
          in premature infants
Authors:  Heimann K, Jergus K, Abbas AK, Heussen N, Leonhardt S,
          Orlikowsky T
Year:     2013
Journal:  Journal of Perinatal Medicine, 41(5), 613-620
DOI:      10.1515/jpm-2012-0239
URL:      https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23443261/

Key Findings:
- Evaluated skin temperature using non-contact infrared thermography (IRT) in
  multiple body areas (leg, back, arm, head, upper abdomen) of 10 premature
  infants, comparing with two conventional temperature sensors.
- Demonstrated that IRT provides detailed spatial and temporal information
  about temperature regulation and distribution across the body, including the
  head, without requiring any contact with the vulnerable neonate.
- Suggested that IRT may reduce the need for skin thermistors in preterm
  temperature monitoring, providing richer thermoregulatory data while
  eliminating risks of skin damage from adhesive sensors.

Relevance to NeoTherm: RELEVANT. Validates infrared thermography as a viable
method for contactless neonatal temperature monitoring including the head
region, demonstrating the technical feasibility of the approach.

PAPER 10 -- Continuous Thermographic Monitoring of Heat Oscillations
================================================================================

Title:    Continuous monitoring using thermography can capture the heat
          oscillations maintaining body temperature in neonates
Authors:  Morimoto A, Nakamura S, Koyano K, et al.
Year:     2024
Journal:  Scientific Reports, 14, 10449
DOI:      10.1038/s41598-024-60718-y
URL:      https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-60718-y

Key Findings:
- Used contactless infrared thermography (IRT) to measure skin temperature
  every 15 seconds in 27 neonates in incubators, extracting two frequency
  components of heat oscillations using the Savitzky-Golay method.
- Demonstrated that continuous IRT can capture the spontaneous thermal
  metabolism of neonates, including cyclic heat oscillations that maintain
  body temperature equilibrium between metabolic heat production and
  environmental heat transfer.
- Provides evidence that continuous thermographic monitoring offers insights
  into neonatal metabolic status that cannot be obtained from intermittent
  temperature spot-checks.

Relevance to NeoTherm: RELEVANT. Demonstrates the power of continuous infrared
thermographic monitoring to capture dynamic metabolic information in neonates,
supporting the concept of using IR cameras for real-time physiological
assessment.

ADDITIONAL RELEVANT REFERENCE -- Brain-Eyelid Thermal Tunnel
================================================================================

Title:    Infrared Imaging of the Brain-Eyelid Thermal Tunnel: A Promising
          Method for Measuring Body Temperature in Afebrile Children
Authors:  (Multiple authors)
Year:     2023
Journal:  International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health,
          20(19), 6867
DOI:      (available at MDPI)
URL:      https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/20/19/6867

Key Findings:
- Identified the Brain Thermal Tunnel (BTT) as a pathway between the cavernous
  sinus and the superior medial orbit providing a non-invasive route for
  measuring internal brain temperature via infrared thermography.
- The skin overlying the BTT (~900 micrometers thick, fat-free) lacks thermal
  insulation, allowing high-intensity infrared emission that reflects
  underlying brain temperature.
- Demonstrates that BTT infrared thermography of the inner canthus of the
  eye reliably measures body/brain temperature, with potential applications
  for estimating brain temperature in cases of severe intracerebral brain
  injury.

Relevance to NeoTherm: RELEVANT. The brain thermal tunnel concept could be
adapted for neonatal brain temperature assessment, and the thin skin of the
neonatal fontanelle provides an even more direct thermal window to the brain.

SUMMARY OF KEY THEMES AND IMPLICATIONS FOR NEOTHERM
================================================================================

1. BRAIN INJURY DETECTION VIA THERMAL SIGNATURES:
   The Walas/Bandola group (Papers 1-2) has demonstrated that non-invasive
   thermal measurements during therapeutic hypothermia can predict brain injury
   severity, validated against MRI. The Thermal Index concept directly supports
   infrared camera-based brain assessment.

2. BRAIN TEMPERATURE AS A DIAGNOSTIC MARKER:
   Simbruner (1994, Paper 4) established that head temperature patterns
   distinguish between normal, damaged, and hypoperfused neonatal brains.
   Bass (2014, Paper 3) showed brain temperature elevation correlates with
   white matter injury. These findings provide the scientific basis for using
   thermal imaging as a diagnostic tool.

3. SUPERFICIAL VS. DEEP BRAIN TEMPERATURE:
   Wu et al. (2020, Paper 5) showed that superficial brain temperature
   diverges from rectal temperature during cooling, meaning surface thermal
   imaging provides unique information not captured by standard monitoring.

4. FONTANELLE AS A THERMAL WINDOW:
   Multiple papers note that neonates have less insulation over deep
   structures, and the fontanelle provides a direct, unossified window to
   brain thermal activity -- ideal for infrared thermography.

5. TECHNICAL FEASIBILITY:
   Papers 8-10 demonstrate that contactless infrared thermography can
   successfully measure and continuously monitor neonatal temperature at
   the cot-side with high temporal resolution, supporting clinical deployment.

6. GAP IN THE LITERATURE:
   No study has yet combined modern high-resolution infrared cameras with
   AI/machine learning to create a real-time, automated brain condition
   screening tool for neonates -- this is the exact gap NeoTherm can fill.

NOTE ON INTRACRANIAL HEMORRHAGE:
No published research was found specifically using infrared thermography for
direct detection of intracranial hemorrhage in neonates. Light-based optical
imaging (time-of-flight) has been explored for hemorrhage detection but uses
a different modality. This represents a potential research gap and opportunity.
