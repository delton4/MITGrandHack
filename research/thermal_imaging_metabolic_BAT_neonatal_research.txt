1. BMP8 AND ACTIVATED BROWN ADIPOSE TISSUE IN HUMAN NEWBORNS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Authors:  Urisarri A, González-García I, Estévez-Salguero Á, Pata MP,
          Milbank E, López N, Funcke J-B, Gerbert B, Perdomo G, Sonzogni SV,
          Martínez-Sánchez N, Kühne H, López-Rivero A, et al.
Year:     2021
Journal:  Nature Communications, 12, 5274
DOI:      https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-021-25456-z

Key findings:
- Applied non-invasive infrared thermography (IRT) to investigate BAT-induced
  thermogenesis in a cohort of 50 healthy human neonates, overcoming ethical
  limitations of PET-CT or SPECT in newborns.
- Demonstrated that BAT temperature (measured in the interscapular region)
  correlates with core body temperature, and that a mild cold stimulus promotes
  BAT activation in newborns.
- Found that a single short-term cold stimulus during the first day of life
  improves the body temperature adaptation to a subsequent cold event. Also
  identified BMP8 as a molecular marker associated with BAT activation in
  human neonates.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR NEOTHERM: This is the landmark paper demonstrating that
infrared thermography can non-invasively detect BAT activation in human
newborns. It validates the fundamental premise that thermal cameras can
assess metabolic thermogenic activity in neonates.


2. AN UPDATED VIEW ON HUMAN NEONATAL THERMOGENESIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Authors:  González-García I, López-Rivero A, Funcke J-B, López M
Year:     2022
Journal:  Nature Reviews Endocrinology, 18, 212–223
DOI:      https://doi.org/10.1038/s41574-022-00642-1

Key findings:
- Comprehensive review of mechanisms of non-shivering thermogenesis in human
  neonates, centered on brown adipose tissue (BAT) as the primary thermogenic
  organ after birth.
- Discusses how infrared thermography is a suitable non-invasive technique that
  can be safely applied to human babies to investigate BAT thermogenesis.
- Highlights that newborns' high surface-area-to-volume ratio increases heat
  loss, making BAT-mediated thermogenesis critical for survival, and that IRT
  can monitor this process in real time.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR NEOTHERM: Provides the authoritative endocrinological
framework linking BAT activation, metabolic rate, and thermal imaging in
neonates. Directly supports the scientific basis for using thermal cameras
to assess metabolic/endocrine status in newborns.


3. USE OF INFRARED THERMOGRAPHIC CALORIMETRY TO DETERMINE ENERGY EXPENDITURE
   IN PRETERM INFANTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Authors:  Heimann K, Peschgens T, Kwiecien R, Stanzel S, Hoernchen H,
          Merz U
Year:     2000
Journal:  American Journal of Clinical Nutrition, 71(4), 969–977
DOI:      https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/71.4.969
PubMed:   10731505

Key findings:
- Validated infrared thermographic calorimetry (ITC) against respiratory
  indirect calorimetry (IC) in 10 healthy preterm infants over 3.5–5.5 hour
  study periods.
- ITC measures mean infant body surface temperature via IR camera and uses heat
  loss theory to calculate radiant, convective, evaporative, and conductive
  heat losses to determine total energy expenditure.
- Concluded that ITC is an accurate, non-invasive method for measurement of
  heat loss and energy expenditure in healthy preterm infants, offering a
  useful clinical and research tool.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR NEOTHERM: Directly demonstrates that infrared cameras can
quantify metabolic energy expenditure in preterm neonates. This is the
foundational validation study for using thermal imaging as a calorimetric
tool in the NICU.


4. INFRARED THERMOGRAPHIC CALORIMETRY APPLIED TO PRETERM INFANTS UNDER
   RADIANT WARMERS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Authors:  Heimann K, Peschgens T, Merz U
Year:     1999
Journal:  Journal of Thermal Biology, 24(1), 47–52
DOI:      https://doi.org/10.1016/S0306-4565(98)00044-8

Key findings:
- Applied infrared thermographic calorimetry to 10 preterm infants (mean
  weight 1.88 ± 1.29 kg) under radiant warmers, measuring mean energy
  expenditure of 3.08 ± 0.55 kcal/kg/h.
- When the radiant warmer was removed for 2.5 minutes, heat loss increased to
  6.50 ± 1.07 kcal/kg/h and mean body temperature dropped by 1.0 ± 0.3°C.
- Developed a correction method for the infrared interference caused by radiant
  warmers, subtracting the warmer's power density output from the measured
  radiant flux.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR NEOTHERM: Shows the practical application of thermal imaging
calorimetry in real NICU conditions (under radiant warmers) and quantifies
how rapidly preterm infants lose heat when warming is interrupted — directly
relevant to cold stress detection.


5. BODY TEMPERATURE IN PREMATURE INFANTS DURING THE FIRST WEEK OF LIFE:
   EXPLORATION USING INFRARED THERMAL IMAGING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Authors:  Knobel-Dail RB, Holditch-Davis D, Sloane R, Guenther BD, Katz AR
Year:     2017
Journal:  Journal of Thermal Biology, 69, 118–123
DOI:      https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtherbio.2017.06.005
PubMed:   29037371; PMC5657603

Key findings:
- Studied 30 VLBW (very low birth weight) infants using infrared thermography
  (FLIR SC640 camera) in heated, humid incubators over their first two weeks
  of life.
- Confirmed that abdominal and foot temperatures measured by IRT closely agree
  with skin thermistor measurements (validated via Bland-Altman technique) in
  extremely premature infants (23–28 weeks gestational age).
- Discovered that very premature infants exhibit abnormal thermal patterns:
  foot temperatures remain warmer than abdominal temperatures for the first
  12 hours of life, suggesting impaired vasomotor control — a pattern that
  may predict adverse clinical outcomes.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR NEOTHERM: Demonstrates thermal maturation patterns in preterm
infants and shows that IRT can detect abnormal thermoregulatory patterns that
may serve as early warning indicators of clinical deterioration.


6. INFRARED THERMOGRAPHY FOR DETAILED REGISTRATION OF THERMOREGULATION IN
   PREMATURE INFANTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Authors:  Heimann K, Jergus K, Abbas AK, Heussen N, Leonhardt S,
          Orlikowsky T
Year:     2013
Journal:  Journal of Perinatal Medicine, 41(5), 613–620
DOI:      https://doi.org/10.1515/jpm-2012-0239
PubMed:   23443261

Key findings:
- Evaluated multi-site skin temperature using non-contact IRT in 10 premature
  infants (median 27 weeks GA, weight 1322 g) across leg, back, arm, head,
  and upper abdomen.
- During transition from incubator to kangaroo (skin-to-skin) care, infants
  cooled by a maximum of 0.62°C. From first to second kangaroo care session,
  central temperatures (abdomen, back) were maintained while head and leg
  temperatures rose distinctly.
- Demonstrated that IRT provides more detailed spatial temperature information
  than conventional contact sensors, revealing regional differences in
  thermoregulation not detectable by standard monitoring.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR NEOTHERM: Validates that IRT captures detailed regional
thermoregulatory patterns in preterm infants that conventional sensors miss.
Supports the concept that thermal imaging can monitor metabolic heat
production across different body regions simultaneously.


7. CONTINUOUS MONITORING USING THERMOGRAPHY CAN CAPTURE THE HEAT OSCILLATIONS
   MAINTAINING BODY TEMPERATURE IN NEONATES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Authors:  Morimoto A, Takahashi N, Kanda H, et al.
Year:     2024
Journal:  Scientific Reports, 14, 10870
DOI:      https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-60718-y
PubMed:   38714775; PMC11076520

Key findings:
- Used infrared thermography to measure skin temperature every 15 seconds in
  27 neonates inside incubators, extracting two frequency components of heat
  oscillation (Components A and B).
- Body temperature in infants is balanced between heat production from
  metabolism and heat transfer to the environment; IRT can capture the dynamic
  oscillatory patterns that maintain thermal homeostasis.
- Demonstrated that contactless IRT can assess spontaneous thermal metabolism
  in vulnerable neonates, revealing metabolic cycling patterns not visible
  through conventional intermittent temperature measurements.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR NEOTHERM: Demonstrates that continuous thermal monitoring
captures dynamic metabolic heat production patterns in neonates. Supports
the use of thermal cameras for ongoing assessment of metabolic status in
NICU patients.


8. THERMAL IMAGING APPLICATIONS IN NEONATAL CARE: A SCOPING REVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Authors:  Topalidou A, Ali N, Sekulic S, Downe S
Year:     2019
Journal:  BMC Pregnancy and Childbirth, 19, 381
DOI:      https://doi.org/10.1186/s12884-019-2533-y
PubMed:   31651266; PMC6814124

Key findings:
- Comprehensive scoping review of 21 studies on thermal imaging in neonatal
  care. Identified five broad themes: (1) general thermal physiology,
  (2) heat loss and respiratory monitoring, (3) identification of internal
  pathologies (including NEC), (4) other uses, and (5) technical concerns.
- Concluded that thermal imaging is a reliable, non-invasive method for
  continuous monitoring of neonatal emitted temperature, with potential for
  wellbeing assessment and diagnosis of pathologies including internal
  abnormalities.
- Noted that IRT is not yet a standard method for neonatal thermal monitoring
  despite accumulating evidence supporting its clinical utility.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR NEOTHERM: The most comprehensive review of neonatal thermal
imaging applications. Confirms clinical potential while identifying that
further research is needed for widespread clinical adoption — exactly the
gap NeoTherm aims to fill.


9. INFRARED THERMOGRAPHY IN NEWBORNS: THE FIRST HOUR AFTER BIRTH
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Authors:  Christidis I, Zotter H, Rosegger H, Engele H, Kurz R, Kerbl R
Year:     2003
Journal:  Gynäkologisch-geburtshilfliche Rundschau, 43(1), 31–35
DOI:      https://doi.org/10.1159/000067168
PubMed:   12499755

Key findings:
- Measured body surface temperature in 42 healthy full-term newborns within the
  first hour after delivery using infrared thermography under several
  environmental conditions (radiant heater, bathing, skin-to-skin contact).
- Found that surface temperature immediately after birth shows a uniform whole-
  body picture but is significantly lower than core temperature; peripheral
  sites cool rapidly while trunk temperature is maintained.
- Concluded that bathing in warm water leads to a more even temperature profile
  and that radiant heaters and skin-to-skin contact are both effective methods
  to prevent heat loss. IRT was validated as a simple and reliable tool for
  neonatal skin temperature profiling.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR NEOTHERM: Early validation study showing IRT can capture the
dynamic thermoregulatory transition at birth, including BAT-mediated heat
production patterns in the interscapular region.


10. THERMOREGULATION AND THERMOGRAPHY IN NEONATAL PHYSIOLOGY AND DISEASE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Authors:  Knobel RB, Guenther BD, Rice HE
Year:     2011
Journal:  Biological Research for Nursing, 13(3), 274–282
DOI:      https://doi.org/10.1177/1099800411403467
PubMed:   21586499; PMC3775585

Key findings:
- Described the development and application of IRT methodology for measuring
  body temperature in neonates during the first month of life, examining
  relationships between thermoregulation, perfusion, and disease.
- Identified potential clinical applications: kangaroo care heat transfer,
  body cooling for hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy treatment, and early
  detection of necrotizing enterocolitis through abdominal thermal patterns.
- Established that IRT reveals perfusion-related temperature differentials
  between central and peripheral body regions that may predict clinical
  deterioration in premature infants.

SIGNIFICANCE FOR NEOTHERM: Foundational methodological paper for neonatal
IRT research. Establishes that thermal imaging can detect disease-related
changes in perfusion and metabolism — supporting broader metabolic assessment
applications.

SUMMARY OF GAPS AND OPPORTUNITIES
================================================================================

WELL-ESTABLISHED EVIDENCE:
- BAT activation detection via IRT in neonates (Urisarri et al. 2021)
- Energy expenditure measurement via IRT calorimetry (Heimann et al. 2000)
- Cold stress / thermoregulation monitoring in preterm infants (Knobel-Dail
  et al. 2017; Heimann et al. 2013)
- Thermal maturation patterns in preterm vs term infants (Knobel-Dail 2017;
  Christidis 2003)
- Continuous metabolic monitoring via thermal oscillations (Morimoto 2024)

NOTABLE RESEARCH GAPS (opportunities for NeoTherm):
1. THYROID SCREENING VIA NECK THERMOGRAPHY IN NEONATES: No published studies
   found applying IRT specifically for neonatal thyroid function screening.
   Adult thyroid nodule detection via IRT exists (50% sensitivity, 82%
   specificity) but has not been translated to neonatal congenital
   hypothyroidism screening.

2. HYPOGLYCEMIA PREDICTION VIA THERMAL IMAGING: No direct studies linking
   thermal camera data to neonatal blood glucose levels. Some emerging work
   (HAPI-BELT system) uses camera + PPG sensors with deep learning to detect
   hypoglycemia via proxy indicators (HR variability, respiratory patterns,
   skin color changes) rather than direct thermal signatures.

3. INTEGRATED METABOLIC-ENDOCRINE ASSESSMENT: No published system combines
   BAT activation monitoring + energy expenditure estimation + metabolic
   screening into a single thermal imaging platform for neonates.

4. REAL-TIME AI-DRIVEN METABOLIC ALERTS: While continuous thermal monitoring
   has been demonstrated, no published system uses machine learning on
   continuous thermal data to generate real-time metabolic alerts in the NICU.

These gaps represent significant innovation opportunities for NeoTherm.
