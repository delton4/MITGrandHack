================================================================================
POSTOPERATIVE SEPSIS & THERMAL IMAGING: PIVOT ANALYSIS
================================================================================
Date: 2026-03-14
Purpose: Go/No-Go decision for pivoting from neonatal to postop sepsis detection

================================================================================
1. HOW BIG IS THE POSTOP SEPSIS PROBLEM?
================================================================================

INCIDENCE:
- 11.6 cases of postop sepsis per 1,000 elective surgery discharges (with LOS >3 days)
- Incidence increased from 1.90% (2002) to 4.40% (2013) — trending upward
- Over 30 million inpatient surgeries/year in the US; ~50M+ total procedures
- 12.5% overall postoperative complication rate (2018) = ~3.75 million complications/year
- SSI rates: superficial 1.3%, deep 0.4% (2018 data)

MORTALITY:
- Overall postop sepsis mortality: 12.5%
- By severity: 5.6% (no organ dysfunction), 14.9% (severe), 34.2% (septic shock)
- Hospital mortality for sepsis after elective surgery: 25.88%
- 1-4% of all surgical patients die postoperatively

COST BURDEN:
- Sepsis costs by severity: $16,324 / $24,638 / $38,298
- Hospital-acquired sepsis (not present at admission): $51,022 per case
- Annual sepsis economic burden: >$20 billion USD in the US
- Postop infections: >$700 million incremental cost burden annually
- Sepsis increases LOS by 4.9 days and readmission odds by 6.4x

BOTTOM LINE: This is a massive, growing problem with enormous clinical and
economic impact. The patient volume dwarfs NICU by orders of magnitude.

================================================================================
2. CURRENT STANDARD OF CARE & GAPS
================================================================================

CURRENT DETECTION METHODS:
- Intermittent vital signs every 4-8 hours on general wards (an "evidence-free zone")
- Early warning scores: NEWS/NEWS2, qSOFA, SIRS criteria
- Blood cultures, lactate levels, procalcitonin (PCT), CRP
- Electronic Health Record (EHR) screening alerts
- Physical assessment by nurses during rounds

CRITICAL GAPS:
- Vital signs checked only every 4-8 hours — 82% of hypoxemia episodes are MISSED
  between nursing assessments
- qSOFA has ~50% sensitivity — designed for mortality prediction, NOT early screening
- qSOFA identifies patients LATE in sepsis course
- SIRS is a poor early warning tool for postoperative infection specifically
- NICE sepsis guidance performs poorly in postoperative colorectal patients
  ("the scores that cry wolf")
- EHR-based screening systems have "traditionally performed sub-optimally"
- Knowledge gaps among nurses and residents about sepsis diagnostic criteria
- Most deaths occur AFTER discharge from acute care to general wards, linked to
  unmonitored vital sign deterioration

THE MONITORING GAP:
- Known as "the critical care gap" between ICU/HDU and general wards
- ICU: continuous monitoring of everything
- General ward: spot checks every 4-8 hours
- This is exactly where patients deteriorate and die
- Failure to rescue is a major patient safety issue

BOTTOM LINE: There is a glaring, well-documented gap in postoperative monitoring
on general wards. Current early warning scores perform poorly in surgical patients.
The 4-8 hour vital sign gap is where patients are lost.

================================================================================
3. THERMAL CAMERAS / IRT FOR POSTOP SEPSIS DETECTION
================================================================================

DIRECT EVIDENCE (Adult Sepsis + Thermal Imaging):

a) Core-to-Peripheral Temperature Gradient:
   - Sepsis affects microcirculation, reflected in temperature changes between
     core and skin
   - IRT of limbs in ED patients with suspected sepsis: thermal imaging of all
     four limbs on arrival and after 3 hours of resuscitation
   - Core-to-index-finger gradient >7C (measured via IRT) associated with
     8-day mortality in septic patients requiring vasopressors
   - IRT can rapidly gauge this gradient in real-time, non-invasively

b) Peripheral Perfusion Assessment in Septic Shock:
   - IRT used in surgical ICU patients with septic shock to evaluate peripheral
     tissue perfusion
   - Non-contact, provides visual evidence of temperature as color maps

c) Machine Learning + IRT for Shock Detection:
   - ML algorithms successfully used IRT images for early identification of
     shock in ICU patients
   - Deep learning approaches for assessing mortality risk of critically ill
     patients at risk of hypoperfusion using IRT

d) Ear-Based Sepsis Recognition:
   - Early sepsis recognition using ear localization with infrared thermography
     (IEEE conference publication)

e) Leicester ED Protocol:
   - Pilot study of medical infrared imaging protocol for fever and sepsis
     in the emergency department (PLOS ONE)

BOTTOM LINE: There IS published research on IRT for adult sepsis detection,
primarily focused on perfusion assessment and core-peripheral gradients.
This is an active and growing research area, but NO commercial product exists
specifically for continuous thermal monitoring of postop patients for sepsis.

================================================================================
4. SURGICAL SITE INFECTION (SSI) DETECTION VIA THERMAL IMAGING
================================================================================

STRONG EVIDENCE BASE:

a) Post-Cesarean SSI Detection:
   - "Coldspots" in abdominal thermal images during first 4 days after surgery
     are highly suggestive of SSI
   - Temperature gradient of -1C between periwound area and abdomen at day 7
     = 3-fold increase in SSI likelihood

b) Orthopedic Applications:
   - Mean differential temperature (MDT) in infections: 1.1-2.5C
   - IRT produces accurate pattern of surgical site healing
   - Elevated MDT used for indirect diagnosis of infections

c) Smartphone-Based Thoracic Surgery Monitoring:
   - Thermal images for 7 consecutive days postop
   - Day 4 temperature readings achieved: 91.67% sensitivity, 85.71% specificity
     for predicting healing status at 1-2 months

d) Temperature Patterns in Wound Healing:
   - Normal: temperature elevated 1-2 weeks post-surgery due to physiological
     inflammation
   - Infection signal: SECONDARY temperature peak during healing phase
   - This is a detectable, predictable pattern amenable to algorithmic detection

e) Lab Validation:
   - IRT can detect pre-visual bacterial growth via metabolic heat detection
     (before visible signs appear)

LIMITATIONS:
   - Literature is diverse with inconsistent reporting
   - "Firm evidence for introduction of thermography for infection surveillance
     of surgical wounds is missing" — but this reflects research gaps, not
     negative findings

BOTTOM LINE: SSI detection via thermal imaging has promising evidence with
high sensitivity/specificity numbers. The ability to detect infection BEFORE
visual signs is a key differentiator. This is arguably the strongest evidence
pillar for the pivot.

================================================================================
5. POST-SURGICAL RECOVERY MONITORING LANDSCAPE
================================================================================

WHAT EXISTS IN PACU/ICU:
- Continuous pulse oximetry, ECG, blood pressure
- Core temperature monitoring (esophageal, tympanic, bladder)
- Ventilator monitoring, arterial lines in ICU

WHAT EXISTS ON GENERAL WARDS:
- Manual vital signs every 4-8 hours
- NEWS/NEWS2 scoring (when done)
- Call buttons for patient self-reporting
- Wound inspection during dressing changes (visual only)

WHAT A THERMAL CAMERA COULD ADD:
- Continuous, contactless temperature trending (vs. spot checks)
- Wound site thermal mapping without removing dressings
- Peripheral perfusion monitoring (hands/feet temperature as sepsis indicator)
- Respiratory rate monitoring (thermal cameras can detect breathing)
- Detection of fever onset between nursing rounds
- Pattern recognition for infection vs. normal healing inflammation

THE KILLER STAT:
- Continuous temperature monitoring detected fevers ~18 HOURS EARLIER than
  periodic temperature checks in cancer patients with confirmed infections
- Continuous vital sign monitoring showed 27% decrease in complication rate
  and decreased ICU transfers and failure-to-rescue events

6. POSTOP VS. NEONATAL SEPSIS: DETECTION CHALLENGES COMPARISON
================================================================================

NEONATAL SEPSIS:
- Symptoms are vague, nonspecific, overlap with many neonatal diseases
- No widely adopted risk score exists
- Immature immune system makes biomarkers unreliable
- Surgical trauma mimics infection inflammatory response
- Very small patient population (~4M births/year US, fraction need NICU)
- Highly specialized market with few hospitals

POSTOP SEPSIS (ADULT/PEDIATRIC):
- Symptoms also overlap with normal postop inflammatory response
- Existing scoring systems (NEWS, qSOFA) have known limitations in surgical pts
- BUT patients can communicate symptoms (adults)
- Larger body surface = easier thermal imaging, more thermal data
- MASSIVE patient population (30M+ surgeries/year US)
- Every hospital with an OR is a potential customer
- Less specialized equipment needs (vs. NICU isolettes)

KEY COMPARISON:
                        NEONATAL              POSTOP (ADULT)
Patient volume/yr       ~500K NICU admits     30M+ surgeries
TAM hospitals           ~1,200 NICUs (US)     ~6,000+ hospitals w/ ORs
Detection gap           Very high             Very high
Thermal imaging ease    Hard (small, isolette) Easier (larger, accessible)
Competing solutions     Few                   Growing (wearables, AI/EHR)
Clinical evidence       Growing               Stronger (SSI detection)
Regulatory pathway      Complex (neonatal)    Standard 510(k) pathway
Reimbursement           Uncertain             CMS HAI penalties help

7. MARKET SIZE
================================================================================

SURGICAL SITE INFECTION CONTROL MARKET:
- $5.55 billion (2025), growing to $6.69 billion by 2030 (CAGR 3.8%)
- Alternative estimate: $3.63B (2025) to $5.60B (2035) (CAGR 4.42%)

HOSPITAL-ACQUIRED INFECTION CONTROL MARKET:
- $35.86 billion (2025), growing to $44.42 billion by 2029 (CAGR 5.5%)

SEPSIS DIAGNOSTICS & MANAGEMENT:
- Sepsis contributes >$20B annual economic burden in the US alone
- AI-driven sepsis detection is a hot investment area

CMS FINANCIAL PENALTIES:
- Hospitals are penalized for HAIs and readmissions
- SSIs are a CMS Hospital-Acquired Condition (HAC) reduction program target
- This creates strong BUYER MOTIVATION — hospitals need solutions

ADDRESSABLE MARKET ESTIMATE:
- If even 1% of 30M surgical patients/year could benefit from enhanced
  thermal monitoring, that's 300,000 patients
- At even $50-100/patient for a monitoring service, that's $15-30M SAM
- For a device/SaaS play sold to hospitals: 6,000 hospitals x $20-50K
  annual subscription = $120-300M TAM (conservative)

8. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE
================================================================================

EXISTING COMPANIES/PRODUCTS:

a) MolecuLight (Toronto, Canada):
   - MolecuLightDX: FDA 510(k) cleared, Class II device
   - Uses FLUORESCENCE imaging (not thermal) to detect bacterial loads
   - Recently added thermal imaging module (April 2025)
   - >100 peer-reviewed publications
   - Only wound care device that is both FDA cleared AND FDA-qualified MDDT
   - LIMITATION: Handheld, point-of-care, NOT continuous monitoring

b) TempTraq:
   - Continuous temperature monitoring wearable patch
   - Bluetooth-connected, real-time data to central workstation
   - Used for sepsis early detection (18 hours earlier detection shown)
   - LIMITATION: Contact-based, single-point temperature, not imaging

c) Solventum (formerly 3M):
   - Perioperative temperature management solutions
   - Focus on preventing hypothermia, not detecting infection
   - LIMITATION: Not a detection/diagnostic play

d) Prenosis Inc.:
   - Sepsis ImmunoScore: FDA-authorized AI system for sepsis prediction
   - Uses blood biomarkers + clinical data
   - LIMITATION: Lab-based, not continuous monitoring

e) Dascena (InSight):
   - AI/ML algorithm for sepsis prediction from EHR data
   - RCT and post-marketing studies
   - LIMITATION: Software-only, relies on existing intermittent data

f) Smart Wound Dressings (multiple research groups):
   - Integrated temperature sensors in wound dressings
   - Bluetooth to smartphone
   - LIMITATION: Still research-stage, contact-based

GAP IN THE MARKET:
Nobody is doing CONTINUOUS, CONTACTLESS, CAMERA-BASED thermal monitoring
for postoperative patients on general wards. This is the white space.

================================================================================
9. WOULD THERMAL CAMERA BE MORE VALUABLE IN POSTOP THAN NICU?
================================================================================

ARGUMENTS FOR POSTOP BEING BETTER:

1. VOLUME: 30M+ surgeries vs. ~500K NICU admissions = 60x larger patient pool
2. MARKET ACCESS: Every hospital vs. specialized NICUs only
3. THE GAP IS CLEARER: General ward monitoring is an acknowledged "evidence-free
   zone" — the problem is well-defined and uncontroversial
4. EVIDENCE BASE: SSI thermal imaging research is stronger and more directly
   applicable than neonatal thermal sepsis detection
5. BUYER MOTIVATION: CMS penalties for HAIs and readmissions create urgency
6. EASIER IMAGING: Adult bodies are larger, more accessible, not inside isolettes
7. FAILURE TO RESCUE: High-profile patient safety issue hospitals want to solve
8. CONTINUOUS MONITORING PROVEN: 27% complication reduction with continuous
   vitals already demonstrated — thermal adds another modality
9. COMPETITION POSITIONING: MolecuLight is handheld/point-of-care;
   a continuous camera system is differentiated
10. DUAL VALUE: Can monitor BOTH vital signs (temperature trending, respiratory
    rate) AND wound sites from a single camera

ARGUMENTS AGAINST (RISKS):

1. More competition from wearables, AI/EHR tools, smart dressings
2. Adult patients can self-report symptoms (reduces urgency vs. nonverbal neonates)
3. General ward is messier — patients ambulate, have visitors, variable clothing
4. Regulatory might be complex for a new monitoring modality claim
5. Hospital IT integration and alarm fatigue are real barriers
6. The neonatal story is more emotionally compelling for fundraising

VERDICT: The postop use case is commercially stronger. The neonatal use case
is more emotionally resonant but commercially limited.

10. TIME FROM SURGERY TO SEPSIS — THE DETECTION WINDOW
================================================================================

TIMELINE:
- Fulminant infections: 48-72 hours (most severe, rapidly progressive)
- SSI-related sepsis: 5-7 days typical onset
- Anastomotic leak presentations: several days to weeks (insidious)
- Some SSIs develop up to 30-90 days post-surgery

CRITICAL DETECTION WINDOWS:
- Days 1-3: Acute infections, highest severity if missed
- Days 4-7: SSI thermal signature becomes detectable (coldspots days 1-4,
  temperature gradient at day 7 predictive of SSI)
- Days 7-14: Secondary temperature peak = infection signal
- This aligns perfectly with general ward stay (typically 2-7 days)

THERMAL CAMERA TIMING ADVANTAGE:
- Day 4 thermal readings achieved 91.67% sensitivity for predicting SSI
- Thermal changes can be detected BEFORE visual signs appear
- Continuous monitoring catches fever onset 18 hours earlier
- The typical 2-7 day general ward stay is the exact window where thermal
  monitoring would provide maximum value

The postoperative use case is commercially superior in nearly every dimension.
The key selling points for a hackathon pitch:

1. "82% of vital sign deterioration is MISSED on general surgical wards"
2. "Thermal imaging detects SSI before visual signs with >90% sensitivity"
3. "Continuous monitoring reduces complications by 27% — our camera does it
    contactlessly"
4. "$20B+ sepsis cost burden, 30M surgeries/year, every hospital is a customer"
5. "No one does continuous, contactless thermal monitoring for postop patients"

The pivot does NOT mean abandoning thermal imaging expertise — it means
applying it to a much larger market with stronger clinical evidence.
