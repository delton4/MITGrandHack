1. SCOPING REVIEWS & COMPREHENSIVE REVIEWS
================================================================================

PAPER 1:
Title: Thermal imaging applications in neonatal care: a scoping review
Authors: Topalidou A, Ali N, Sekulic S, Downe S
Year: 2019
Journal: BMC Pregnancy and Childbirth, 19, 381
Key Findings: Systematic review of 21 studies (from 442 hits across EMBASE,
MEDLINE, and MIDIRS). Concludes that thermal imaging is a reliable and
non-invasive method for continuous monitoring of emitted temperature of
neonates, with potential for diagnosing pathologies including internal
abnormalities. However, introduction into everyday neonatology practice faces
several methodological challenges, particularly environmental parameters
when infants are in incubators or open radiant warmers. Big-cohort studies
using established methodological protocols are notably absent from the
literature.
DOI: 10.1186/s12884-019-2533-y
Link: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12884-019-2533-y

PAPER 2:
Title: Review of Biomedical Applications of Contactless Imaging of Neonates
       Using Infrared Thermography and Beyond
Authors: AlZubaidi AK, et al.
Year: 2018
Journal: Methods and Protocols, 1(4), 39 (MDPI)
Key Findings: Comprehensive review of contactless imaging for recording
physiological signals, surface changes, and internal structures. Highlights
advantages including quick data generation, no skin contact (reducing skin
breakdown and infection risk), and minimizing the number of probes attached
to fragile preterm infants. Covers MWIR and LWIR camera technologies and
their respective applications in neonatal monitoring.
DOI: 10.3390/mps1040039
Link: https://www.mdpi.com/2409-9279/1/4/39

PAPER 3:
Title: Emerging innovations in neonatal monitoring: a comprehensive review
       of progress and potential for non-contact technologies
Authors: (Multiple authors)
Year: 2024
Journal: Frontiers in Pediatrics, 12, 1442753
Key Findings: Reviews the current reliance on wearable or contact sensor
technologies for vital sign monitoring and their complications (discomfort,
skin damage, infections). Examines emerging non-contact technologies
including thermal imaging, camera-based systems, and radar. Thermal imaging
is noted as particularly advantageous as it is less affected by visual
obstructions that compromise other camera-based systems.
Link: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pediatrics/articles/10.3389/fped.2024.1442753/full

2. COMMERCIAL CAMERA SYSTEMS EVALUATED IN NICU SETTINGS
================================================================================

PAPER 4:
Title: Body temperature in premature infants during the first week of life:
       Exploration using infrared thermal imaging
Authors: Knobel-Dail RB, Holditch-Davis D, Sloane R, Guenther BD, Katz AR
Year: 2017
Journal: Journal of Thermal Biology, 69, 118-123
Key Findings: Used a FLIR SC640 uncooled infrared camera to capture full
body temperatures of 30 extremely premature VLBW infants in heated, humid
incubators. Found that very premature infants exhibit abnormal thermal
patterns, keeping foot temperatures warmer than abdominal temperatures
during their first 12 hours. IR imaging measurements were in close agreement
with skin thermistors using Bland-Altman technique. Demonstrates IR
thermography as viable for continuous monitoring in heated/humid incubators.
Camera: FLIR SC640 (uncooled, LWIR)
DOI: 10.1016/j.jtherbio.2017.06.007
Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29037371/

PAPER 5:
Title: Infrared thermography for detailed registration of thermoregulation
       in premature infants
Authors: Heimann K, Jergus K, Abbas AK, Heussen N, Leonhardt S,
         Orlikowsky T
Year: 2013
Journal: Journal of Perinatal Medicine, 41(5), 613-620
Key Findings: Evaluated skin temperature using non-contact IRT in multiple
body areas of 10 preterm infants (median 27 weeks gestation). Temperature
on central areas (abdomen, back) was maintained but rose distinctively on
head and leg during certain procedures. Demonstrated that IRT can provide
detailed temperature mapping across multiple body regions simultaneously,
something not possible with conventional point thermometry.
Camera: Used in conjunction with IRBIS Professional software
DOI: 10.1515/jpm-2012-0239
Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23443261/

PAPER 6:
Title: Multi-camera infrared thermography for infant respiration monitoring
Authors: Lorato I, Stuijk S, Meftah M, Verkruijsse W, de Haan G
Year: 2020
Journal: Biomedical Optics Express, 11(9), 4950-4964
Key Findings: Used three FLIR Lepton cameras (low-cost, uncooled
microbolometer, LWIR) to collect thermal videos of neonates in open beds.
Multiple cameras positioned to increase coverage of both respiration motion
and flow. Tested on 152 minutes of recordings on 7 infants, achieving mean
absolute error of 2.07 breaths/min compared with chest impedance. Shows
that consumer-grade, low-cost thermal cameras can be viable for NICU
respiratory monitoring.
Camera: FLIR Lepton (3x cameras, uncooled microbolometer, LWIR, ~$200 each)
DOI: 10.1364/BOE.397188
Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7510882/

3. INCUBATOR-COMPATIBLE THERMAL IMAGING SOLUTIONS (PLEXIGLAS/LWIR)
================================================================================

KEY CHALLENGE:
Plexiglas (polymethylmethacrylate/PMMA) used in incubator hoods is opaque
to long-wave infrared (LWIR, 8-14 um) radiation, with emissivity ~0.97,
making it act as a thermal radiator rather than a window. This means the
camera sees the temperature of the Plexiglas itself, not the infant inside.

SOLUTIONS DOCUMENTED IN LITERATURE:

(a) Polyethylene (PE) Window Replacement:
    - PE foil (0.01 mm thickness) provides ~92-94% transmissivity for LWIR
    - A 10x15 cm window cut in the incubator canopy, sealed with PE foil
    - Medical-grade PE used to maintain sterility
    - Referenced in Abbas et al. 2012, Heimann et al. 2013

(b) Plastic Wrap/Cling Film:
    - Cutout in incubator lid covered with standard plastic wrap
    - Preserves thermal environment while allowing IR transmission
    - Lower image quality than PE foil due to wrinkles/non-uniformity
    - Referenced in Knobel-Dail et al. 2017

(c) Camera Inside Incubator:
    - Some studies mount small cameras inside the incubator
    - Avoids the Plexiglas problem entirely
    - Challenge: camera must tolerate high humidity (60-80% RH) and
      warmth (32-36C) inside incubator

(d) Open-Bed / Radiant Warmer Studies:
    - Many studies avoid the problem by using open-bed or radiant warmer
      settings where there is direct line-of-sight to the infant

PAPER 7:
Title: Intelligent neonatal monitoring based on a virtual thermal sensor
Authors: Abbas AK, Leonhardt S
Year: 2014
Journal: BMC Medical Imaging, 14, 9
Key Findings: Designed a virtual temperature sensing application using IR
camera with IRBIS Professional software, imaging through a PE window
(0.01 mm thickness, 92-94% IR transmission) installed in the incubator
canopy. Addressed temperature and humidity variations as the main factors
preventing accurate calibration in convective incubators. Implemented
real-time calibration during clinical measurement to avoid incorrect
temperature registration.
DOI: 10.1186/1471-2342-14-9
Link: https://bmcmedimaging.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/1471-2342-14-9

4. CAMERA CALIBRATION METHODS FOR NICU ENVIRONMENTS
================================================================================

PAPER 8:
Title: Neonatal infrared thermography imaging: Analysis of heat flux during
       different clinical scenarios
Authors: Abbas AK, Heimann K, Blazek V, Orlikowsky T, Leonhardt S
Year: 2012
Journal: Infrared Physics & Technology, 55(6), 538-548
Key Findings: Introduced a compensation technique based on modeling physical
interactions within the measurement scene. Derived different temperature
compensation equations for three measurement environments: (1) convective
neonatal incubators, (2) Kangaroo Mother Care, and (3) open radiant warmers.
Each environment requires a different calibration approach due to different
sources of thermal interference (humidity, air convection, radiant heat).
With appropriate compensation, IRT can serve as a standard temperature
monitoring technique for neonates.
DOI: 10.1016/j.infrared.2012.07.001
Link: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S135044951200059X

PAPER 9:
Title: Infrared Thermography with High Accuracy in a Neonatal Incubator
Authors: Hamada T, et al.
Year: 2022
Journal: Annals of Biomedical Engineering, 50(10), 1257-1267
Key Findings: Established a new calibration method for IRT in incubators,
evaluated using a variable-temperature blackbody furnace at different
incubator settings. Improved the percentage of data with mean absolute error
(MAE) < 0.3C from 4.2% (standard method) to 93.1% (new method). Two of
three IRTs achieved MAE < 0.1C under all conditions. The method uses a
non-heating blackbody as a reference source (concept: both measured object
and reference source are equally influenced by thermal drift), and includes
automatic non-uniformity calibration (ANUC) to compensate for temperature
drift during measurements. Provides high accuracy for both point-in-time
and continuous monitoring.
DOI: 10.1007/s10439-022-02937-w
Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8890465/

CALIBRATION CHALLENGES SUMMARY:
- Incubator air temperature (32-36C) raises ambient background
- High humidity (60-80% RH) affects IR transmission through air
- Convective air currents create non-uniform thermal environment
- Plexiglas/PE window introduces transmission losses
- Camera thermal drift over time requires periodic NUC
- Different clinical scenarios (incubator vs KMC vs radiant warmer) need
  different calibration models

5. CORE-PERIPHERAL TEMPERATURE DIFFERENCE & DEEP LEARNING SYSTEMS
================================================================================

PAPER 10:
Title: Thermal imaging-based core peripheral temperature difference
       measurement for neonatal monitoring in the NICU
Authors: Zhang Y, Song R, et al.
Year: 2025
Journal: Biomedical Optics Express, 16(3), 965
Key Findings: Clinical trial with 40 preterm infants (Feb-Aug 2024) at
Nanfang Hospital, Southern Medical University, China. Used deep
learning-based infant body landmark detection to automate identification of
core (chest/abdomen) and peripheral (hand) sites for continuous CPTD
monitoring. Fine-tuned a high-resolution Transformer architecture for pose
estimation in neonatal thermal images. CPTD is a key biomarker for
hemodynamic status, early shock recognition, peripheral perfusion
assessment, and sepsis monitoring in preterm infants.
DOI: 10.1364/BOE.547131
Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11919349/

PAPER 11:
Title: Camera fusion for real-time temperature monitoring of neonates using
       deep learning
Authors: Lyra S, Rixen J, Heimann K, Karthik S, Joseph J, Leonhardt S,
         Hoog Antink C
Year: 2022
Journal: Medical & Biological Engineering & Computing, 60(7), 1887-1901
Key Findings: Deep learning approach combining RGB + IR cameras for neonatal
temperature monitoring. Used transfer-learned keypoint detector for body
landmark prediction in RGB images, then image registration to transform
landmarks to IRT frames for temperature extraction. Demonstrated feasibility
of low-cost embedded GPUs for real-time processing. The multi-modal approach
(RGB for landmark detection + IR for temperature) addresses the challenge
that body landmarks are difficult to detect directly in thermal images.
DOI: 10.1007/s11517-022-02561-9
Link: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11517-022-02561-9

6. RESPIRATORY MONITORING VIA THERMAL IMAGING
================================================================================

PAPER 12:
Title: Neonatal non-contact respiratory monitoring based on real-time
       infrared thermography
Authors: Abbas AK, Heimann K, Jergus K, Orlikowsky T, Leonhardt S
Year: 2011
Journal: BioMedical Engineering OnLine, 10, 93
Key Findings: Demonstrated that neonatal respiration rate can be monitored
by analyzing anterior naris (nostril) temperature profiles associated with
inspiration/expiration phases. IR thermography allows non-contact respiratory
monitoring inside the NICU, detecting convective heat transfer changes that
differentiate between different types of apnea (central vs obstructive).
This represents a step towards replacing potentially harmful cable-based
sensors in neonatology.
DOI: 10.1186/1475-925X-10-93
Link: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/1475-925X-10-93

PAPER 13:
Title: Non-contact respiratory rate monitoring using thermal and visible
       imaging: a pilot study on neonates
Authors: (Multiple authors)
Year: 2022
Journal: Journal of Clinical Monitoring and Computing
Key Findings: Pilot study using thermal and visible imaging modalities for
non-contact respiratory rate monitoring in neonates. Thermal imaging detects
temperature variations around the nose during inspiratory/expiratory phases.
The technique leverages the fact that exhaled air is warmer than inhaled air,
creating measurable thermal signatures at the nostrils.
Link: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10877-022-00945-8

7. WEARABLE vs NON-CONTACT MONITORING COMPARISONS
================================================================================

KEY COMPARISON POINTS FROM LITERATURE:

WEARABLE SENSORS:
- Thermistors: accuracy +/- 0.1C
- Thermocouples: accuracy +/- 0.5C
- Continuous monitoring capability
- Risk of skin damage in preterm infants (fragile skin)
- Risk of infection from adhesives and cables
- Limited to single-point measurement
- Wearable sensors detected temperatures 0.2C lower than clinical thermometers

NON-CONTACT THERMAL IMAGING:
- Full-body temperature mapping (hundreds/thousands of measurement points)
- No skin contact = no skin damage or infection risk
- Can detect spatial temperature gradients and patterns
- Affected by environmental factors (humidity, ambient temperature)
- Requires line-of-sight (problematic with Plexiglas incubators)
- Higher equipment cost (though FLIR Lepton ~$200 changing this)
- Requires calibration for NICU environment
- Cannot directly measure core body temperature (surface only)

PAPER 14:
Title: Continuous monitoring using thermography can capture the heat
       oscillations maintaining body temperature in neonates
Authors: Morimoto N, et al.
Year: 2024
Journal: Scientific Reports, 14, 11143
Key Findings: Used IRT to measure skin temperature every 15 seconds of 27
neonates (32 measurements) in incubators. Extracted two frequency components
of temperature oscillation using Savitzky-Golay method. Demonstrated that
contactless IRT can capture subtle thermoregulatory oscillations that are
invisible to conventional point thermometry, providing insights into
spontaneous thermal metabolism in neonates.
DOI: 10.1038/s41598-024-60718-y
Link: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-60718-y

PAPER 15:
Title: Health status detection of neonates using infrared thermography and
       deep convolutional neural networks
Authors: (Multiple authors)
Year: 2019 (published online)
Journal: Infrared Physics & Technology
Key Findings: Combined IRT and deep CNNs for the first time to detect health
status of neonates. Thermal images from 19 healthy and 19 unhealthy neonates
achieved 99.58% accuracy, 99.73% specificity, and 99.43% sensitivity. This
demonstrates the diagnostic potential of thermal imaging beyond simple
temperature measurement, using AI pattern recognition on thermal images.
Link: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1350449519303123

8. REGULATORY PATHWAYS (FDA & CE MARKING)
================================================================================

FDA REGULATORY FRAMEWORK:
- Telethermographic systems: classified under 21 CFR 884.2980(a)
- Product code: LHQ (for adjunctive diagnostic use)
- Class I (adjunctive): 510(k) exempt in some cases, or standard 510(k)
- Class III (standalone diagnostic): requires Premarket Approval (PMA)
- 510(k) pathway: demonstrate substantial equivalence to predicate device
- Typical 510(k) timeline: 90 days after FDA acceptance
- FDA requires premarket notification for IR clinical electronic thermometers
  with telethermographic functions due to "newer technology requiring
  additional testing"

FDA-CLEARED EXAMPLES:
- Meditherm thermal imaging cameras (Class I, 510(k) cleared, adjunctive)
- Thermidas IR System ThIR-A615 (K200999)
- Med-Hot MTI 2000 Thermal Imaging System
- Multiple FLIR, ICI, Optotherm systems cleared under product code LHQ

IMPORTANT FDA DISTINCTION:
- Simple infrared clinical electronic thermometers: recently exempted from
  510(k) requirements (Federal Register, June 2025)
- Infrared thermometers WITH telethermographic functions: still require
  510(k) because these include newer technology requiring additional testing
- For a NICU thermal imaging system: would likely require 510(k) at minimum,
  potentially as Class II device with special controls

EU/CE MARKING UNDER MDR 2017/745:
- Medical Device Regulation (MDR) fully applicable since May 2021
- Infrared thermometers: typically Class IIa (measuring devices)
- Requirements: Quality Management System (ISO 13485), technical
  documentation (Annexes II & III), clinical evaluation, Notified Body
  assessment (for Class IIa and above), EU Declaration of Conformity,
  Unique Device Identifier (UDI), post-market surveillance
- Clinical Evaluation Report (CER) required
- For novel NICU thermal monitoring: may need clinical investigation data

Title: Evaluation of a continuous neonatal temperature monitor for
       low-resource settings: a device feasibility pilot study
Authors: (Multiple authors)
Year: 2020
Journal: BMJ Paediatrics Open, 4(1), e000668
Key Findings: Low-cost continuous neonatal temperature monitor (NTM)
developed for low-resource settings meets ISO 80601-2-56 standards for

COST COMPARISON (approximate, from literature):
- Single-use skin temperature probe: $5-15 per probe
- Continuous wired thermistor system: $500-2,000 per unit
- FLIR Lepton (uncooled microbolometer): ~$200 per camera
- FLIR SC640 (research-grade uncooled): ~$10,000-20,000
- Cooled MWIR camera (research-grade): ~$30,000-100,000+
- Optris PI series (industrial uncooled): ~$3,000-8,000
- "Smart" incubator with integrated thermal control: part of incubator
  cost ($15,000-50,000+)

10. IR BAND COMPARISON (LWIR vs MWIR vs SWIR) FOR NEONATAL USE
================================================================================

LWIR (Long-Wave Infrared, 8-14 um):
- Sensor: uncooled microbolometer (e.g., vanadium oxide, amorphous silicon)
- Cost: lowest (consumer cameras available)
- Peak emission of human body (~37C) falls in this range (Wien's law peak ~9.3 um)
- Best for ambient/body temperature measurement
- BLOCKED by Plexiglas/PMMA incubator hoods (major limitation)
- Transmitted by polyethylene (PE) film (~92-94%)
- Most widely used in neonatal thermal imaging studies
- Examples: FLIR Lepton, FLIR SC640, Optris PI series

MWIR (Mid-Wave Infrared, 3-5 um):
- Sensor: cooled InSb or HgCdTe detectors
- Cost: high ($30,000-100,000+)
- Better spatial resolution than LWIR
- Also blocked by Plexiglas, but some glass/crystal materials transmit
- Higher sensitivity to temperature differences
- Used in some research studies (Abbas/Leonhardt group)
- Requires cryogenic cooling = bulky, noisy, power-hungry
- Not practical for routine clinical NICU use currently

SWIR (Short-Wave Infrared, 0.9-1.7 um):
- Sensor: InGaAs
- Detects reflected IR, not emitted thermal radiation
- NOT suitable for body temperature measurement
- Could be useful for imaging through certain materials
- Not relevant for thermal monitoring of neonates

RECOMMENDATION FROM LITERATURE:
LWIR with uncooled microbolometer sensors is the consensus choice for
clinical NICU thermal imaging due to cost, size, and adequate sensitivity
for body temperature ranges. The Plexiglas challenge is addressed through
PE window modifications or open-bed monitoring.
