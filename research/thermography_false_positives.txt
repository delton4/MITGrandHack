NON-SEPSIS CAUSES OF THERMOGRAPHIC FALSE POSITIVES
====================================================
Common conditions that could produce false positive sepsis signals in an
infrared thermography-based sepsis detection system.


1. CIRCULATORY / HEMODYNAMIC CAUSES
-------------------------------------
These directly mimic sepsis physiology — peripheral vasoconstriction and gradient loss.
Any shock state produces the same thermal signal as septic shock.

Condition                  | Mechanism
---------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------
Cardiogenic shock          | Low cardiac output → peripheral vasoconstriction → cold extremities
Hypovolemic shock          | Volume depletion → sympathetic response → peripheral shutdown
Neurogenic shock           | Autonomic dysregulation → abnormal temperature distribution
Pulmonary embolism         | Right heart strain → reduced perfusion → peripheral cooling
Acute MI / heart failure   | Reduced output → compensatory vasoconstriction
Severe anemia              | Altered peripheral perfusion patterns

Key insight: The thermal gradient signal reflects microcirculatory failure generically —
not sepsis specifically.


2. DRUG AND VASOPRESSOR EFFECTS
---------------------------------
Massive confounder in ICU/ED settings.

- Vasopressors (norepinephrine, vasopressin, phenylephrine) — directly cause peripheral
  vasoconstriction; will produce artificially high gradients
- Beta-blockers — blunt the normal thermal response to stress
- Vasodilators (nitroglycerin, calcium channel blockers) — can normalize gradients in a
  sick patient, causing false negatives
- Inotropes (dobutamine) — alter peripheral blood flow unpredictably
- Opioids / sedation — alter thermoregulation centrally
- Epidural anesthesia — causes unilateral or regional vasodilation, asymmetric patterns


3. ENVIRONMENTAL AND MEASUREMENT CONFOUNDERS
----------------------------------------------
Protocol failures, not true physiologic states.

- Cold ambient temperature — vasoconstricts periphery in any patient
- Insufficient acclimatization time — standard is 15-20 min; most studies used 5-10 min
- Recent IV fluid infusion — cold IV fluids transiently lower peripheral temperatures
- Warming blankets / heating pads — artificially elevate local temperatures
- Wet skin / sweating — evaporative cooling creates artifactual cold zones
- Tight clothing or compression devices — tourniquet effect on limb temperature
- Camera angle and distance variation — changes absolute temperature readings


4. INFLAMMATORY AND AUTOIMMUNE CONDITIONS
-------------------------------------------
Produce genuine thermal signal changes unrelated to sepsis.

- Rheumatoid arthritis / inflammatory arthritis — warm joints, abnormal hand/finger temps
- Lupus (SLE) — Raynaud's phenomenon causes cold finger gradients
- Raynaud's phenomenon / disease — episodic finger vasospasm; fingertip cold = classic
  sepsis signal; HIGH false positive risk
- Scleroderma — persistent peripheral vasoconstriction
- Vasculitis — focal thermal abnormalities
- Inflammatory bowel disease flare — elevated core temperature, systemic inflammation


5. NEUROLOGICAL CONDITIONS
----------------------------
Disrupt normal thermoregulatory control.

- Stroke — unilateral thermal asymmetry; hemispheric strokes alter autonomic control
- Spinal cord injury — below injury level loses sympathetic vasoconstriction →
  paradoxically warm periphery
- Autonomic neuropathy (diabetic, alcoholic) — blunts normal vasoconstriction response
- Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS) — extreme focal thermal abnormalities
- Seizures — postictal state causes transient thermal changes


6. ENDOCRINE AND METABOLIC CONDITIONS
---------------------------------------
- Diabetic peripheral vascular disease — altered foot/toe temperatures; the great toe is
  the primary measurement site in multiple sepsis studies
- Hypothyroidism — reduced metabolic rate, cool periphery, low core temperature
- Hyperthyroidism — elevated baseline temperatures throughout
- Adrenal insufficiency (Addisonian crisis) — shock-like state with peripheral cooling
- Pheochromocytoma — episodic catecholamine surges cause vasoconstriction
- Severe hypothermia — confounds all temperature measurements


7. POST-SURGICAL AND TRAUMA STATES
-------------------------------------
- Recent surgery — surgery itself causes hypothermia, vasoconstriction, and fluid shifts
  that persist for hours (Hasanin et al. 2024 specifically studied this population)
- Burns — massively alter skin thermal properties and emissivity
- Traumatic injury / hemorrhagic shock — identical peripheral cooling to septic shock
- Wound dressings and casts — block or alter thermal emission from underlying tissue


8. NEONATAL-SPECIFIC FALSE POSITIVES
--------------------------------------
Especially important given the research gap in neonatal IRT.

- Neonatal thermoregulatory immaturity — neonates cannot vasoconstrict efficiently;
  temperature gradients are inherently less stable than in adults
- Hypothermia of prematurity — preterm infants lose heat rapidly; any cold stress
  creates peripheral-core gradient
- Neonatal abstinence syndrome — maternal drug exposure causes abnormal autonomic responses
- Congenital heart defects — altered cardiac output → abnormal peripheral perfusion
- Polycythemia — elevated hematocrit alters peripheral perfusion patterns
- Jaundice / phototherapy — phototherapy lights significantly alter skin temperature


9. SKIN AND DERMATOLOGIC CONDITIONS
-------------------------------------
Alter emissivity or local thermal properties.

- Cellulitis / localized infection — hot spot at infection site; may create artifactual
  gradient if on a measured limb
- Psoriasis / eczema — altered skin blood flow
- Tattoos — alter local thermal absorption and emission
- Edema — fluid accumulation changes thermal conductivity of tissue


IMPLICATIONS FOR PRODUCT DESIGN
=================================

1. MULTI-SIGNAL APPROACH IS ESSENTIAL
   No single thermographic metric is specific to sepsis. Combine IRT with at least one
   other data point (HR, RR, SpO2 — available from bedside monitors via FHIR) to improve
   specificity. Best-performing studies did this (Luo et al.: thermal heterogeneity +
   clinical variables).

2. SHOCK-TYPE DISAMBIGUATION IS THE HARDEST PROBLEM
   Cardiogenic, hypovolemic, and septic shock are thermographically indistinguishable.
   Options:
   a) Flag all shock states — reframe as "microcirculatory failure detector"
   b) Incorporate clinical context (recent surgery, fluid status, troponin) to disambiguate

3. VASOPRESSOR FLAG
   Any output should be suppressed or flagged as unreliable when vasopressors are active.
   This should be a hard rule in the algorithm.

4. ENVIRONMENTAL PROTOCOL ENFORCEMENT
   The app should require the user to confirm:
   - Acclimatization time completed
   - Ambient temperature within acceptable range
   - Absence of warming devices
   Like a pre-flight checklist before accepting an image.

5. RAYNAUD'S / PERIPHERAL VASCULAR DISEASE SCREENING
   A simple intake question ("does the patient have Raynaud's or known peripheral vascular
   disease?") could flag high false-positive risk patients before imaging.

6. CONFIDENCE + CONFOUNDER OUTPUT
   The algorithm should output not just a risk score but:
   - A confidence level
   - A list of detected confounders
   So the clinician knows exactly how much to trust the signal.

   Hackathon framing: "Our algorithm doesn't just output a score — it outputs a confidence
   level and a list of confounders it detected, so the clinician knows exactly how much to
   trust the signal."
