================================================================================
LITERATURE REVIEW: Thermal Cameras / Infrared Thermography in the NICU
for Neonatal Thermoregulation, Perfusion, and Cardiopulmonary Monitoring
================================================================================
Compiled: March 2026

================================================================================
1. THERMOREGULATION MONITORING IN NEONATES USING INFRARED/THERMAL CAMERAS
================================================================================

1a. Topalidou A, Ali N, Sekulic S, Downe S.
    "Thermal imaging applications in neonatal care: a scoping review"
    Year: 2019
    Journal: BMC Pregnancy and Childbirth, 19, 381
    DOI: 10.1186/s12884-019-2533-y
    Key findings: Comprehensive scoping review that identified 21 studies (from
    442 initial hits) meeting inclusion criteria on thermal imaging in neonatal
    care. Found that thermal imaging is a reliable and non-invasive method for
    continuous monitoring of neonatal temperature with potential for assessing
    wellbeing and diagnosing pathologies, including internal abnormalities.
    A significant number of studies (n=9) were published in the most recent
    8 years, indicating growing interest. Noted challenges with methodology
    when infants are in incubators or open radiant warmers.

1b. Knobel RB, Guenther BD, Rice HE.
    "Thermoregulation and thermography in neonatal physiology and disease"
    Year: 2011
    Journal: Biological Research for Nursing, 13(3), 274-282
    DOI: 10.1177/1099800411403467
    Key findings: Seminal review of infrared thermography applications in
    neonatal physiology. Examined thermograms to analyze temperature
    distributions between central and peripheral body regions in extremely low
    birth weight infants. Demonstrated the technology's potential for examining
    relationships between body temperature patterns and development of
    necrotizing enterocolitis in premature infants.

1c. Hamada K, Hirakawa E, Asano H, et al.
    "Infrared Thermography with High Accuracy in a Neonatal Incubator"
    Year: 2022
    Journal: Annals of Biomedical Engineering, 50, 529-539
    DOI: 10.1007/s10439-022-02937-w
    Key findings: Established a new calibration method for infrared thermography
    (IRT) in an incubator and evaluated its accuracy at different incubator
    settings using a variable-temperature blackbody furnace. Improved accuracy
    dramatically, with percentage of data with MAE < 0.3 degrees C increasing
    from 4.2% (standard method) to 93.1% (new method). Two of three IRTs
    achieved MAE < 0.1 degrees C under all conditions, enabling both point-in-
    time and continuous monitoring.

1d. Morimoto A, Nakamura S, Koyano K, Nishisho S, Nakao Y, Arioka M, et al.
    "Continuous monitoring using thermography can capture the heat oscillations
    maintaining body temperature in neonates"
    Year: 2024
    Journal: Scientific Reports, 14, 10449
    DOI: 10.1038/s41598-024-60718-y
    Key findings: Used IRT to measure skin temperature every 15 seconds of
    neonates in an incubator, analyzing thermal data from 27 neonates (32
    measurements). Extracted two frequency components (Components A and B)
    using the Savitzky-Golay method, demonstrating that contactless IRT can
    continuously measure and visualize heat oscillations involved in
    thermoregulation, offering insight into spontaneous thermal metabolism.

1e. Knobel-Dail RB, Holditch-Davis D, Sloane R, Guenther BD, Katz LM.
    "Body temperature in premature infants during the first week of life:
    Exploration using infrared thermal imaging"
    Year: 2017
    Journal: Journal of Thermal Biology, 69, 118-123
    DOI: 10.1016/j.jtherbio.2017.06.005
    Key findings: Explored the utility of IRT as a non-invasive method for
    measuring body temperature in premature infants, regionally examining
    differential temperatures. Abdominal and foot temperature measurements
    for extremely premature infants in heated, humid incubators were successful
    and showed close agreement with skin thermistors using Bland-Altman
    technique.

1f. Heimann K, Jergus K, Abbas AK, Heussen N, Leonhardt S, Orlikowsky T.
    "Infrared thermography for detailed registration of thermoregulation in
    premature infants"
    Year: 2013
    Journal: Journal of Perinatal Medicine, 41(5), 613-620
    DOI: 10.1515/jpm-2012-0239
    Key findings: Evaluated skin temperature using non-contact IRT in multiple
    body areas of preterm infants for detailed information about temperature
    regulation and distribution. Demonstrated that IRT can provide spatially
    resolved temperature maps of the neonatal body surface, revealing regional
    thermoregulatory patterns not visible with conventional point sensors.

1g. Lyra S, Rixen J, Heimann K, Karthik S, Joseph J, Jayaraman K,
    Orlikowsky T, Sivaprakasam M, Leonhardt S, Hoog Antink C.
    "Camera fusion for real-time temperature monitoring of neonates using
    deep learning"
    Year: 2022
    Journal: Medical & Biological Engineering & Computing, 60, 1787-1800
    DOI: 10.1007/s11517-022-02561-9
    Key findings: Presented a real-time approach for local extraction of body
    surface temperatures in neonates using a deep learning-based keypoint
    detector for body landmark prediction in RGB images, with image
    registration transferring RGB points to thermographic recordings. This
    innovative camera fusion technique enables automated, continuous
    temperature monitoring without physical contact.

2. PERIPHERAL PERFUSION ASSESSMENT VIA THERMAL IMAGING IN NEWBORNS
================================================================================

2a. Zhang N, Song X, He J, Wang W.
    "Thermal imaging-based core peripheral temperature difference measurement
    for neonatal monitoring in the NICU"
    Year: 2025
    Journal: Biomedical Optics Express, 16(3), 965-981
    DOI: 10.1364/BOE.549693
    Key findings: First clinical showcase of using a thermal infrared (TIR)
    camera for continuous non-contact core-peripheral temperature difference
    (CPTD) monitoring of preterm infants in the NICU. CPTD (difference between
    core and peripheral skin temperature) serves as a key biomarker for
    assessing hemodynamic status and is an early warning indicator of shock
    and severe infection. A normal gradient of 2-4 degrees C indicates adequate
    perfusion; >4 degrees C suggests vasoconstriction and possible hypovolemia,
    hypothermia, or low cardiac output.

2b. Vats V, Nagori A, Singh P, Dutt R, Bandhey H, Wason M, Lodha R, Sethi T.
    "Early Prediction of Hemodynamic Shock in Pediatric Intensive Care Units
    With Deep Learning on Thermal Videos"
    Year: 2022
    Journal: Frontiers in Physiology, 13, 862411
    DOI: 10.3389/fphys.2022.862411
    Key findings: Applied deep learning to 103,936 frames from 406 thermal
    videos recorded on 22 pediatric patients. Extracted center-to-peripheral
    difference (CPD) in temperature from thermal videos. Achieved AUROC of
    0.81 +/- 0.06 and AUPRC of 0.78 +/- 0.05 at 5 hours before shock onset,
    providing sufficient lead time for clinical intervention. Demonstrated that
    "cold shock" (elevated systemic vascular resistance with cold extremities
    due to vasoconstriction) can be identified with thermal imaging CPD.

2c. Rice HE, Hollingsworth CL, Bradsher E, Danko ME, Crosby SM,
    Goldberg RN, Tanaka DT, Knobel RB.
    "Infrared thermal imaging (thermography) of the abdomen in extremely
    low birthweight infants"
    Year: 2010
    Journal: Journal of Surgical Radiology, 1(2), 82-89
    Key findings: Used infrared thermography to examine temperature
    differentials between chest and abdomen in ELBW infants. Found that
    abdominal skin temperature was significantly lower in infants with
    necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC) than in those without. Demonstrated
    regional perfusion changes detectable by thermal imaging could serve as
    early warning signs for NEC.

2d. Krbec BA, Zhang X, Chityat I, Brady-Mine A, Linton E, Copeland D,
    Anthony BW, Edelman ER, Davis JM.
    "Emerging innovations in neonatal monitoring: a comprehensive review of
    progress and potential for non-contact technologies"
    Year: 2024
    Journal: Frontiers in Pediatrics, 12, 1442753
    DOI: 10.3389/fped.2024.1442753
    Key findings: Comprehensive review addressing limitations of wearable/
    contact sensors (discomfort, skin damage, infections) and summarizing
    advances in non-contact technologies including thermal imaging. Proposed
    that fusion of non-contact and wearable sensors into multi-modal platforms
    can enhance reliability and provide a more comprehensive set of
    physiological data for neonatal monitoring.

3. PATENT DUCTUS ARTERIOSUS (PDA) DETECTION USING THERMAL/INFRARED IMAGING
================================================================================

NOTE: Direct thermal camera-based PDA detection studies are very limited in the
published literature. Most infrared-based PDA research uses near-infrared
spectroscopy (NIRS), which is a different modality from thermal imaging. However,
thermal imaging may indirectly contribute through perfusion assessment, as PDA
causes hemodynamic changes detectable through peripheral perfusion patterns.

3a. Chock VY, Rose LA, Mante JV, Punn R.
    "Near-infrared spectroscopy for detection of a significant patent ductus
    arteriosus"
    Year: 2016
    Journal: Pediatric Research, 80(5), 675-680
    DOI: 10.1038/pr.2016.148
    Key findings: Evaluated NIRS as a bedside screening tool for hemodynamically
    significant PDA (hsPDA). NIRS can detect changes in regional cerebral and
    renal oxygen saturations resulting from hsPDA, potentially aiding clinicians
    in identifying hsPDA and guiding treatment decisions based on organ
    perfusion data. [NOTE: Uses NIRS, not thermal camera.]

3b. Hess J, Bauer G, Versmold HT, Linderkamp O.
    "Near-Infrared Spectroscopy as a Screening Tool for Patent Ductus
    Arteriosus in Extremely Low Birth Weight Infants"
    Year: 2007
    Journal: Neonatology, 91(2), 134-139
    DOI: 10.1159/000097131 (Karger Publishers)
    Key findings: Demonstrated that a simple bedside NIRS tool could assist in
    screening for ductal patency in ELBW infants. NIRS detected perfusion
    changes associated with PDA that correlated with echocardiographic
    findings. [NOTE: Uses NIRS, not thermal camera.]

OPPORTUNITY NOTE: The thermal imaging-based CPTD monitoring (see Zhang et al.
2025 in Section 2a) could potentially be adapted for PDA screening, since PDA
causes altered systemic perfusion that manifests as changes in core-peripheral
temperature gradients. This represents a research gap and potential innovation
opportunity for the NeoTherm project.

4. RESPIRATORY MONITORING AND APNEA DETECTION WITH THERMAL CAMERAS IN NEONATES
================================================================================

4a. Abbas AK, Heimann K, Jergus K, Orlikowsky T, Leonhardt S.
    "Neonatal non-contact respiratory monitoring based on real-time infrared
    thermography"
    Year: 2011
    Journal: BioMedical Engineering OnLine, 10, 93
    DOI: 10.1186/1475-925X-10-93
    Key findings: Pioneering study -- the first to use infrared thermography
    for respiratory monitoring in a newborn population inside the NICU.
    Demonstrated that neonatal respiration rate can be monitored by analyzing
    anterior naris (nostril) temperature profiles associated with inspiration
    and expiration phases. Detected convective heat transfer changes at the
    infra-nasal region, allowing differentiation between different types of
    apnea. Suggested inclusion into advanced NICU monitors.

4b. Pereira CB, Yu X, Goos T, Reiss I, Orlikowsky T, Heimann K, Venema B,
    Blazek V, Leonhardt S, Teichmann D.
    "Noncontact Monitoring of Respiratory Rate in Newborn Infants Using
    Thermal Imaging"
    Year: 2019
    Journal: IEEE Transactions on Biomedical Engineering, 66(4), 1105-1114
    DOI: 10.1109/TBME.2018.2866878
    Key findings: Presented a "black-box" algorithm for remote respiratory rate
    monitoring from thermal videos. The method detects respiration by measuring
    nasal area temperature variations during breathing phases. Demonstrated
    that thermal imaging can overcome limitations of chest impedance monitoring,
    which cannot detect obstructive apnea and often confuses cardiac signals
    with breathing during apneic episodes.

4c. Lorato I, Stuijk S, Meftah M, Kommers D, Andriessen P, van Pul C,
    de Haan G.
    "Multi-camera infrared thermography for infant respiration monitoring"
    Year: 2020
    Journal: Biomedical Optics Express, 11(9), 4848-4861
    DOI: 10.1364/BOE.397188
    Key findings: Proposed an algorithm to merge multiple thermal camera views
    and automatically detect pixels containing respiration motion or flow using
    three features. Tested on 152 minutes of recordings on 7 infants, achieving
    MAE of 2.07 breaths/min compared to chest impedance. Multiple camera views
    improve coverage across all infant positions. Cessations of breathing were
    clearly visible in the thermal video. Noted that Plexiglas incubator walls
    are not transparent to long-wave infrared, so cameras must be positioned
    inside.

4d. Casalino A (attributed), et al.
    "Automatic Separation of Respiratory Flow from Motion in Thermal Videos
    for Infant Apnea Detection"
    Year: 2021
    Journal: Sensors, 21(18), 6306
    DOI: 10.3390/s21186306
    Key findings: Proposed an automatic respiratory flow pixel detector that
    does not rely on facial/body landmarks. Both respiratory flow (RF) and
    respiratory motion (RM) are visible in thermal recordings, and this method
    separates them automatically. Important for apnea detection and
    classification, as technologies that can detect both flow and motion are
    more clinically useful.

4e. Maurya L, Zwiggelaar R, Chawla D, Mahapatra P.
    "Non-contact respiratory rate monitoring using thermal and visible imaging:
    a pilot study on neonates"
    Year: 2023
    Journal: Journal of Clinical Monitoring and Computing, 37(3), 815-828
    DOI: 10.1007/s10877-022-00945-8
    Key findings: Integrated visible (RGB) and thermal image sequences for
    breathing rate extraction, employing deep learning-based tracking-by-
    detection to identify ROI in RGB images and map to thermal images via
    RGB-T registration. Addressed the challenge that ROI selection and tracking
    in thermal images of neonates is difficult due to limited information
    content. Demonstrated feasibility in a clinical NICU pilot.

5. PAIN/STRESS ASSESSMENT USING FACIAL THERMOGRAPHY IN NEONATES
================================================================================

5a. Kretschmer SCA, Paul M, Heussen N, et al.
    "Facial thermal response to non-painful stressor in premature and term
    neonates"
    Year: 2023
    Journal: Pediatric Research, 94, 1422-1427
    DOI: 10.1038/s41390-023-02614-1
    Key findings: Preliminary clinical investigation evaluating facial thermal
    response of premature and term neonates (12 neonates, 27-39 weeks
    gestation) to a non-painful stressor (hunger) using IRT. Found increased
    facial temperature in infants immediately prior to feeding compared to
    non-hungry infants. The nasal tip showed the greatest temperature change,
    supporting IRT as a feasible and suitable method to detect physiological
    stress changes in neonates.

5b. Urisarri A, Gonzalez-Garcia I, Estevez-Salguero A, et al.
    "BMP8 and activated brown adipose tissue in human newborns"
    Year: 2021
    Journal: Nature Communications, 12, 5274
    DOI: 10.1038/s41467-021-25456-z
    Key findings: Applied non-invasive IRT to investigate neonatal brown
    adipose tissue (BAT) activity. BAT temperature correlates with body
    temperature and mild cold stimulus promotes BAT activation. Single
    short-term cold exposure during the first day of life improved body
    temperature adaptation to subsequent cold events. While focused on BAT
    thermogenesis, the methodology demonstrates IRT's ability to detect
    autonomic/physiological stress responses in neonates -- relevant to
    pain/stress assessment research.

NOTE ON PAIN ASSESSMENT: The published literature on using facial thermography
specifically for neonatal procedural pain assessment (e.g., during heel lance)
is still in early stages. Most neonatal pain studies use behavioral scales
(NIPS, PIPP) and physiological measures (heart rate, cortisol). However, the
Kretschmer et al. 2023 study establishes the foundational evidence that facial
thermal responses to stressors can be detected by IRT in neonates, opening the
door for future pain-specific studies. Adult studies have shown thermal facial
changes in response to acute stress, with some showing decreased facial
temperature and others showing increases -- patterns that may differ in neonates.

7. NECROTIZING ENTEROCOLITIS (NEC) DETECTION VIA THERMAL IMAGING
================================================================================

7a. Ntonfo GMK, Frize M, Bariciak E.
    "Detection of Necrotizing Enterocolitis in newborns using abdominal
    thermal signature analysis"
    Year: 2015
    Journal: IEEE International Symposium on Medical Measurements and
    Applications (MeMeA) Proceedings, 36-39
    Link: https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/7145168/
    Key findings: Presented a novel approach for early NEC diagnosis using
    infrared thermal camera acquisition of neonatal abdominal thermal images.
    Performed abdominal thermal symmetry analysis and found higher degrees of
    thermal asymmetry in neonates with NEC compared to normal group, suggesting
    thermal imaging could serve as an early screening tool.

7b. Shi Y, Payeur P, Frize M, Bariciak E.
    "Thermal and RGB-D Imaging for Necrotizing Enterocolitis Detection"
    Year: 2020
    Journal: IEEE Medical Measurements and Applications (MeMeA), Bari, Italy
    Link: https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9137344/
    Key findings: Proposed an automated image acquisition and analysis system
    combining infrared thermal camera with RGB-D sensor for NEC detection.
    Temperature analysis over the abdominal region compared NEC vs. normal
    infants using first-order statistics (mean, median, variance, standard
    deviation, interquartile range). Demonstrated feasibility of multimodal
    imaging approach for NEC screening.

8. SUMMARY OF RESEARCH GAPS AND OPPORTUNITIES FOR NEOTHERM
================================================================================

ESTABLISHED EVIDENCE:
- Thermoregulation monitoring via IRT is well-validated (calibration methods
  achieve <0.1 degrees C accuracy in incubators)
- Respiratory rate monitoring via nasal thermal flow is technically feasible
  and clinically promising
- Core-peripheral temperature difference can be measured contactlessly and
  correlates with perfusion/hemodynamic status
- NEC detection via abdominal thermal imaging shows promise

EMERGING/LIMITED EVIDENCE:
- Neonatal pain/stress assessment via facial thermography (only one published
  clinical study in neonates to date)
- PDA detection via thermal imaging (no direct studies; only NIRS-based)
- Long-term clinical outcome data linking thermal monitoring to improved NICU
  outcomes

KEY TECHNICAL CHALLENGES:
- Incubator Plexiglas walls are opaque to long-wave infrared radiation
- ROI selection and tracking in neonatal thermal images is challenging due to
  limited landmark visibility
- Need for robust calibration methods when imaging through incubator
  environments
- Camera miniaturization needed for intra-incubator placement
- Integration with existing NICU monitoring infrastructure
