1. FOUNDATIONAL STUDIES ON NEONATAL RESPIRATORY THERMAL MONITORING
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 1: Abbas et al. (2011)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Neonatal non-contact respiratory monitoring based on real-time
           infrared thermography"
Authors:  Abbas AK, Heimann K, Jergus K, Orlikowsky T, Leonhardt S
Year:     2011
Journal:  BioMedical Engineering OnLine, 10:93
DOI:      10.1186/1475-925X-10-93
Link:     https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3258209/

Key Findings:
- First study to apply infrared thermography-based respiratory monitoring
  to a newborn infant population inside the NICU (previously only tested
  on adults).
- Respiration was successfully monitored based on a 0.3-0.5 degrees C
  temperature difference between the inspiration and expiration phases at
  the anterior naris (nostrils).
- Used continuous wavelet transformation based on Daubechies wavelet
  function to detect the breathing signal within the thermal image stream.
- Demonstrated feasibility of non-contact respiratory monitoring in the
  challenging NICU environment.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 2: Pereira et al. (2017)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Estimation of respiratory rate from thermal videos of preterm
           infants"
Authors:  Pereira CB, Heimann K, Venema B, Blazek V, Czaplik M, Leonhardt S
Year:     2017
Journal:  39th Annual International Conference of the IEEE Engineering in
          Medicine and Biology Society (EMBC), pp. 3818-3821
DOI:      10.1109/EMBC.2017.8037689
Link:     https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/8037689/

Key Findings:
- Used temperature modulation around the nostrils over the respiratory cycle
  to extract respiratory rate from preterm infants.
- Incorporated a tracking algorithm to compensate for movement artifacts,
  a key challenge in neonatal monitoring.
- Served as an important early demonstration that thermal video processing
  could be applied specifically to preterm infants in clinical settings.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 3: Pereira et al. (2019)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Noncontact Monitoring of Respiratory Rate in Newborn Infants
           Using Thermal Imaging"
Authors:  Pereira CB, Yu X, Goos T, Reiss I, Orlikowsky T, Heimann K,
          Blazek V, Leonhardt S
Year:     2019 (published online August 2018)
Journal:  IEEE Transactions on Biomedical Engineering, Vol. 66, No. 4,
          pp. 1105-1114
DOI:      10.1109/TBME.2018.2866878
Link:     https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30139045/

Key Findings:
- Presented a "black-box" algorithm for remote respiratory rate monitoring
  in thermal videos that does NOT rely on tracking of specific anatomic
  landmarks. Instead, it automatically distinguishes regions of interest
  containing respiratory signal from those containing only noise.
- Validated on 12 healthy adult volunteers first, then conducted a clinical
  study on 8 newborn infants in the NICU.
- Mean RMSE of approximately 4.15 +/- 1.44 breaths/min when compared to
  reference methods.
- Landmark-free approach is significant because neonatal faces are small
  and facial feature detection is unreliable in this population.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 4: Dos Santos et al. (2021)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "The use of thermographic techniques and analysis of thermal images
           to monitor the respiratory rate of premature new-borns"
Authors:  Dos Santos KM et al.
Year:     2021
Journal:  Case Studies in Thermal Engineering, Vol. 25, 100897
DOI:      10.1016/j.csite.2021.100897
Link:     https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2214157X21000897

Key Findings:
- Presented thermographic imaging as a real-time monitoring tool for
  respiratory rate in premature newborns, analyzing temperature changes
  at the anterior nasal area (nostrils).
- Emphasized the non-invasive nature as critical for monitoring this very
  fragile patient population.
- Demonstrated that thermal monitoring could track respiration continuously
  without the skin damage risks associated with adhesive electrodes.

2. APNEA DETECTION AND CLASSIFICATION (OBSTRUCTIVE VS CENTRAL)
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 5: Lorato et al. (2021a) — Apnea Detection
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Automatic Separation of Respiratory Flow from Motion in Thermal
           Videos for Infant Apnea Detection"
Authors:  Lorato I, Stuijk S, Meftah M, Kommers D, Andriessen P,
          van Pul C, de Haan G
Year:     2021
Journal:  Sensors, 21(18), 6306
DOI:      10.3390/s21186306
Link:     https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8472592/

Key Findings:
- Proposed an automatic Respiratory Flow (RF) pixel detector NOT based on
  facial/body landmarks. Based on properties of RF pixels in thermal videos
  which appear in areas with a smooth circular gradient.
- Five features combined with Gabor filters allow automatic selection of RF
  pixels, enabling separation of respiratory flow from respiratory motion.
- This separation is CRITICAL for apnea classification:
    * Central apnea (CA): both respiratory motion AND flow are absent
    * Obstructive apnea (OA): respiratory motion present but flow absent
    * Mixed apnea: combination of both
- Used low-cost thermal cameras on 15 infants in open beds (3 hours each).
  Low resolution makes cameras cheaper and reduces privacy concerns.
- Achieved 84% accuracy in separating flow from motion.
- Patent applications filed for both the algorithm and a smart soother device.

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE FOR APNEA DIFFERENTIATION:
- Current chest impedance (CI) monitoring used in clinical practice has a
  MAJOR limitation: it cannot distinguish between obstructive and central
  apnea. CI detects chest wall motion only, so obstructive apneas (where
  the infant makes breathing efforts against a blocked airway) appear as
  normal breathing on CI.
- Thermal imaging can detect BOTH flow (nasal airflow temperature changes)
  AND motion (chest wall movement), making it uniquely suited to
  differentiate apnea types — a clinically important distinction because
  treatment differs for each type.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 6: Contactless Apnea Event Detection (2025)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Contactless apnea event detection using visible-thermal imaging"
Authors:  (Authors from Signal, Image and Video Processing)
Year:     2025
Journal:  Signal, Image and Video Processing
DOI:      10.1007/s11760-025-03959-2
Link:     https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11760-025-03959-2

Key Findings:
- Combined far infrared and near infrared imaging for breathing extraction
  by analyzing chest motion AND nostril temperature variation.
- Used a support vector machine (SVM) to classify apnea events.
- Achieved F1 scores of 0.86 for obstructive apnea and 0.93 for central
  apnea detection.
- Demonstrates that multi-modal thermal+visible imaging can reliably
  classify different apnea types in a contactless manner.

3. MULTI-CAMERA AND ADVANCED ALGORITHM APPROACHES
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 7: Lorato et al. (2020) — Multi-Camera
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Multi-camera infrared thermography for infant respiration
           monitoring"
Authors:  Lorato I, Stuijk S, Meftah M, Kommers D, Andriessen P,
          van Pul C, de Haan G
Year:     2020
Journal:  Biomedical Optics Express, Vol. 11, pp. 4848-4861
DOI:      10.1364/BOE.396222
Link:     https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7510882/

Key Findings:
- Proposed an algorithm to merge three low-resolution thermal camera views
  and automatically detect pixels containing respiration motion or flow.
- Tested on 152 minutes of recordings from 7 infants.
- Comparison to chest impedance respiratory rate yielded a mean absolute
  error of 2.07 breaths/min.
- Three features used for pixel classification:
    1. Pseudo-periodicity (predominant frequency of each pixel)
    2. Respiration rate clusters (similarity of pixel to neighbors)
    3. Thermal gradient (measure of thermal contrast)
- Addressed key challenges: did NOT depend on facial landmark detection,
  handled significant infant motion, and managed the presence of another
  person (e.g., parent or nurse) in the field of view.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 8: Lorato et al. (2021b) — Continuous Monitoring
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Towards Continuous Camera-Based Respiration Monitoring in Infants"
Authors:  Lorato I, Stuijk S, Meftah M, Kommers D, Andriessen P,
          van Pul C, de Haan G
Year:     2021
Journal:  Sensors, 21(7), 2268
DOI:      10.3390/s21072268
Link:     https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33804913/

Key Findings:
- Focused on continuous unobtrusive respiration monitoring with robust
  motion handling.
- Proposed a motion detector optimized for severe infant motion (head and
  limb movements, non-nutritive sucking) combined with an automatic pixel
  selection strategy for respiratory rate detection.
- Dataset: 43 hours of both thermal AND RGB video from 17 infants.
- Method successfully applied to BOTH RGB and thermal videos, compared
  against chest impedance signals.
- Addressed motion robustness as the primary challenge for continuous
  clinical deployment.

4. COMBINED RGB-THERMAL IMAGING FOR RESPIRATORY RATE
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 9: Maurya et al. (2022)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Non-contact respiratory rate monitoring using thermal and visible
           imaging: a pilot study on neonates"
Authors:  Maurya L, Zwiggelaar R, Chawla D, Mahapatra P
Year:     2022 (published online December 2022)
Journal:  Journal of Clinical Monitoring and Computing, 37, pp. 1175-1188
DOI:      10.1007/s10877-022-00945-8
Link:     https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10175339/

Key Findings:
- Integrated visible (RGB) and thermal (T) image sequences for region of
  interest selection and tracking for breathing rate extraction.
- Used a deep-learning based tracking-by-detection approach: detects ROI in
  RGB images and maps it to thermal images using RGB-T image registration.
- In healthy adults: average absolute error of 0.1 bpm in stable conditions,
  ~1.5 bpm with motion, ~1.8 bpm while talking.
- In neonates: average error of 1.5 bpm.
- Bland-Altman analysis showed good agreement with reference method.
- Provided evidence for using this approach as a contactless method for
  respiratory rate detection of neonates in clinical settings.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 10: Lorato et al. (2024) — Comprehensive Review
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Thermal Cameras for Continuous and Contactless Respiration
           Monitoring"
Authors:  Lorato I et al.
Year:     2024
Journal:  Sensors, 24(24), 8118
DOI:      10.3390/s24248118
Link:     https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/24/24/8118

Key Findings:
- Comprehensive review of the state-of-the-art in respiration monitoring
  using thermal cameras, dividing the system into: (1) acquiring data,
  (2) defining and tracking region of interest, (3) extracting breathing
  signal and respiration rate.
- Highlights advantages of thermal cameras: contactless nature,
  affordability, ability to capture both respiration motion and flow,
  ability to operate in complete darkness without additional lighting
  (suitable for overnight monitoring).
- Notes that thermal cameras have become a focal point in research due to
  their potential for clinical deployment in neonatal and other settings.

5. DEEP LEARNING / CNN APPROACHES FOR RESPIRATORY ANOMALY DETECTION
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 11: CNN-Based Neonatal Respiratory Anomaly Detection
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Convolutional Neural Networks-Based Approach to Detect Neonatal
           Respiratory System Anomalies with Limited Thermal Image"
Authors:  Cay G et al.
Year:     2021
Journal:  Traitement du Signal, Vol. 38, No. 2, pp. 437-442
DOI:      10.18280/ts.380222
Link:     https://www.iieta.org/journals/ts/paper/10.18280/ts.380222

Key Findings:
- Applied CNNs to classify neonatal respiratory abnormalities from thermal
  images. Studied 34 neonates (16 pulmonary diseases, 10 cardiovascular,
  8 renal/abdominal anomalies).
- With data augmentation (4x increase in images via brightness, sharpening,
  contrast adjustments), classification accuracy improved from 84.5% to
  90.9%.
- Demonstrates potential for automated screening of respiratory anomalies
  (including respiratory distress syndrome) using thermal imaging combined
  with deep learning.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 12: Health Status Detection via IRT + Deep CNN
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Health status detection of neonates using infrared thermography
           and deep convolutional neural networks"
Authors:  (Various)
Year:     2020
Journal:  Infrared Physics & Technology
Link:     https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1350449519303123

Key Findings:
- Achieved 99.58% accuracy, 99.73% specificity, 99.43% sensitivity, and
  0.996 AUC for classification using 30,400 thermal images.
- Demonstrated that thermal anomalies in neonates can be reliably detected
  using infrared thermography combined with deep CNNs.
- Capable of monitoring heart rate, respiratory rate, sleeplessness, and
  restlessness — all from thermal imaging data.

6. ENDOTRACHEAL TUBE TEMPERATURE MONITORING VIA IRT
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 13: Hamada et al. (2025)
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Internal Temperature of Neonatal Endotracheal Tube Predicted by
           Infrared Thermography: A Neonatal Bench Study"
Authors:  Hamada K, Hirakawa E, Tanabe T, Mine T, Ichikawa T
Year:     2025
Journal:  Pediatric Pulmonology
DOI:      10.1002/ppul.27425
Link:     https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ppul.27425

Key Findings:
- Addressed the problem that neonatal ventilators monitor inspiratory gas
  temperature outside the incubator, but temperature decreases as gas passes
  through the circuit and ETT — no established method exists for measuring
  internal ETT temperature.
- Established a new method for predicting internal ETT temperature using
  IRT camera to measure external surface temperature, validated against
  thin thermocouple measurements inside the tube.
- Achieved high accuracy: R^2 adj = 0.946 (proximal end) and 0.974
  (distal end).
- Non-contact method allows continuous monitoring without interfering with
  ventilation. Important for airway management and preventing thermal
  injury in the NICU.

NOTE: This is a bench study (not yet tested on live neonates), but
represents an important proof-of-concept for IRT in airway management.

7. SCOPING / COMPREHENSIVE REVIEWS
================================================================================

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 14: Topalidou et al. (2019) — Scoping Review
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Thermal imaging applications in neonatal care: a scoping review"
Authors:  Topalidou A, Ali N, Sekulic S, Downe S
Year:     2019
Journal:  BMC Pregnancy and Childbirth, 19:388
DOI:      10.1186/s12884-019-2533-y
Link:     https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6814124/

Key Findings:
- Searched EMBASE, MEDLINE, MIDIRS; from 442 hits, 21 met inclusion criteria.
- Five broad themes emerged:
    1. General thermal physiology
    2. Heat loss and respiratory monitoring
    3. Identification of internal pathologies (e.g., necrotising enterocolitis)
    4. Other uses of thermal imaging
    5. Technical concerns
- Concluded that thermal imaging is a reliable and non-invasive method for
  continuous monitoring of neonatal temperature, with potential for
  contributing to wellbeing assurance and pathology diagnosis.
- Noted that despite promising results since the 1970s, no large prospective
  studies have examined both benefits and barriers to clinical use.
- Key methodological challenges: environmental parameters (incubators,
  radiant warmers), standardization of measurement protocols.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Paper 15: Pereira et al. (2021) — Non-contact Breathing Rate Review
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Title:    "Non-contact breathing rate monitoring in newborns: A review"
Authors:  Pereira CB et al.
Year:     2021
Journal:  Computers in Biology and Medicine, Vol. 131, 104257
DOI:      10.1016/j.compbiomed.2021.104257
Link:     https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0010482521001153

Key Findings:
- Comprehensive review of non-contact breathing rate monitoring technologies
  for newborns, including thermal imaging, radar-based, and RGB camera-based
  approaches.
- Highlighted that chest impedance-based monitoring (the clinical standard)
  has major issues including mistaking the cardiac signal for breathing
  during apnea events — a critical failure mode.
- Noted immense scope for development in both thermal and radar-based
  modalities for neonatal respiratory monitoring.

8. SUMMARY OF KEY GAPS AND NOTES
================================================================================

TOPICS WITH STRONG EVIDENCE:
- Respiratory rate monitoring via nasal airflow thermal imaging (Papers 1-4, 7-9)
- Apnea detection and obstructive vs central differentiation (Papers 5-6)
- Non-contact vs contact comparison (discussed in Papers 14-16 reviews)

TOPICS WITH LIMITED/EMERGING EVIDENCE:
- CPAP/ventilator leak detection via thermal imaging:
  ** No published studies found specifically using thermal/infrared cameras
     for CPAP leak detection in neonates. Current leak detection relies on
     flow sensors, pressure monitoring, and subjective clinical observation.
  ** This represents a SIGNIFICANT RESEARCH GAP and potential innovation
     area for NeoTherm. Thermal cameras could theoretically detect warm
     exhaled air leaking around CPAP interfaces by visualizing temperature
     differentials at the mask-face seal.

- Endotracheal tube placement verification:
  ** No studies found using thermal cameras to verify ETT position.
  ** Hamada et al. (2025) is the closest — uses IRT to predict internal ETT
     temperature (bench study). A separate line of research uses near-
     infrared light (Opt-ETT device) for continuous ETT position monitoring,
     but this is not thermal imaging.
  ** Potential application: thermal cameras could detect exhaled warm air
     patterns to confirm ETT is in the trachea (not esophagus), but this
     has not been studied.

- Pneumothorax detection via thermal imaging:
  ** No direct studies found. One CNN study (Paper 11) detected neonatal
     respiratory anomalies (including pulmonary diseases) from thermal
     images, but did not specifically address pneumothorax.
  ** Theoretical basis: a pneumothorax could cause asymmetric chest wall
     thermal patterns due to altered ventilation, but this remains
     unvalidated.

- Respiratory distress syndrome assessment via thermal patterns:
  ** No direct studies found specifically correlating thermal imaging
     patterns with RDS severity or diagnosis.
  ** The CNN approaches (Papers 11-12) classified respiratory anomalies
     broadly but did not isolate RDS as a specific detection target.

IMPLICATIONS FOR NEOTHERM PROJECT
================================================================================

1. RESPIRATORY RATE MONITORING is the most validated thermal imaging
   application in neonatal care, with multiple studies showing feasibility
   and reasonable accuracy (MAE 1.5-4.15 bpm).

2. APNEA CLASSIFICATION (obstructive vs central) is a unique advantage of
   thermal imaging over chest impedance — this is a strong differentiator
   and clinical value proposition. Chest impedance CANNOT distinguish apnea
   types; thermal imaging CAN.

3. The field is moving toward MULTI-MODAL approaches (RGB + thermal) and
   DEEP LEARNING for more robust detection.

4. LOW-COST thermal cameras have been validated (Lorato et al.) — important
   for cost-sensitive NICU deployments.

5. KEY UNSOLVED CHALLENGES:
   - Motion robustness (head turning, limb movement, non-nutritive sucking)
   - Operation inside incubators (germanium windows needed for IR)
   - Standardization of measurement protocols
   - No large-scale prospective clinical trials yet

6. UNEXPLORED OPPORTUNITIES (potential NeoTherm innovations):
   - CPAP leak detection via thermal visualization
   - ETT placement confirmation via exhaled air thermal patterns
   - Pneumothorax detection via asymmetric thermal patterns
   - RDS severity scoring from thermal breathing patterns
