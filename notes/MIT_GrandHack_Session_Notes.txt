SECTION 1: IRT FOR SEPSIS — EVALUATION FROM 6 RESEARCH PAPERS
================================================================================

Papers reviewed:
  1. Du et al. (2026) — Emergency Medicine International (Systematic Review)
  2. Hasanin et al. (2024) — BMC Anesthesiology
  3. Stanley et al. (2024) — Frontiers in Medicine (Systematic Review)
  4. Lath et al. (2025) — International Journal of Emergency Medicine
  5. Ferraris et al. (2018) — PLOS ONE
  6. Kazune et al. (2023) — Bioengineering

--- IS IRT BETTER THAN STANDARD METHODS? ---

No study demonstrates IRT is definitively superior to serum lactate, CRT,
SOFA, or APACHE II. Two studies (Hasanin, Ferraris) explicitly show it is
inferior or non-significant. Best framing: useful ADJUNCT, not replacement.

Where IRT adds value over standard methods:
  - Dark-skinned patients (mottling score unreliable)
  - Resource-limited settings (lactate unavailable)
  - Infection control / non-contact requirements
  - Early warning: detects physiological changes HOURS before clinical signs
  - Continuous monitoring without disturbing the patient

--- ACCURACY STATS ---

  Best performance:
    - Paediatric ICU deep learning thermal video: AUC 0.81 ± 0.06
      at 5h pre-shock (Vats et al.)
    - Deep learning ResNet model: 84% sensitivity / 91% specificity (Luo et al.)

  Moderate performance:
    - Toe temperature at 12h: AUC 0.72, NPV 70% (Hasanin)
    - Leg thermal heterogeneity: AUROC 0.866 for mortality (Luo et al.)
    - Core-to-knee gradient for 7-day mortality: AUC 0.62 (Lath)

  Poor / null performance:
    - Core-to-toe for 28-day mortality: AUCs 0.5–0.6 (Lath)
    - Fingertip IRT in COVID-19 sepsis: no predictive value (Gutowski)
    - Knee temperature alone: not significant for ICU mortality (Ferraris)

--- IS IT REALISTIC TO OFFER IN HOSPITALS? ---

  Technically yes. Devices (FLIR C2, E8, A600) are commercially available,
  portable, no consumables, ~£500–£3,000. Measurements take 5–10 seconds.

  Barriers:
    - Needs temperature-controlled room (~22°C)
    - 10-minute patient acclimatisation required
    - No validated clinical protocol currently exists
    - Manual image analysis without AI is time-consuming
    - No formal cost-effectiveness studies published

--- IS IT WORTH USING vs. EMPIRICAL TREATMENT? ---

  No — does not replace empirical treatment. Sepsis protocols should not
  be delayed for IRT. Role is in monitoring, risk stratification, and
  supplementing existing tools.

SECTION 2: IRT IN NEONATAL MEDICINE & RADIATION RISK
================================================================================

--- NEONATAL EVIDENCE ---

  Direct NICU use (Barcat 2017, cited in Stanley et al.):
    - IRT used in 29 preterm neonates to study sleep onset
    - Narrower CPTDs associated with shorter wakefulness episodes
    - Novel observation: vasodilation promotes sleep onset in preterm neonates

  Neonatal sepsis marker (Leante-Castellanos 2017, cited in Du et al.):
    - Persistent CPTD >2°C for ≥4 hours = sensitive early diagnostic marker
      for LATE-ONSET neonatal sepsis
    - This is the key validated threshold for neonatal IRT application

  Paediatric ICU evidence:
    - Nagori et al. (2019): ML on thermal gradients, AUC 0.75–0.77
    - Vats et al. (2022): LSTM deep learning, AUC 0.81, detected shock
      5 hours before onset

  Caution (Stanley et al.):
    "IRT should be used with caution in children until more data are
    available to assist interpretation."

--- RADIATION RISK ---

  NONE. IRT is completely non-ionising.

  The camera passively detects infrared radiation (8–14 μm wavelength)
  naturally emitted by the body as heat. It emits nothing at the patient.
  Explicitly described as "non-ionising" across all reviewed literature.

  SAFETY NOTE: Active dynamic thermography (ADT), which applies a cold
  stimulus, DOES carry risk in hypoperfused patients. Standard passive
  IRT has no such concern.

SECTION 3: HOW IRT DETECTS SEPSIS — THE MECHANISM
================================================================================

--- THE PHYSICS ---

Every object above absolute zero emits infrared radiation proportional to
its surface temperature (Stefan-Boltzmann's law). The human body naturally
emits infrared in the 8–14 μm wavelength range.

An IRT camera is entirely passive — it reads naturally emitted body heat
pixel by pixel, converting it into a real-time pseudocolour temperature map.

--- THE PHYSIOLOGY ---

In sepsis, the inflammatory response disrupts blood distribution:

  Step 1 — Cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β) cause central vasodilation,
           dropping systemic vascular resistance.

  Step 2 — To protect vital organs, the sympathetic nervous system triggers
           peripheral vasoconstriction (hands, feet, legs, skin).

  Step 3 — Reduced peripheral blood flow → less warm blood reaching
           extremities → periphery cools while core stays warm.

  Step 4 — A measurable Central-Peripheral Temperature Difference (CPTD)
           develops. This is what IRT detects.

Validated threshold: >2°C difference sustained for ≥4 hours
= early marker for sepsis/septic shock.

--- WHAT IRT MEASURES ---

  - CPTD: degree of peripheral vasoconstriction / shock severity
  - Toe/fingertip temperature: distal perfusion (lowest in shock)
  - Thermal heterogeneity: uneven microvascular flow (mottling made objective)
  - Rate of temperature change over time: trajectory of deterioration
  - Spatial skin temperature gradient maps

--- WHY IRT DETECTS SEPSIS EARLIER THAN CLINICAL SIGNS ---

  Peripheral vasoconstriction → skin cooling  ← IRT detects HERE
           ↓
  Blood pressure starts falling
           ↓
  Tachycardia, altered mental status, reduced urine output
           ↓
  Clinical sepsis recognised at bedside       ← Currently detected HERE

Vats et al.: IRT predicted septic shock 5 HOURS before onset.

--- WHY NEONATES ARE ESPECIALLY SUITED ---

  - High surface-area-to-volume ratio → skin temp more sensitive to
    perfusion changes
  - Immature thermoregulation → more pronounced peripheral cooling response
    to infection
  - Cannot communicate symptoms → objective monitoring is critical

SECTION 4: NEONATAL SEPSIS STATISTICS — UNITED STATES
================================================================================

--- THE SCALE ---

  - More than 18 babies under 28 days old die from sepsis EVERY DAY in the US
    (Source: UF Health, 2024)
  - Neonatal sepsis represents 18% of all baby deaths in the US
  - >75,000 babies and children develop severe sepsis each year in the US
  - Annual neonatal sepsis healthcare costs: $1.1 billion/year
  - Neonates have the HIGHEST rate of ICU admissions for sepsis of any
    age group in the US

  Premature babies:
    - 33% of infants born <28 weeks will acquire sepsis during NICU stay
    - 36% of premature babies born before 28 weeks have ≥1 episode of
      bacteremia during initial hospitalisation
    - EOS is 10–20x more likely in premature very low birthweight infants

--- MORTALITY (US) ---

  All septic infants (US):                    ~10%
  Infants with any comorbidity:               up to 30%
  Premature infants <28 weeks (bacteremia):   up to 50%
  Post-discharge mortality vs. non-sepsis:    RR 2.1

--- LONG-TERM OUTCOMES IN SURVIVORS ---

  - 35% of culture-proven sepsis survivors develop neurodevelopmental
    impairment vs. 17% without sepsis
  - ~50% of newborns who survive sepsis require ≥1 additional hospitalisation
  - Cerebral palsy: OR 1.4–1.7
  - Developmental delay: OR 1.3–1.6
  - Vision impairment: OR 1.3–2.2
  - Neurosensory deficits overall: OR 3.18 (95% CI 2.29–4.41)
  - Periventricular leukomalacia (PVL): 22.4%
  - Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD): 30.8%
  - Retinopathy of prematurity (ROP): 10.5%
  - Impaired head growth at 2 years corrected age

--- ECONOMIC BURDEN (US) ---

  Total US cost treating severe sepsis in children (2016): $7.31 billion
    (up from $4.8B in 2005)
  Annual neonatal sepsis healthcare costs:                 $1.1 billion/yr
  Additional cost per late-onset sepsis episode (preterm): ~$32,954
  Hospital cost per 1,000 births — current screening:      $182,491
  Hospital cost per 1,000 births — enhanced observation:   $85,879 (53% less)
  Neonatal sepsis patients cost 2–3x more per admission than adults

--- ANTIBIOTIC OVERTREATMENT ---

  - 50–100 neonates receive empiric IV antibiotics for every 1 confirmed
    case of early-onset sepsis (up to 250x the actual incidence)
  - Consequences: antimicrobial resistance, microbiome disruption, increased
    chronic disease risk, unnecessary hospitalisation, family separation

SECTION 5: EARLY-ONSET vs. LATE-ONSET SEPSIS — IRT APPLICABILITY
================================================================================

  Early-Onset Sepsis (EOS): within first 72 hours of life
  Late-Onset Sepsis (LOS): after 72 hours

  IRT is NOT well-suited for EOS because:
    - All newborns undergo massive thermoregulatory transition at birth
    - Normal CPTD is unstable and variable in first hours — no baseline
    - EOS often presents with hypothermia, not fever — signal less predictable
    - No validated IRT threshold exists for EOS
    - Thermoregulatory instability makes sepsis signal indistinguishable
      from normal newborn physiology

  IRT IS well-suited for LOS because:
    - Stable physiological baseline established by 72 hours
    - Baby is already in NICU, continuously monitored
    - >2°C sustained CPTD is validated specifically for LOS
    - LOS accounts for 94.9% of all neonatal sepsis episodes

  CONCLUSION: Focus on LATE-ONSET neonatal sepsis.
  Acknowledge EOS as a limitation/future direction in the problem statement.


================================================================================
SECTION 6: WHY THE NICU IS THE IDEAL ENVIRONMENT FOR IRT
================================================================================

  1. High-risk population already identified and captive
  2. LOS dominates — clear monitoring window (after 72h, during NICU stay)
  3. No radiation, non-contact — ideal for fragile neonates
  4. Diagnostic marker already validated (CPTD >2°C for ≥4h)
  5. NICU solves IRT's main practical barriers:
       - Incubators are already temperature-controlled
       - Babies are immobile and undressed
       - No acclimatisation problem
       - Continuous monitoring is standard of care — IRT fits seamlessly
  6. Technology demonstrated in NICU setting:
       - Continuous monitoring shown with <0.55°C accuracy
       - Respiratory rate, skin temperature, CPTD all measurable
         non-contact and continuously
  7. Could dramatically reduce antibiotic overtreatment
  8. At $7.31B/year in paediatric sepsis costs, even modest improvement
     in early detection has enormous economic and clinical ROI

SECTION 7: PROBLEM STATEMENT (FINAL)
================================================================================

"In the US, more than 18 babies under 28 days old die from sepsis every
day, yet current detection methods — blood cultures, inflammatory markers,
and clinical observation — identify sepsis only after clinical deterioration
has already begun. This diagnostic delay drives both preventable deaths and
rampant antibiotic overtreatment, with 50–100 neonates receiving empiric IV
antibiotics for every single confirmed case."

Key decisions made:
  - Focus: ALL neonates (not just premature)
  - Sepsis type: Late-onset (not early-onset — IRT not validated for EOS)
  - No solution described in problem statement
  - Stakeholder: Neonates in NICU
  - Current practice: blood cultures, CRP, WCC, clinical observation
  - Gap: reactive not preventative; no continuous non-invasive early warning
