US REGULATORY AND LEGAL FRAMEWORK FOR AI SEPSIS THERMOGRAPHY SOFTWARE
=======================================================================

SOFTWARE LAYER ON A 510(k)-CLEARED DEVICE: WHAT'S REQUIRED
============================================================

1. DOES THE AI SOFTWARE NEED ITS OWN FDA CLEARANCE?
----------------------------------------------------
Almost certainly yes — but there's a clear path. The cleared hardware device's 510(k) does not
extend to downstream software that outputs a clinical risk score. The software is a separate
Software as a Medical Device (SaMD).

The CDS exemption won't save you. To skip FDA review, software must meet 4 criteria simultaneously.
The sepsis AI layer fails at least two:
- It processes medical images (thermal) — explicitly excluded by FDA's 2026 CDS guidance
- It outputs a specific clinical risk score in a time-sensitive emergency setting

The good news: There are now real predicates. FDA authorized the Sepsis ImmunoScore
(DEN230036, April 2024) — the first AI sepsis prediction tool — via De Novo. AlgoDx's
NAVOY CDS then got a 510(k) citing it as predicate. A thermal AI could potentially pursue
a 510(k) using these as predicates, though the different input modality (thermal vs. EHR
lab values) may require De Novo instead.

Recommended first step: Request a Pre-Sub (Q-Sub) meeting with FDA before committing to a
pathway — free, takes ~3 months, and tells you definitively which route is required.


2. QUALITY SYSTEM REQUIREMENTS
-------------------------------
Under the new QMSR (effective February 2, 2026), the startup must maintain:
- Full design controls and software validation
- Complaint handling and CAPA
- Post-market MDR adverse event reporting (21 CFR Part 803)
- A Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP) — highly recommended if the AI model will be
  retrained over time, as it lets you update the algorithm post-clearance without filing a
  new 510(k)


3. HIPAA — AGREEMENTS REQUIRED
--------------------------------
Thermal images of patients are PHI. The startup is a Business Associate the moment any
patient data touches its systems.

Agreement            | When Required                              | Who Signs
---------------------|--------------------------------------------|--------------------------
BAA                  | Before any PHI flows                       | Startup + every hospital
Sub-BAA              | If startup uses cloud vendors touching PHI | Startup + each subcontractor
Data Use Agreement   | If hospital provides data for model training| Startup + hospital
IRB Agreement        | If any data collection constitutes research | Startup + hospital's IRB

Key BAA rules:
- The startup cannot use patient thermal images to retrain the model without explicit
  authorization or a DUA covering de-identified data
- Any breach of PHI must be reported to the hospital within 60 days
- Penalties for BAA failures: up to $1.9M per violation category


4. FULL HOSPITAL CONTRACT STACK
---------------------------------
To deploy in a hospital the startup needs:
1. BAA — HIPAA compliance, non-negotiable
2. MSA/SaaS Agreement — license, SLAs, IP ownership, liability caps
3. DUA — if using patient data for model improvement
4. IT Security Assessment — SOC 2 Type II, HECVAT questionnaire, penetration test reports;
   increasingly HITRUST CSF certification required by large health systems
5. IRB Agreement — if any pilot/validation study is involved


5. NEONATAL-SPECIFIC PRIVACY
------------------------------
- Parents are the HIPAA personal representative for neonates — any research use of neonatal
  thermal images requires parental consent (both parents under Common Rule Subpart B),
  no waivers allowed
- De-identification is harder for neonates — small NICU populations have higher
  re-identification risk; use Expert Determination method, not just Safe Harbor
- California, New York, Texas each have state health privacy laws stricter than HIPAA


6. INTEROPERABILITY STANDARDS
-------------------------------
Standard        | Purpose                                  | Required?
----------------|------------------------------------------|---------------------------
DICOM           | Receiving thermal images from device/PACS | Yes, for hospital integration
HL7 FHIR R4     | Writing risk score back to EHR           | Yes, ONC-mandated
SMART on FHIR   | Authorization for EHR app access         | Yes (Epic, Oracle, Meditech)
HL7 v2          | Legacy hospital workflow systems         | Often needed

Note: No dedicated DICOM modality tag exists for infrared thermography — a practical
integration challenge the startup needs to solve early.


7. LIABILITY PROTECTION
------------------------
Key contract provisions the startup must fight for:
- "Adjunctive use only" labeling — required by FDA and the primary liability shield
- Limitation of liability cap — fees paid in prior 12 months
- Consequential damages waiver — mutual, no lost profits claims
- Indemnification carve-out for clinician misuse or off-label use
- Bias testing documentation — hospitals increasingly demand indemnification for
  algorithmic bias claims; document demographic performance testing in the FDA submission

Insurance hospitals will require:
- E&O / Technology E&O: $2-5M
- Cyber liability: $2-5M
- Product liability: separate coverage often required
- CGL: $1-2M per occurrence


BOTTOM LINE
-----------
The software-on-cleared-hardware approach is strategically smart because:
- The hardware regulatory burden is already solved
- The software's own FDA path is shorter than it was 2 years ago (predicates now exist)
- The "adjunctive tool" framing significantly reduces liability
- BAA + MSA is standard enterprise SaaS — not exotic

Pitch framing: "We don't build the camera. We build the AI brain that turns any cleared
thermal camera into a sepsis early warning system — plugging into the hospital's EHR via FHIR."
