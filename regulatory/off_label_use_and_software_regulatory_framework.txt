OFF-LABEL USE OF FDA-CLEARED DEVICES & SOFTWARE REGULATORY FRAMEWORK
======================================================================
Research compiled: 2026-03-14
Applicable to: NeoTherm — software platform using FDA-cleared thermal cameras in NICU

This document answers seven specific regulatory questions relevant to NeoTherm's
business model: building a software platform that takes input from an FDA 510(k)-
cleared thermal camera and uses it for neonatal monitoring in the NICU.

================================================================================

1. CAN PHYSICIANS LEGALLY USE FDA-CLEARED MEDICAL DEVICES OFF-LABEL?
=====================================================================

YES. Unequivocally.

LEGAL BASIS:
- The FDA does NOT regulate the practice of medicine. This is a foundational
  principle codified in the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FD&C Act).
  State law, not federal law, provides the authority and legal standards for the
  practice of medicine.

- 21 CFR 801.4 ("Meaning of intended uses") governs how the FDA determines a
  device's intended use — but this regulation applies to MANUFACTURERS, not to
  physicians. A firm's "intended use" is determined by labeling claims, advertising,
  and circumstances of distribution — not by how a doctor ultimately uses the device.

- The FDA itself has stated: "Once an approved device or drug is available, doctors
  can prescribe it for any purpose they believe is warranted — regardless of what
  the label says." (FDA FAQ on Medical Devices)

- The American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons (AAOS) position statement confirms:
  "Federal laws governing FDA regulations and drug use place no restrictions on
  physicians' use of the product, and the agency has no authority to either allow
  or disallow off-label use."

PHYSICIAN RESPONSIBILITIES FOR OFF-LABEL USE:
- Must be based on "firm scientific rationale and sound medical evidence"
- Physician should maintain records of the product's use and effects
- If expanding use to multiple patients systematically or publishing results, the
  activity may cross into clinical research, requiring an IDE (Investigational
  Device Exemption) and IRB approval
- Standard malpractice liability applies — physician must exercise reasonable
  clinical judgment

CRITICAL DISTINCTION — MANUFACTURER vs. PHYSICIAN:
- PHYSICIANS: Free to use any cleared device for any purpose they deem clinically
  appropriate. This is the practice of medicine.
- MANUFACTURERS: CANNOT promote, advertise, or market a device for off-label uses.
  FDA regulations strictly prohibit manufacturer promotion of off-label uses.
- Under 21 CFR 801.4, a manufacturer is not regarded as intending an unapproved use
  "based solely on that firm's knowledge that such device was being prescribed or
  used by health care providers for such use." (2021 Final Rule on Intended Use)

NICU-SPECIFIC CONTEXT:
- Off-label use is especially common — and often the standard of care — in
  pediatric and neonatal medicine. Most devices used in NICUs were never specifically
  cleared or approved for neonatal populations.
- A study by Shah et al. found that 78.9% of children discharged from pediatric
  hospitals were taking at least one off-label medication.
- The FDA explicitly acknowledges this reality in its pediatric device guidance.

BOTTOM LINE: A neonatologist CAN legally use an FDA 510(k)-cleared thermal camera
(product code LHQ, "adjunctive diagnostic screening") in the NICU for neonatal
temperature monitoring. No additional FDA clearance is needed for the PHYSICIAN'S
clinical use of the device.

2. CAN A HOSPITAL USE A 510(k)-CLEARED THERMAL CAMERA IN THE NICU?
====================================================================

YES. If the camera has a valid 510(k) clearance for "adjunctive diagnostic
screening," a hospital can deploy it in any clinical setting, including the NICU.

HOW IT WORKS:
- Thermal cameras cleared under product code LHQ (21 CFR 884.2980) are cleared for
  "use as an adjunct to other clinical diagnostic procedures in the diagnosis,
  quantifying, and screening of differences in skin surface temperature changes."
- This clearance does NOT restrict the camera to a particular clinical setting
  (e.g., breast clinic vs. NICU). The clearance is for a FUNCTION (adjunctive
  thermal imaging), not a LOCATION.
- A hospital's use of the camera in the NICU for temperature pattern monitoring is
  off-label use by the treating physician — which is legal (see Section 1 above).
- The hospital may need to go through its own internal processes:
  - Medical staff committee approval for new technology
  - Clinical engineering/biomedical review
  - If used in a research protocol, IRB approval
  - Risk management review (for liability purposes)

IMPORTANT CAVEATS:
- The HOSPITAL (and NeoTherm as a vendor) cannot MARKET or PROMOTE the camera for
  neonatal use unless that specific intended use is cleared by the FDA.
- The hospital's purchasing department may want documentation that the camera has
  510(k) clearance, even if the specific NICU use is off-label.
- Insurance/reimbursement for thermographic procedures may be limited (Aetna, for
  example, considers thermography "experimental and investigational" for most uses
  per their clinical policy bulletin).

3. "CLEARED" vs. "APPROVED" — WHAT IS THE DIFFERENCE?
======================================================

These terms have precise legal meanings and are NOT interchangeable.

FDA CLEARED (510(k) Pathway):
- Applies to: Class I and Class II medical devices (low to moderate risk)
- Standard: Device must be "substantially equivalent" to a legally marketed
  predicate device already on the market
- Process: Manufacturer submits a 510(k) premarket notification demonstrating
  substantial equivalence in intended use and technological characteristics
- Evidence required: Bench testing, biocompatibility data, software validation;
  clinical trials are usually NOT required
- Timeline: Typically 3-12 months
- Scope: Accounts for ~80% of all medical devices on the US market
- Example: Thermal cameras (product code LHQ) are Class I devices that require
  510(k) clearance

FDA APPROVED (PMA Pathway):
- Applies to: Class III medical devices (high risk — e.g., implants, life-
  sustaining devices)
- Standard: Must demonstrate "reasonable assurance of safety and effectiveness"
  through rigorous scientific evidence
- Process: Manufacturer submits a Premarket Approval (PMA) application with
  extensive clinical trial data
- Evidence required: Clinical trials (often randomized controlled trials),
  manufacturing data, labeling review
- Timeline: Typically 1-3+ years
- Scope: ~10% of devices; reserved for highest-risk products
- Example: Pacemakers, breast implants, artificial hearts

FDA GRANTED (De Novo Pathway):
- Applies to: Novel devices with no predicate, but low-to-moderate risk
- Standard: "Reasonable assurance of safety and effectiveness" (like PMA) but
  for lower-risk devices
- Process: Manufacturer files a De Novo classification request
- Once granted: The device becomes a predicate for future 510(k) submissions
- Example: Sepsis ImmunoScore (DEN230036, April 2024) — the first AI sepsis
  prediction tool — was authorized via De Novo
- Relevance to NeoTherm: If NeoTherm's AI software has no exact predicate, De
  Novo may be the required pathway, and a granted device would then become a
  predicate for competitors

WHY THIS MATTERS FOR NEOTHERM:
- Thermal cameras are CLEARED (510(k), Class I) — not approved (PMA, Class III).
  This is a much lower regulatory bar.
- NeoTherm's AI software layer would likely need its own clearance or De Novo
  authorization as SaMD. The camera's existing 510(k) does NOT cover the software.

4. CAN SOFTWARE THAT TAKES INPUT FROM AN FDA-CLEARED CAMERA BE SOLD WITHOUT
   ITS OWN FDA CLEARANCE?
==========================================================================

IT DEPENDS on what the software DOES and what CLAIMS it makes.

SCENARIO A: Software that ONLY DISPLAYS thermal data (no analysis, no clinical
claims) — LIKELY DOES NOT NEED ITS OWN 510(k)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------
Under FDA guidance on Medical Device Data Systems (MDDS):
- "Software functions that are solely intended to transfer, store, convert formats,
  or display medical device data and results, including medical images, waveforms,
  signals, or other clinical information, are not devices and are not subject to
  applicable FDA regulatory requirements."
- Key word: "SOLELY." The software must do nothing beyond transfer, store, format-
  convert, or display. No analysis. No alerts. No interpretation.
- If NeoTherm built software that literally just displays the thermal camera's feed
  on a monitor (like a fancy screen), this would fall under MDDS enforcement
  discretion and would NOT need a 510(k).

SCENARIO B: Software that ANALYZES thermal data or makes DIAGNOSTIC CLAIMS —
NEEDS ITS OWN FDA CLEARANCE
-------------------------------------------------------------------------
If the software does ANY of the following, it is likely a regulated medical device
(SaMD — Software as a Medical Device):
- Processes or analyzes thermal images to detect patterns
- Generates a clinical risk score, alert, or recommendation
- Claims to detect, diagnose, or screen for any condition (e.g., sepsis, NEC,
  hypothermia)
- Applies AI/ML algorithms to thermal data for clinical purposes
- Triggers clinical alerts based on thermal analysis

SCENARIO C: Software with SIMPLE ALERTS (e.g., "temperature outside range") —
GRAY AREA, BUT LIKELY NEEDS CLEARANCE
-------------------------------------------------------------------------
- FDA enforcement discretion examples include "software functions that provide
  historical trending and comparison of vital signs (for example, body temperature,
  heart rate, blood pressure, or respiratory rate)" — this suggests basic trending
  MAY fall under enforcement discretion.
- However, if the alert is intended to prompt clinical action (e.g., "investigate
  for sepsis"), it crosses into SaMD territory.

CAUTIONARY TALE — TOTAL THERMAL IMAGING, INC. (FDA Warning Letter, Feb 2019):
- Company bundled FLIR's FDA-cleared thermal camera (K033967) with proprietary
  software into a "Thermography Business Package."
- Marketed the package for breast cancer screening as a standalone diagnostic tool.
- FDA's response: "Clearance of a component of the Thermography Business Package
  does not permit the marketing of the Thermography Business Package."
- The software + camera combination created a new intended use (sole diagnostic
  screening) that was NOT covered by FLIR's existing 510(k).
- FDA ordered immediate cessation of distribution and cited 9 quality system
  violations under 21 CFR Part 820.

KEY REGULATORY PRINCIPLE:
The camera's 510(k) covers the camera. Any software that materially changes the
intended use, adds clinical claims, or creates a new device function requires its
own regulatory authorization.

5. CLINICAL DECISION SUPPORT SOFTWARE — WHEN DOES IT NEED FDA CLEARANCE?
=========================================================================

The 21st Century Cures Act (Section 3060, enacted December 13, 2016) amended
Section 520(o)(1)(E) of the FD&C Act to exclude certain Clinical Decision Support
(CDS) software from the definition of "device" — meaning they do NOT need FDA
clearance.

THE FOUR CRITERIA (ALL MUST BE MET):

To be excluded from the device definition, CDS software must meet ALL four criteria
simultaneously under 21 U.S.C. Section 360j(o):

CRITERION 1: The software is NOT intended to acquire, process, or analyze:
  - A medical image, OR
  - A signal from an in vitro diagnostic device, OR
  - A pattern or signal from a signal acquisition system

CRITERION 2: The software IS intended for the purpose of displaying, analyzing,
or printing medical information about a patient or other medical information
(such as peer-reviewed clinical studies and clinical practice guidelines).

CRITERION 3: The software IS intended for the purpose of supporting or providing
recommendations to a health care professional about prevention, diagnosis, or
treatment of a disease or condition.

CRITERION 4: The software IS intended for the purpose of enabling health care
professionals to independently review the basis for such recommendations so that
it is NOT the intent that such health care professional rely primarily on any of
such recommendations to make a clinical diagnosis or treatment decision regarding
an individual patient without independently reviewing the recommendation.

HOW NEOTHERM'S SOFTWARE MEASURES AGAINST THE FOUR CRITERIA:

Criterion 1: FAILS.
  NeoTherm software would process thermal IMAGES from an infrared camera. The FDA
  interprets "medical image" broadly to include "images generated by medical imaging
  systems...that view any part(s) of the body or images acquired for a medical
  purpose." Thermal images acquired to monitor neonatal temperature ARE medical
  images. The 2026 CDS guidance explicitly preserves Criterion 1 unchanged —
  software that analyzes medical images is NOT eligible for the CDS exemption.

Criterion 2: PASSES.
  The software would display and analyze medical information about a patient.

Criterion 3: DEPENDS on design.
  If the software provides recommendations to an HCP about prevention or diagnosis,
  this criterion is met. The 2026 guidance now extends enforcement discretion to
  software providing a "single output or recommendation" when clinically appropriate
  (previously, single-output software was excluded from the exemption).

Criterion 4: DEPENDS on design.
  If the software is designed so the HCP can review the underlying basis (e.g.,
  transparent algorithms, access to raw thermal data), this criterion could be met.
  However, if the HCP is expected to primarily rely on the software's output (e.g.,
  an automated sepsis alert), this criterion FAILS.

VERDICT: NeoTherm's software CANNOT qualify for the CDS exemption because it
fails Criterion 1 — it processes medical images (thermal images from an infrared
camera). This is a statutory exclusion, not a matter of FDA interpretation.

Even if Criterion 1 were somehow met, the 2026 CDS guidance specifically excludes
from enforcement discretion software that "predict[s] time-critical events" —
which a sepsis early warning system inherently does.

6. FDA GUIDANCE ON CDS EXEMPTIONS — 2022 AND 2026 UPDATES
===========================================================

TIMELINE OF KEY GUIDANCE DOCUMENTS:

December 13, 2016: 21st Century Cures Act signed into law. Section 3060 amends
  the FD&C Act to create statutory exclusions for certain software.

September 28, 2022: FDA issues FINAL guidance on Clinical Decision Support
  Software (superseding 2019 draft guidance). Key decisions:
  - Narrow interpretation of Criterion 3: Software that provides a "specific
    output or directive" rather than "options" or "recommendations" does NOT
    qualify for exemption.
  - Single-output CDS (e.g., "this patient has sepsis risk of 85%") is regulated
    as a device.
  - Software must present multiple options for the HCP to evaluate.

January 6, 2026: FDA issues REVISED final guidance on CDS software. Key changes:

  1. CRITERION 3 EXPANSION: FDA will now exercise enforcement discretion for CDS
     that provides a single recommendation when "clinically appropriate" — reversing
     the 2022 position. Example given: software recommending a specific FDA-approved
     drug based on patient symptoms.

  2. CLINICAL DOCUMENTATION TOOLS: Software that analyzes radiologist findings
     (NOT the underlying images) to generate proposed report summaries may qualify
     under enforcement discretion — provided the tool itself does not analyze the
     underlying images and an HCP remains involved.

  3. CRITERION 1 UNCHANGED: Software that acquires, processes, or analyzes medical
     images or signals remains EXCLUDED from the CDS exemption. No change here.

  4. TIME-CRITICAL PREDICTIONS EXCLUDED: The 2026 guidance specifically excludes
     from enforcement discretion software that "predicts time-critical events"
     (e.g., cardiovascular events within 24 hours). This directly impacts sepsis
     prediction software.

  5. USABILITY EMPHASIS: For Criterion 4, FDA stresses that transparency
     information must be presented in ways that avoid information overload and
     prioritize decision-relevant details.

  6. NOTABLE OMISSIONS: The 2026 guidance says nothing about consumer-facing
     decision support tools (patient chatbots, symptom checkers) or AI-specific
     considerations, despite Commissioner Makary framing these as AI-focused
     guidance.

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR NEOTHERM:
- The CDS exemption is NOT available for NeoTherm's core product (Criterion 1
  failure: processes thermal images; plus time-critical prediction exclusion).
- However, any AUXILIARY software NeoTherm builds that does NOT process images
  and instead works with structured data (e.g., EHR data, lab values, nurse-
  entered observations) MIGHT qualify for CDS exemption if it meets all four
  criteria.

7. CAN YOU BUILD A "WELLNESS" OR "MONITORING" PRODUCT USING DATA FROM AN
   FDA-CLEARED THERMAL CAMERA WITHOUT GETTING YOUR OWN 510(k)?
========================================================================

POTENTIALLY YES — but only if you strictly avoid clinical/diagnostic claims and
meet the general wellness criteria. Here is the framework:

PATHWAY A: GENERAL WELLNESS PRODUCT (Enforcement Discretion)
-------------------------------------------------------------
The FDA's General Wellness Policy (revised January 6, 2026) provides enforcement
discretion for products meeting these requirements:

Requirements to qualify:
1. Intended solely for maintaining or encouraging a healthy lifestyle
2. NOT intended to diagnose, prevent, or treat a disease or condition
3. NOT intended as a substitute for an FDA-authorized device
4. Does NOT include claims, functionality, or outputs that prompt or guide
   specific clinical action
5. Does NOT include values that mimic those used clinically (unless validated)
6. Product must be low-risk (non-invasive, non-implanted)

What the 2026 guidance NEWLY ALLOWS:
- Non-invasive sensing devices (optical sensors) that estimate physiological
  parameters for wellness purposes
- Products that display values, ranges, trends, or longitudinal summaries in
  domains like sleep, activity, and stress
- General notifications when outputs fall outside normal ranges (as long as the
  notification does not prompt specific clinical action)
- Blood pressure estimation wearables for wellness (reversing prior enforcement
  against WHOOP, Inc.)

What is STILL PROHIBITED for wellness products:
- Claims of "clinical accuracy" or "medical grade"
- References to specific diseases or diagnostic thresholds
- Ongoing alerts intended to manage disease
- Prompts directing clinical action or medical management
- Minimally invasive sensors (regardless of wellness-only claims)

COULD NEOTHERM QUALIFY AS A WELLNESS PRODUCT?
Likely NO for the core product. Here's why:
- NeoTherm is intended for use in a NICU — inherently a clinical/medical setting
- Neonates are patients under active medical care, not "wellness" subjects
- Temperature monitoring in the NICU is inherently for medical management, not
  "maintaining a healthy lifestyle"
- Any sepsis detection or early warning function is diagnosing/screening for a
  disease, which disqualifies the product from wellness status

PATHWAY B: MDDS (DISPLAY-ONLY SOFTWARE) — Enforcement Discretion
------------------------------------------------------------------
If NeoTherm's software ONLY transfers, stores, converts formats, or displays the
thermal camera's output — with NO analysis, NO alerts, NO interpretation — it may
qualify under the MDDS enforcement discretion policy:

- "Software functions that are solely intended to transfer, store, convert formats,
  or display medical device data and results, including medical images...are not
  devices."
- The software must NOT modify the data or control any connected device.
- The software must NOT add any clinical interpretation layer.

A pure display product might be viable as a first version while pursuing FDA
clearance for the AI layer, but it would have very limited clinical value and
could not make any marketing claims about improving patient outcomes.

PATHWAY C: ENFORCEMENT DISCRETION FOR LOW-RISK SOFTWARE
--------------------------------------------------------
The FDA maintains a list of software functions for which it exercises enforcement
discretion. Relevant examples:
- "Software functions that provide historical trending and comparison of vital
  signs (for example, body temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, or respiratory
  rate)" — THIS COULD APPLY to basic thermal trending.
- "Software functions that aggregate and display trends in personal health
  incidents"

However, the enforcement discretion examples are illustrative, not exhaustive, and
FDA reserves the right to change its approach. Building a business on enforcement
discretion alone is risky.

RECOMMENDED STRATEGY FOR NEOTHERM:
===================================

PHASE 1 (No FDA clearance needed):
- Build software that DISPLAYS thermal camera feed in the NICU
- Provide basic data storage and retrieval
- Enable clinicians to view historical thermal images
- Market as a "thermal visualization platform" with NO clinical claims
- Regulatory basis: MDDS enforcement discretion + physician off-label use of the
  cleared camera hardware

PHASE 2 (Gray area — enforcement discretion):
- Add basic vital sign trending (temperature over time)
- Show trend lines and historical comparisons
- NO automated alerts, NO risk scores, NO diagnostic claims
- Regulatory basis: FDA enforcement discretion for vital sign trending software

PHASE 3 (Requires FDA clearance/authorization):
- Add AI analysis, sepsis risk scoring, automated alerts
- Make clinical claims about early detection
- Regulatory basis: 510(k) or De Novo authorization as SaMD
- Predicates: Sepsis ImmunoScore (DEN230036), NAVOY CDS (510(k))
- Start Pre-Sub (Q-Sub) meeting process with FDA during Phase 1

This phased approach lets NeoTherm get to market faster with Phase 1 while
pursuing the FDA pathway needed for Phase 3's full clinical product.
