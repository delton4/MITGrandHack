FDA 510(k)-CLEARED THERMAL/INFRARED IMAGING SYSTEMS
Product Code: LHQ - System, Telethermographic (Adjunctive Use)
21 CFR 884.2980
Research compiled: 2026-03-14
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
Product code LHQ covers telethermographic systems for adjunctive use. These are
Class I medical devices (exempt from PMA but requiring 510(k) clearance). The
standard intended use across virtually all cleared devices is:

  "For use as an adjunct to other clinical diagnostic procedures in the
   diagnosis, quantifying, and screening of differences in skin surface
   temperature changes."

Key constraint: NO thermal camera has FDA clearance to be used as a SOLE
diagnostic or screening device. All are cleared for ADJUNCTIVE use only.

There are approximately 37 total 510(k) entries under product code LHQ in the
FDA database.

FDA ENFORCEMENT ACTIONS & WARNINGS
-----------------------------------
1. Total Thermal Imaging, Inc. - Warning Letter (02/22/2019)
   - Illegally marketed the "Thermography Business Package" (included a FLIR
     camera + proprietary software) as a SOLE screening tool for breast cancer
     and other diseases.
   - Claims included: early detection of breast cancer, inflammatory breast
     cancer, pre-stroke, heart disease, deep vein thrombosis.
   - FDA ordered immediate cessation of distribution.
   - Key lesson: You CANNOT market a thermal camera as a standalone diagnostic
     tool, even if the camera itself has 510(k) clearance. The clearance is for
     ADJUNCTIVE use only.

2. Meditherm, Inc. - FDA Warning Letter (03/22/2011)
   - Received a warning letter regarding product claims.

3. FLIR System AB - FDA Warning Letter (01/2011)
   - Received a warning letter regarding product claims.

4. FDA Safety Communication (2019):
   - "Thermography is not cleared by the FDA as an alternative to mammography
     and should not replace mammography for breast cancer screening or diagnosis."
   - "There is no valid scientific data to show that thermographic devices, when
     used on their own or with another diagnostic test, are an effective
     screening tool for any medical condition."

OFF-LABEL USE OF FDA-CLEARED MEDICAL DEVICES
---------------------------------------------

KEY LEGAL PRINCIPLES:

1. The FDA does NOT regulate the practice of medicine.
   - Once a device is legally marketed (i.e., has 510(k) clearance), a
     physician can use it for ANY purpose they believe is clinically warranted.
   - This is the "practice of medicine" doctrine.

2. Physician off-label use is LEGAL and COMMON.
   - "Once an approved device or drug is available, doctors can prescribe it for
     any purpose they believe is warranted -- regardless of what the label says."
   - "Sometimes it is the medical standard of care to prescribe a medical
     product for uses other than what is stated on the label."
   - Requirements: Must be based on "firm scientific rationale and sound medical
     evidence." Physician should maintain records of the product's use and effects.

3. NICU-specific context:
   - "Most medical and surgical devices currently used for neonatal patients
     were NOT approved or cleared by the FDA for use in pediatric or neonatal
     populations."
   - Physicians routinely use devices off-label in NICUs when no neonatal-
     specific device exists.
   - This is explicitly acknowledged by the FDA in their pediatric/neonatal
     device guidance.

4. Manufacturer vs. Physician distinction:
   - MANUFACTURERS cannot promote off-label uses. They can only market the
     device for its cleared intended use.
   - PHYSICIANS are free to use cleared devices off-label at their discretion.
   - A physician CAN use an FDA-cleared thermal camera in a NICU for neonatal
     temperature monitoring, even though the camera is not specifically cleared
     for that use.

5. Hospital/IRB considerations:
   - Hospitals may have their own policies about off-label device use.
   - If used in research, IRB approval may be needed.
   - For clinical care (not research), physician discretion applies.

BOTTOM LINE FOR NEOTHERM:
- A physician CAN legally use any FDA-cleared thermal camera (product code LHQ)
  in a NICU setting for neonatal temperature monitoring under off-label use.
- NeoTherm as a SOFTWARE PLATFORM cannot itself market or promote the thermal
  camera for neonatal use unless NeoTherm obtains its own 510(k) clearance for
  that specific intended use.
- Strategy: Use an already-cleared thermal camera as the hardware component.
  The software platform (NeoTherm) would need its own regulatory pathway if it
  makes diagnostic claims.

SUITABILITY FOR SOFTWARE PLATFORM INTEGRATION
----------------------------------------------

CAMERAS MOST SUITABLE FOR INTEGRATION INTO A SOFTWARE PLATFORM:

1. FLIR/Teledyne FLIR (A-Series, E-Series, etc.) -- K033967
   BEST FOR SOFTWARE INTEGRATION
   - FLIR Atlas SDK: Supports communication, streaming, and recording via
     GigE, RTSP, and USB. Full access to radiometric data for individual pixels.
   - FLIR Science Camera SDK: Camera control, data streaming, acquisition via
     GigE and Camera Link. Supports C/C++ and C#/.NET.
   - FLIR BHP SDK: For A6600/A6200sc/A6700sc/A8300sc series. C/C++ functions
     for camera control and GigE data streaming.
   - FLIR Mobile SDK: For mobile integration.
   - REST API available for automation cameras.
   - MOST MATURE SDK ECOSYSTEM for thermal cameras.
   - Already FDA-cleared (K033967) covering Series A, E, S, P.

2. ICI P-Series (Infrared Cameras Inc.) -- K101641
   GOOD FOR INTEGRATION
   - FDA-cleared medical-specific thermal cameras.
   - Multiple form factors (handheld, fixed mount, medical cart).
   - Company focused on medical/industrial applications.
   - SDK availability less documented publicly than FLIR.

3. Meditherm IRIS Series -- K003332
   MODERATE FOR INTEGRATION
   - Uses proprietary WinTES software.
   - Purpose-built for medical thermography.
   - Calibrated to 0.01C accuracy.
   - 307,000+ temperature measurements per image.
   - Less open SDK/API ecosystem; more closed/proprietary.

4. Thermidas ThIR-A615 -- K200999
   POTENTIALLY GOOD FOR INTEGRATION
   - Finnish company, newer entrant.
   - FDA-cleared 2020.
   - Two products in FDA device listing.
   - Integration capabilities not well-documented in public sources.

5. SMILE-100 (Niramai) -- K212965
   INTERESTING BUT NICHE
   - Already has AI-based image quality algorithms built in.
   - Breast-specific focus.
   - May have proprietary integration approach.

6. ThermPix (USA Therm) -- K232462
   NEWEST DEVICE
   - Most recently cleared (2023).
   - Portable, handheld, point-of-care.
   - Software/integration capabilities not well-documented publicly.

RECOMMENDATION FOR NEOTHERM:
The FLIR/Teledyne FLIR platform (cleared under K033967) offers the best
combination of:
  (a) FDA clearance (since 2004, well-established)
  (b) Software SDK ecosystem (Atlas SDK, Science Camera SDK, REST API)
  (c) Multiple camera form factors suitable for NICU mounting
  (d) Radiometric data access at the pixel level
  (e) Industry standard interfaces (GigE, USB, Camera Link)
  (f) Largest installed base and community support

Second choice: ICI P-Series cameras, which are specifically marketed as medical
devices with FDA clearance and offer medical cart configurations.

REGULATORY PATHWAY FOR NEOTHERM SOFTWARE
-----------------------------------------
If NeoTherm is purely SOFTWARE that processes images from an already-cleared
camera:
- The software itself may need 510(k) clearance as a "Software as a Medical
  Device" (SaMD) if it makes diagnostic or clinical claims.
- If the software merely displays thermal images without making clinical
  interpretations, the regulatory burden may be lower.
- FDA's 2023 guidance uses a risk-based approach: Basic Documentation (minimal
  risk) vs. Enhanced Documentation (potential serious injury/death).
- The camera hardware would remain covered under its existing 510(k).
- The software + camera combination may need a new 510(k) if the combination
  creates a new intended use.
