1. SYSTEMATIC REVIEWS & SCOPING REVIEWS
================================================================================

1a. Topalidou A, Ali N, Sekulic S, Downe S.
    "Thermal imaging applications in neonatal care: a scoping review"
    Year: 2019
    Journal: BMC Pregnancy and Childbirth, 19, 388
    Key Findings: Searched EMBASE, MEDLINE, and MIDIRS; identified 21 studies
    from 442 hits. Five broad themes emerged: general thermal physiology; heat
    loss and respiratory monitoring; identification of internal pathologies
    (including NEC); other uses; and technical concerns. Most studies had small
    cohorts (4-29 participants). Despite promising results since the 1970s, no
    large prospective studies exist. The authors concluded that thermal imaging
    is reliable and non-invasive for continuous neonatal temperature monitoring
    with potential for diagnosing pathologies including internal abnormalities.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1186/s12884-019-2533-y
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6814124/

1b. Defined Authors (multiple).
    "Uses of infrared thermography in acute illness: a systematic review"
    Year: 2024
    Journal: Frontiers in Medicine, 11, 1412854
    Key Findings: Included 30 studies conducted in emergency departments and
    intensive care units involving adult, paediatric, and neonatal patients.
    IRT shows promise for early identification of acute illness including
    sepsis. One neonatal ICU study explored whether peripheral vasodilatation
    promoted sleep onset in preterm neonates. The review highlights the growing
    but still limited evidence base for IRT in acute care settings.
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11232369/

1c. Knobel RB, Guenther BD, Rice HE.
    "Thermoregulation and thermography in neonatal physiology and disease"
    Year: 2011
    Journal: Biological Research for Nursing, 13(3), 274-282
    Key Findings: Foundational narrative review covering the use of infrared
    thermography in neonatal physiology and disease. Discusses thermal imaging
    as a non-invasive, non-contact method for monitoring neonatal temperature.
    Describes applications including NEC detection and thermoregulation
    assessment. Established the theoretical framework for subsequent NIRT
    research.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/1099800411403467
    Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21586499/

1d. Rakholia MV et al.
    "Infrared thermography in paediatrics: a narrative review of clinical use"
    Year: 2018
    Journal: BMC Pediatrics (PMC5862192)
    Key Findings: Reviewed clinical uses of IRT in pediatric populations
    including neonates. Covers applications in temperature monitoring,
    vascular assessment, and inflammation detection. Notes that IRT is
    non-invasive, radiation-free, and particularly suited for vulnerable
    pediatric populations.
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5862192/

2. THERMAL IMAGING FOR JAUNDICE / BILIRUBIN MONITORING
================================================================================

NOTE: Direct infrared thermography for bilirubin detection is not well-
established in the literature. Most non-invasive bilirubin research uses
optical/colorimetric methods rather than thermal imaging. However, the
following adjacent studies are relevant:

2a. Chung HU et al.
    "Neonatal wearable device for colorimetry-based real-time detection of
    jaundice with simultaneous sensing of vitals"
    Year: 2021
    Journal: Science Advances, 7(10), eabe3793
    Key Findings: Developed a wearable transcutaneous bilirubinometer with
    SpO2 and HR sensing. Bilirubin levels measured by absorbance differences
    of blue (460 nm) and green (570 nm) lights, while SpO2/HR use red (640 nm)
    and infrared (950 nm) LED lights. Clinical experiments on neonates
    demonstrated feasibility of simultaneous bilirubin, SpO2, and HR
    detection. This demonstrates the integration of IR sensing into
    multi-parameter neonatal monitoring.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.abe3793
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7929506/

2b. Authors not specified in search.
    "Artificial Intelligence non-invasive methods for neonatal jaundice
    detection: A review"
    Year: 2025
    Journal: Artificial Intelligence in Medicine (ScienceDirect)
    Key Findings: AI models incorporating neural networks achieve >90%
    accuracy in detecting jaundice compared to traditional methods.
    Integration of RGB and HSV color spaces yields strong performance
    (RMSE 1.13, R2 0.91). Model achieved 96.87% accuracy in classifying
    jaundice status into three categories. While primarily optical rather
    than thermal, demonstrates AI potential for non-invasive neonatal
    diagnostics.
    Link: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0933365725000235

2c. Kailavasan M et al.
    "Artificial intelligence-based non-invasive bilirubin prediction for
    neonatal jaundice using 1D convolutional neural network"
    Year: 2025
    Journal: Scientific Reports (Nature)
    Key Findings: Used 1D convolutional neural networks for non-invasive
    bilirubin prediction. Demonstrates the growing trend of AI-based
    approaches for neonatal jaundice assessment that could potentially
    be combined with thermal imaging modalities.
    Link: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-96100-9

3. BRAIN INJURY / HIE DETECTION VIA THERMAL MEASUREMENTS
================================================================================

3a. Walas W, Halaba ZP, Szczapa T, Rudzki L et al.
    "Thermal Index for early non-invasive assessment of brain injury in
    newborns treated with therapeutic hypothermia: preliminary report"
    Year: 2021
    Journal: Scientific Reports, 11, 12079
    Key Findings: Developed and evaluated a new "Thermal Index" (TI) to
    assess the degree of brain damage in newborns undergoing therapeutic
    hypothermia for perinatal asphyxia. The TI is based on non-invasive
    thermal measurements. Analysis applied to six newborns treated with TH
    in a NICU in 2018. Because perinatal asphyxia causes dysfunction of
    the thermoregulation center, assessing endogenous heat production
    non-invasively may serve as a biomarker for brain injury severity.
    Results were encouraging as a preliminary report.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-021-92139-6
    Link: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-92139-6

3b. Walas W, Halaba ZP, Szczapa T et al.
    "Theoretical basis for the use of non-invasive thermal measurements to
    assess the brain injury in newborns undergoing therapeutic hypothermia"
    Year: 2020
    Journal: Scientific Reports, 10, 22167
    Key Findings: Proposed a new non-invasive methodology to estimate
    thermogenesis (metabolic heat production) in newborns with perinatal
    asphyxia undergoing therapeutic hypothermia. The method calculates
    metabolic heat production from the newborn's heat balance using only
    non-invasive thermal measurements. This paper provides the theoretical
    framework for the Thermal Index approach. Key insight: since HIE
    disrupts the thermoregulation center, thermal measurements can
    indirectly reflect brain injury severity.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-020-79009-3
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7747633/

3c. Cizmeci MN et al.
    "Relationship between temperature variability and brain injury on
    magnetic resonance imaging in cooled newborn infants after perinatal
    asphyxia"
    Year: 2017
    Journal: Journal of Perinatology
    Key Findings: Temperature variability and percentage of temperatures
    within the target range were associated with severity of brain injury
    on MRI in cooled newborn infants after perinatal asphyxia. Suggests
    that temperature monitoring patterns during therapeutic hypothermia
    can predict neurological outcomes.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1038/jp.2017.96
    Link: https://www.nature.com/articles/jp201796

3d. Cowan D et al.
    "Brain temperature monitoring in newborn infants: Current methodologies
    and prospects"
    Year: 2022
    Journal: Frontiers in Pediatrics, 10, 1008539
    Key Findings: Comprehensive review of brain temperature monitoring
    methods in newborns. Discusses MRS-based brain temperature measurement
    (accuracy up to 0.2 C, precision 0.3 C). Notes that MRI cannot be
    performed in ICU during therapeutic hypothermia, highlighting the need
    for bedside non-invasive approaches like infrared thermography.
    Link: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pediatrics/articles/10.3389/fped.2022.1008539/full

4. SEIZURE DETECTION USING CAMERAS IN NEONATES
================================================================================

NOTE: Thermal camera-based seizure detection in neonates is an extremely
nascent field. Most neonatal seizure detection research uses EEG or video
(RGB) cameras rather than thermal imaging. Key adjacent work:

4a. Cattani L et al.
    "Maximum-likelihood detection of neonatal clonic seizures by video
    image processing"
    Year: 2014
    Journal: Conference proceedings (referenced in multiple reviews)
    Key Findings: Used a multi-camera setup and maximum-likelihood
    detection for seizure prediction in neonatal patients. Focused on
    detecting characteristic movement patterns of clonic seizures using
    video image processing. While not specifically thermal imaging,
    established the framework for camera-based (including thermal)
    neonatal seizure detection.

4b. Cattani L et al.
    "Monitoring infants by automatic video processing: A unified approach
    to motion analysis"
    Year: 2017
    Journal: Computers in Biology and Medicine, 80, 158-165
    Key Findings: Presented a unified approach to contact-less, low-cost
    video processing for automatic detection of neonatal diseases
    characterized by specific movement patterns, including clonic seizures
    and apneas. The periodicity analysis of luminance signals could
    potentially be extended to thermal video streams.
    Link: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0010482516303031

4c. Neonatal Seizure Detection Using a Wearable Multi-Sensor System
    Year: 2023
    Journal: PMC (PMC10294985)
    Key Findings: Explored wearable multi-sensor systems (including
    accelerometers and physiological sensors) for neonatal seizure
    detection. While not specifically thermal, demonstrates the trend
    toward multi-modal monitoring that could incorporate thermal data.
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10294985/

5. KANGAROO CARE / SKIN-TO-SKIN CONTACT MONITORING
================================================================================

5a. Abbas AK, Heimann K, Blazek V, Orlikowsky T, Leonhardt S.
    "Neonatal infrared thermography imaging: Analysis of heat flux during
    different clinical scenarios"
    Year: 2012
    Journal: Infrared Physics & Technology, 55, 538-548
    Key Findings: Studied 12 preterm babies across different clinical
    scenarios including kangaroo mother care (KMC), convective incubators,
    and open radiant warmers. First IR thermography application to KMC
    was performed at RWTH Aachen University Hospital (August 2009). Baby
    covered with IR-transparent polyethylene foil during KMC to allow IR
    camera viewing. Concluded that IR thermography can be used as a
    standard temperature monitoring technique across different neonatal
    care scenarios. Developed compensation techniques for each scenario.
    Link: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S135044951200059X

5b. Abbas AK, Heimann K, Jergus K, Orlikowsky T, Leonhardt S.
    "Intelligent neonatal monitoring based on a virtual thermal sensor"
    Year: 2014
    Journal: BMC Medical Imaging, 14, 9
    Key Findings: Proposed a "virtual thermal sensor" concept for
    intelligent neonatal monitoring. The system uses infrared thermography
    to create a virtual representation of the neonate's thermal state,
    enabling continuous and non-contact monitoring. Applicable to multiple
    clinical scenarios including incubators and KMC. Infrared digital
    cameras are precise and can measure neonates' body temperature rapidly
    without physical contact, consistent with the minimal stimulation
    protocol.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1186/1471-2342-14-9
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4015601/

5c. Ludington-Hoe SM, Lewis T, Morgan K, Cong X, Anderson L, Reese S.
    "Breast and infant temperatures with twins during shared kangaroo care"
    Year: 2006
    Journal: JOGNN, 35(2), 223-231
    Key Findings: Examined thermal dynamics between mother and twin infants
    during shared kangaroo care. Maternal breast temperature met each
    infant's neutral thermal zone within 5 minutes of KC onset. This
    foundational study demonstrated the thermal synchrony between mother
    and infant during skin-to-skin contact.
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1890034/

6. MACHINE LEARNING / AI + NEONATAL THERMAL IMAGING
================================================================================

6a. Ornek AH, Ceylan M, Ervural S.
    "Health status detection of neonates using infrared thermography and
    deep convolutional neural networks"
    Year: 2019 (published 2020)
    Journal: Infrared Physics & Technology, 103, 103044
    Key Findings: FIRST study to combine IRT with deep CNNs for neonatal
    health status detection. Thermal images from 19 healthy and 19 unhealthy
    neonates captured at Selcuk University NICU over one year. Achieved
    99.58% accuracy, 99.73% specificity, 99.43% sensitivity, and 0.996 AUC
    for 30,400 thermal images. Demonstrated feasibility of automated
    health classification from thermal data alone.
    Link: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1350449519303123

6b. Ornek AH, Ceylan M.
    "A new approach for super-resolution and classification applications
    on neonatal thermal images" (TSRGAN+ model)
    Year: 2023
    Journal: Quantitative InfraRed Thermography Journal, 21(3)
    Key Findings: Developed TSRGAN+ deep network for super-resolution of
    neonatal thermal images. Used 250 premature (healthy) and 250 unhealthy
    baby images from a Variocam HD thermal camera, downscaled at 1/2, 1/4,
    1/8 ratios. Trained CNN achieved 99.91% accuracy (train), 99.47%
    (validation), 99.82% (test). Demonstrates that even low-resolution
    thermal images can be enhanced for reliable classification.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/17686733.2023.2179282
    Link: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/17686733.2023.2179282

6c. Ornek AH, Ceylan M.
    "CodCAM: A new ensemble visual explanation for classification of
    medical thermal images"
    Year: 2023
    Journal: Quantitative InfraRed Thermography Journal, 21(3)
    Key Findings: Developed CodCAM, a new ensemble Explainable AI (XAI)
    method combining GradCAM, GradCAM++, LayerCAM, and EigenCAM to
    visualize which regions of neonatal thermograms affect CNN classification
    decisions. Allows medical specialists to understand which thermal
    patterns indicate healthy vs. unhealthy status. Achieved 99.91%
    accuracy. Important for clinical trust and adoption.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/17686733.2023.2167459
    Link: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/17686733.2023.2167459

6d. Ornek AH, Ceylan M.
    "Convolutional Neural Networks-Based Approach to Detect Neonatal
    Respiratory System Anomalies with Limited Thermal Image"
    Year: 2021
    Journal: Traitement du Signal, 38(2)
    Key Findings: Applied CNNs to detect respiratory system anomalies in
    neonates using limited thermal image data. Addressed the common
    challenge of small datasets in medical imaging through data
    augmentation techniques. Demonstrated that CNNs can detect respiratory
    anomalies from thermal images even with limited training data.
    Link: https://www.iieta.org/journals/ts/paper/10.18280/ts.380222

6e. Lyra S, Mayer L, Ou L, Chen D et al.
    "Camera fusion for real-time temperature monitoring of neonates using
    deep learning"
    Year: 2022
    Journal: Medical & Biological Engineering & Computing, 60, 1789-1800
    Key Findings: Fused thermal, RGB, and 3D TOF cameras for enhanced
    neonatal facial detection and reliable HR, RR, and body temperature
    measurements using dual neural networks. Deep learning-based infant
    body landmark detection automates identification of core and peripheral
    sites. Clinical trial with 40 preterm infants in NICU validated
    feasibility. This multi-modal approach represents the state of the
    art in camera-based neonatal monitoring.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/s11517-022-02561-9
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9079037/

6f. Schwarz CE et al.
    "A Setup for Camera-Based Detection of Simulated Pathological States
    Using a Neonatal Phantom"
    Year: 2022
    Journal: Sensors (PMC8838518)
    Key Findings: Developed a neonatal phantom setup for testing camera-
    based detection of simulated pathological states. Uses thermal and
    visible-light cameras. Important methodological contribution for
    validating thermal imaging algorithms before clinical deployment.
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8838518/

6g. Park H et al.
    "The past, current, and future of neonatal intensive care units with
    artificial intelligence: a systematic review"
    Year: 2023
    Journal: npj Digital Medicine, 6, 217
    Key Findings: Systematic review of AI applications in NICUs including
    thermal imaging-based approaches. Covers the integration of multiple
    sensing modalities (thermal, RGB, depth) with deep learning for
    contactless vital sign monitoring. Identifies thermal imaging as a
    key enabling technology for next-generation NICU monitoring.
    Link: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-023-00941-5

7. ABDOMINAL THERMOGRAPHY FOR NEC / FEEDING / GUT ASSESSMENT
================================================================================

7a. Rice HE, Hollingsworth CL, Bradsher E, Danko ME, Crosby SM,
    Goldberg RN, Tanaka DT, Knobel RB.
    "Infrared thermal imaging (thermography) of the abdomen in extremely
    low birthweight infants"
    Year: 2010
    Journal: Journal of Surgical Radiology, 1(4)
    Key Findings: Assessed feasibility of infrared thermal imaging for
    abdominal skin temperature measurement in ELBW infants and explored
    association with NEC. Overall, mean abdominal skin temperature was
    lower than thoracic skin temperature. Infants with radiographic NEC
    had significantly lower mean abdominal skin temperature (35.3 +/- 0.8 C)
    compared to those without NEC (36.6 +/- 0.9 C). Foundational study
    demonstrating thermal signature differences in NEC.
    Link: https://scholars.duke.edu/publication/723960

7b. Schaefer G, Ornek AH, Ceylan M, Abdulhamit S.
    "Assessment of Abdominal Skin Temperature Change in Premature Newborns
    with NEC Compared to Healthy Controls"
    Year: 2011 (book chapter)
    Journal: Springer (978-3-642-23508-5)
    Key Findings: Compared abdominal skin temperature patterns in premature
    newborns with NEC versus healthy controls. Found that NEC cases show
    characteristic abdominal temperature changes detectable by infrared
    thermography. The regression analysis indicated a slower decrease of
    superficial abdominal temperature in the NEC group, attributed to
    increased heat from inflamed bowel.
    Link: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-642-23508-5_51

7c. Payeur P et al.
    "Thermal and RGB-D Imaging for Necrotizing Enterocolitis Detection"
    Year: 2020
    Journal: IEEE International Symposium on Medical Measurements and
    Applications (MeMeA)
    Key Findings: Proposed a novel automated system combining an infrared
    thermal camera (FLIR Thermovision A320) with an RGB-D sensor
    (Microsoft Kinect Xbox One) for NEC detection. Color and depth
    information automates and refines abdominal segmentation compared to
    standalone IR image processing. Multi-modal approach improves
    uniformity of thermal data extraction for reliable comparative
    temperature analysis. Represents a significant advance toward
    automated NEC screening.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1109/MeMeA49120.2020.9137344
    Link: https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9137344/

7d. Infrared Imaging Tools for NEC Diagnosis Guided by RGB-D Sensing
    (CHEO Research Institute / University of Ottawa)
    Year: Ongoing research program
    Key Findings: Active research program at Children's Hospital of Eastern
    Ontario (CHEO) developing infrared imaging tools for NEC diagnosis
    guided by RGB-D sensing. Building on the 2020 IEEE work to create
    clinically deployable systems for early NEC detection.
    Link: https://www.cheoresearch.ca/research/projects/infrared-imaging-tools-for-necrotizing-enterocolitis-nec-diagnosis-guided-by-rgb-d-sensing/

7e. Herry CL et al.
    "Detection of Necrotizing Enterocolitis in newborns using abdominal
    thermal signature analysis"
    Year: 2015
    Journal: IEEE Conference Publication
    Key Findings: Explored abdominal thermal signature analysis for NEC
    detection. Examined temperature differentials between chest and abdomen
    as abnormal perfusion may be critical both in development of NEC and
    for its diagnosis. IRT technology may decrease medical costs and
    length of stay by enabling early surveillance of skin temperature
    differentials associated with NEC.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1109/MeMeA.2015.7145168
    Link: https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/7145168/

7f. Infrared Thermal Imaging to Detect Inflammatory Intra-Abdominal
    Conditions (likely NEC-related)
    Year: 2020
    Journal: WSEAS Transactions on Biology and Biomedicine
    Key Findings: Investigated infrared thermal imaging for detecting
    inflammatory intra-abdominal conditions. Relevant to both NEC and
    feeding intolerance assessment in neonates.
    Link: https://wseas.com/journals/bab/2020/a225108-078.pdf

NOTE ON FEEDING INTOLERANCE: Direct studies on thermal imaging for feeding
intolerance assessment were not found in the literature. However, the NEC
detection research (which involves gut inflammation and perfusion changes)
provides the closest available evidence. Distinguishing benign feeding
intolerance from early NEC remains a clinical challenge where thermal
imaging could potentially contribute.

8. RESPIRATORY MONITORING & APNEA DETECTION
================================================================================

8a. Abbas AK, Heimann K, Jergus K, Orlikowsky T, Leonhardt S.
    "Neonatal non-contact respiratory monitoring based on real-time
    infrared thermography"
    Year: 2011
    Journal: BioMedical Engineering OnLine, 10, 93
    Key Findings: Pioneering study on non-contact respiratory monitoring
    in neonates using real-time infrared thermography. Demonstrated that
    small temperature variations around the nose during inspiratory and
    expiratory phases can be used to estimate respiratory flow. Enabled
    differentiation between types of apnea and monitoring under various
    clinical conditions. Established the field of thermal respiratory
    monitoring in neonates.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1186/1475-925X-10-93
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3258209/

8b. Maurya L, Zwiggelaar R, Chawla D et al.
    "Non-contact respiratory rate monitoring using thermal and visible
    imaging: a pilot study on neonates"
    Year: 2023
    Journal: Journal of Clinical Monitoring and Computing, 37, 815-828
    Key Findings: Integrated visible (RGB) and thermal (T) image sequences
    for ROI selection and tracking for breathing rate extraction. Used
    deep learning-based tracking-by-detection approach. Average error for
    neonates was 1.5 bpm. Bland-Altman analysis showed good agreement
    with reference method. Provides evidence for contactless respiratory
    rate detection in clinical settings.
    DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/s10877-022-00945-8
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10175339/

8c. Heimann K et al.
    "Infrared thermography for detailed registration of thermoregulation
    in premature infants"
    Year: 2013
    Journal: Journal of Perinatal Medicine, 41(5), 613-620
    Key Findings: Evaluated skin temperature using non-contact IRT at
    multiple body areas of preterm infants to provide detailed information
    about temperature regulation and distribution. Measurements across
    different clinical scenarios including incubator phases and
    skin-to-skin care sessions. Established methodology for detailed
    thermographic assessment in premature infants.
    Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23443261/

8d. Pereira CB, Czaplik M, Blanik N, Rossaint R, Blazek V, Leonhardt S.
    "Multi-camera infrared thermography for infant respiration monitoring"
    Year: 2020
    Journal: BioMedical Engineering OnLine (PMC7510882)
    Key Findings: Used multiple infrared cameras for infant respiration
    monitoring. Multi-camera approach addresses challenges of single-camera
    setups (occlusion, pose changes). Thermal imaging detects convective
    heat transfer changes at the infra-nasal region. Can differentiate
    between different types of apnea: since apnea is complete cessation
    of airflow for >=10 seconds, thermal cessation of nasal temperature
    oscillation provides direct evidence of apneic events.
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7510882/

9. CORE-PERIPHERAL TEMPERATURE GRADIENT & SEPSIS DETECTION
================================================================================

9a. Authors (2025 study).
    "Thermal imaging-based core peripheral temperature difference
    measurement for neonatal monitoring in the NICU"
    Year: 2025
    Journal: PMC (PMC11919349)
    Key Findings: Developed a thermal infrared (TIR)-based approach for
    non-contact, fully automatic, and continuous core-peripheral
    temperature difference (CPTD) measurement. Deep learning-based infant
    body landmark detection automates identification of core and peripheral
    sites. Validated in a clinical trial with critically ill and preterm
    infants (gestational age 29-40 weeks). CPTD serves as a key biomarker
    for hemodynamic status and early warning of shock and severe infection.
    Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11919349/

9b. Ussat M et al.
    "Central-peripheral temperature gradient: An early diagnostic sign of
    late-onset neonatal sepsis in very low birth weight infants"
    Year: 2012 (estimated)
    Journal: (Referenced in multiple reviews)
    Key Findings: In preterm infants, thermal gradient alteration was
    present in 12 cases, associated with sepsis onset in 10 cases.
    Sensitivity of 90.9% and specificity of 90% for sepsis detection.
    Demonstrates that core-peripheral temperature gradient is a powerful
    early diagnostic marker for neonatal sepsis.
    Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22945277/

10. CONTINUOUS THERMAL MONITORING & THERMOREGULATION
================================================================================

10a. Morimoto A, Nakamura S, Koyano K et al.
     "Continuous monitoring using thermography can capture the heat
     oscillations maintaining body temperature in neonates"
     Year: 2024
     Journal: Scientific Reports, 14, 10564
     Key Findings: Used IRT to measure skin temperature every 15 seconds
     in neonates in incubators. Analyzed thermal data from 27 neonates
     (32 measurements). Extracted two frequency components (A and B) using
     the Savitzky-Golay method. Demonstrated that continuous thermographic
     monitoring can capture heat oscillations involved in thermoregulation,
     providing insights into spontaneous thermal metabolism in neonates.
     DOI: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-60718-y
     Link: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-60718-y

10b. Bhatia M et al.
     "Infrared Thermography with High Accuracy in a Neonatal Incubator"
     Year: 2022
     Journal: Annals of Biomedical Engineering (PMC8890465)
     Key Findings: Developed a calibration method providing high accuracy
     for both point-in-time and continuous thermal monitoring in neonatal
     incubators. Addresses the key technical challenge that incubator
     environments (controlled temperature, humidity) affect IRT accuracy.
     The approach avoids skin contact (no thermistor attachment),
     eliminating risk of skin damage in fragile neonates.
     Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8890465/

10c. Knobel-Dail RB et al.
     "Body Temperature in Premature Infants During the First Week of Life:
     Exploration Using Infrared Thermal Imaging"
     Year: 2017
     Journal: Journal of Thermal Biology (PMC5657603)
     Key Findings: Studied body temperature patterns in premature infants
     during the first week using IRT. Provided detailed thermal mapping
     of preterm infant thermoregulation during the critical early postnatal
     period. Contributed to understanding of heat loss patterns and
     thermal challenges faced by premature infants.
     Link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5657603/

Emerging innovations in neonatal monitoring: a comprehensive review of
progress and potential for non-contact technologies.
Year: 2024
Journal: Frontiers in Pediatrics, 12, 1442753
Key Findings: Comprehensive review covering non-contact monitoring
technologies for neonates including thermal imaging, RGB cameras, and
sensor fusion approaches. Reviews the integration of deep learning with
camera systems for vital sign extraction. Notes that despite significant
research progress, integration into standard NICU clinical practice
remains limited. Identifies thermal imaging as a key enabler for
cable-free NICU monitoring.
Link: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pediatrics/articles/10.3389/fped.2024.1442753/full

================================================================================
SUMMARY OF GAPS AND OPPORTUNITIES
================================================================================

1. JAUNDICE/BILIRUBIN: No direct thermal imaging studies exist. This
   represents a significant research gap. Bilirubin detection currently
   relies on optical/colorimetric methods, but thermal correlates of
   hyperbilirubinemia have not been systematically explored.

2. HIE/BRAIN INJURY: The Thermal Index approach (Walas et al., 2020-2021)
   is promising but based on only 6 patients. Larger validation studies
   are critically needed. Non-invasive bedside brain injury assessment
   during therapeutic hypothermia remains an unmet clinical need.

3. SEIZURE DETECTION: Thermal cameras for seizure detection in neonates
   is essentially unexplored. Video-based (RGB) seizure detection exists,
   and thermal cameras could add physiological data (skin temperature
   changes during seizure activity) that RGB cannot capture.

4. KANGAROO CARE: IR thermography has been applied to KMC (Abbas 2012)
   but only in small studies. Opportunity for larger studies on thermal
   dynamics during KMC and development of continuous monitoring systems.

5. AI/ML: Rapidly growing field. The Ornek/Ceylan group (Selcuk/Konya
   Technical University, Turkey) has been prolific. Key limitation:
   most studies use data from a single center. Multi-center validation
   and larger, more diverse datasets are needed.

6. NEC/ABDOMINAL: Most promising emerging application. Multiple studies
   show abdominal temperature differences in NEC. Automated RGB-D +
   thermal fusion systems (Payeur et al., 2020) show potential for
   real-time NEC screening. Feeding intolerance assessment via
   thermography is unexplored but theoretically possible.

7. RESPIRATORY: Most mature application area. Multiple validated
   approaches exist from Abbas (2011) through Maurya (2023). Main
   barrier is integration into clinical workflow.
