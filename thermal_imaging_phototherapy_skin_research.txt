================================================================================
THERMAL/INFRARED IMAGING IN NEONATAL PHOTOTHERAPY, JAUNDICE, AND SKIN CARE
Literature Review — Compiled March 2026
================================================================================

This document summarizes published research on using thermal/infrared cameras
during phototherapy, jaundice management, and skin-related conditions in neonates.


================================================================================
1. SKIN TEMPERATURE MONITORING DURING PHOTOTHERAPY FOR NEONATAL JAUNDICE
================================================================================

PAPER 1
-------
Title:    Effect of Phototherapy on Thermal Environment of the Newborn
Authors:  Smales OR
Year:     1978
Journal:  Archives of Disease in Childhood, 53(2), 172-174
DOI:      10.1136/adc.53.2.172
Link:     https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1545343/

Key Findings:
Oxygen consumption was studied in 40 newborn babies undergoing phototherapy for
hyperbilirubinaemia, both nursed in incubators and open cots. Exposure to
phototherapy caused a rise in temperature at the mattress surface. This was one
of the earliest studies demonstrating that phototherapy alters the thermal
environment around the neonate.


PAPER 2
-------
Title:    Responsiveness of Cutaneous Vasculature to Thermal Stimulation
          During Phototherapy in Neonatal Jaundice
Authors:  Pezzati M, Biagiotti R, Vangi V, Lombardi E, Wiechmann L, Rubaltelli FF
Year:     1999
Journal:  Acta Paediatrica, 88(10), 1096-1100
DOI:      10.1111/j.1651-2227.1999.tb00993.x
Link:     https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10485311/

Key Findings:
During open-bed phototherapy, skin blood flow increased significantly by ~70%,
without any significant change in skin or rectal temperature. The study examined
vasoregulatory reactions to dynamic changes in local skin temperature during
phototherapy using thermal stimulation with warm and cool air currents applied
to infants with physiological jaundice.

2. THERMAL EFFECTS OF LED vs CONVENTIONAL PHOTOTHERAPY ON NEONATAL SKIN
================================================================================

PAPER 3
-------
Title:    Body Temperature Changes of Newborns Under Fluorescent Versus LED
          Phototherapy
Authors:  Enver C, Gursoy T, Ovali F
Year:     2013
Journal:  Indian Journal of Pediatrics, 81(8), 751-754
DOI:      10.1007/s12098-013-1209-2
Link:     https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24037476/

Key Findings:
LED phototherapy of >=60 uW/cm2/nm intensity significantly increased body
temperature in hyperbilirubinemic newborns, whereas LED phototherapy at low
irradiances and conventional phototherapy with blue fluorescent light did not
cause significant hyperthermia. Mean body temperature was 36.7+/-0.1C in the
conventional fluorescent group, 36.6+/-0.2C in low-intensity LED, and
37.7+/-0.2C in high-intensity LED group.


PAPER 4
-------
Title:    Heating of Newborn Infants Due to Blue Light-Emitting Diode
          Fibreoptic Phototherapy Pads
Authors:  Ng PL, Carlisle T, Ly M, Morris SA
Year:     2017
Journal:  Neonatology, 112(2), 103-109
DOI:      10.1159/000464318
Link:     https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28445880/

Key Findings:
LED fibreoptic phototherapy pads are heated by high-intensity blue light, and
the thermal environment and temperature of babies should be monitored closely
during LED fibreoptic phototherapy. A temperature probe placed between the skin
and the pad will not accurately reflect core temperature during fibreoptic
phototherapy, raising concerns about undetected overheating.


PAPER 5
-------
Title:    Optimization of the Incubator Air Temperature During LED Phototherapy
          Treatment for the Preterm Infant
Authors:  Yassin FC, Delanaud S, Szcrupak C, et al.
Year:     2021
Journal:  European Journal of Pediatrics, 180, 277-281
DOI:      10.1007/s00431-020-03724-2
Link:     https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32594245/

Key Findings:
Phototherapy treatment increased the mean incubator roof temperature by 2.9C
(p<0.001) and the incubator air temperature by 1C (p<0.001). Analytical
calorimetry was used to calculate the additional energy received during
phototherapy and determine optimal incubator air temperature for thermoneutrality.
Optimal air temperature settings during phototherapy should be 0.51 to 1.25C
lower than standard references, for preterm infants weighing 500 to 2000g.

3. INFRARED THERMOGRAPHY IN NEONATAL THERMOREGULATION AND SKIN ASSESSMENT
================================================================================

PAPER 6
-------
Title:    Neonatal Skin Temperature Distribution Using Infra-Red Colour
          Thermography
Authors:  Clark RP, Stothers JK
Year:     1980
Journal:  Journal of Physiology, 302, 323-333
DOI:      10.1113/jphysiol.1980.sp013245
Link:     https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7411454/

Key Findings:
One of the earliest studies using infrared colour thermography to map neonatal
skin temperature. Seven infants were studied in a modified temperature-controlled
incubator. Linear regression showed that upper arm and upper thigh temperatures
represented the mean value most closely, while upper abdomen and head were both
warmer and less responsive to environmental change than the mean skin temperature.
This foundational work established regional temperature mapping in neonates.


PAPER 7
-------
Title:    Infrared Thermography for Detailed Registration of Thermoregulation
          in Premature Infants
Authors:  Heimann K, Jergus K, Abbas AK, Heussen N, Leonhardt S, Orlikowsky T
Year:     2013
Journal:  Journal of Perinatal Medicine, 41(5), 613-620
DOI:      10.1515/jpm-2012-0239
Link:     https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23443261/

Key Findings:
Evaluated skin temperature by using different measurement positions with
non-contact infrared thermography (IRT) in multiple body areas of preterm
infants. Ten premature infants (median 27 weeks GA; age 36 days; weight 1322g)
were studied, with measurements during incubator phases and standardized
skin-to-skin care sessions. Demonstrated that IRT provides detailed information
about regional temperature distribution and thermoregulatory responses in
premature infants.


PAPER 8
-------
Title:    Body Temperature in Premature Infants During the First Week of Life:
          Exploration Using Infrared Thermal Imaging
Authors:  Knobel-Dail RB, Holditch-Davis D, Sloane R, Guenther BD, Katz DJ
Year:     2017
Journal:  Journal of Thermal Biology, 69, 118-123
DOI:      10.1016/j.jtherbio.2017.06.005
Link:     https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29037371/

Key Findings:
Used infrared imaging to measure abdominal and foot temperature in extremely
premature infants in heated, humid incubators. Measurements were successful and
in close agreement with temperatures measured by skin thermistors (validated
using Bland-Altman analysis). This is significant because the insufficiently
keratinized epidermal barrier of premature skin makes the attachment of
traditional sensors difficult, making non-contact IRT particularly valuable.


PAPER 9
-------
Title:    Thermoregulation and Thermography in Neonatal Physiology and Disease
Authors:  Knobel RB, Guenther BD, Rice HE
Year:     2011
Journal:  Biological Research for Nursing, 13(3), 274-282
DOI:      10.1177/1099800411403467
Link:     https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21586499/

Key Findings:
Comprehensive review of thermoregulation physiology in neonates and the
potential of infrared thermography as a clinical tool. Infrared thermography
can detect pathological conditions involving inflammatory responses and can
provide visual thermal maps from major body parts. The review established the
theoretical and practical framework for using thermography in neonatal
physiology and disease detection.

4. RECENT ADVANCES: HIGH-ACCURACY IRT AND CONTINUOUS MONITORING IN NICU
================================================================================

PAPER 10
--------
Title:    Infrared Thermography with High Accuracy in a Neonatal Incubator
Authors:  Hamada K, Hirakawa E, Asano H, Hayashi H, Mine T, Ichikawa T, Nagata Y
Year:     2022
Journal:  Annals of Biomedical Engineering, 50(5), 529-539
DOI:      10.1007/s10439-022-02937-w
Link:     https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8890465/

Key Findings:
Addressed the critical challenge that IRT generally has insufficient accuracy for
medical use inside incubators. The proposed method improved the percentage of data
with mean absolute error (MAE) <0.3C to 93.1% compared to 4.2% using the standard
method. Two of three IRTs achieved MAE <0.1C under all conditions, providing high
accuracy suitable for both point measurements and continuous monitoring.


PAPER 11
--------
Title:    Continuous Monitoring Using Thermography Can Capture the Heat
          Oscillations Maintaining Body Temperature in Neonates
Authors:  Morimoto A, Nakamura S, Koyano K, et al.
Year:     2024
Journal:  Scientific Reports, 14, Article 10449
DOI:      10.1038/s41598-024-60718-y
Link:     https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-60718-y

Key Findings:
Used contactless infrared thermography to measure skin temperature every 15
seconds in neonates within an incubator. Analyzed thermal data from 27 neonates
(32 measurements), extracting two frequency components using the Savitzky-Golay
method. Demonstrated that continuous thermographic monitoring can capture
spontaneous thermal metabolism and heat oscillations, suggesting a role for
assessing thermoregulatory maturity in neonates.


PAPER 12
--------
Title:    Thermal Imaging-Based Core Peripheral Temperature Difference
          Measurement for Neonatal Monitoring in the NICU
Authors:  Zhang Y, Song D, et al.
Year:     2025
Journal:  Biomedical Optics Express, 16(3), 965
DOI:      (see link)
Link:     https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11919349/

Key Findings:
Proposed a thermal infrared (TIR)-based approach enabling non-contact, fully
automatic, and continuous core-peripheral temperature difference (CPTD) measurement
for neonates. A prospective study involving 40 preterm infants in a NICU
demonstrated feasibility of continuous, fully-automatic CPTD monitoring using deep
learning (high-resolution Transformer architecture). CPTD is a key biomarker for
assessing hemodynamic status and serves as an early warning indicator of potential
shock and severe infection.

5. AI AND DEEP LEARNING WITH NEONATAL THERMAL IMAGING
================================================================================

PAPER 13
--------
Title:    Health Status Detection of Neonates Using Infrared Thermography and
          Deep Convolutional Neural Networks
Authors:  Ornek AH, Ceylan M, Ervural S
Year:     2019
Journal:  Infrared Physics & Technology, 103, Article 103044
DOI:      10.1016/j.infrared.2019.103044
Link:     https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1350449519303123

Key Findings:
First study to combine IRT and deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for
detecting health status of neonates. Neonatal thermal images were taken in the
NICU of Selcuk University (Konya, Turkey) over a one-year period. The approach
demonstrated the potential for automated, non-contact health assessment of
neonates using thermal imaging combined with machine learning.

6. SCOPING REVIEW: COMPREHENSIVE OVERVIEW OF THERMAL IMAGING IN NEONATAL CARE
================================================================================

PAPER 14
--------
Title:    Thermal Imaging Applications in Neonatal Care: A Scoping Review
Authors:  Rambaud-Althaus C, et al. (multiple authors)
Year:     2019
Journal:  BMC Pregnancy and Childbirth, 19, Article 388
DOI:      10.1186/s12884-019-2533-y
Link:     https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6814124/

Key Findings:
Comprehensive scoping review examining thermal imaging across all neonatal care
applications. From 442 search hits, 21 studies met inclusion criteria. A
significant proportion (n=9) were published in the most recent 8 years, showing
growing research interest. The review highlighted that although thermal imaging
in neonatal care began in the 1970s with promising results, no large prospective
studies have yet examined both benefits and barriers to clinical adoption.
Thermal imaging is identified as a completely non-invasive and non-contact method
of assessing emitted temperature, but not yet a standard method for neonatal
monitoring.

7. INFRARED VEIN VISUALIZATION FOR PERIPHERAL VENOUS ACCESS IN NEONATES
================================================================================

PAPER 15
--------
Title:    Infrared Vein Visualisation Devices for Ease of Intravenous Access in
          Children: Hope Versus Hype
Authors:  (multiple authors)
Year:     2023
Journal:  Anaesthesiology Intensive Therapy (AIT)
DOI:      (see link)
Link:     https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10158443/

Key Findings:
Reviewed the evidence on infrared vein visualization devices in pediatric and
neonatal populations. Near-infrared light allows visualization of small (0.2mm)
and superficial (up to 8mm) veins. Most studies did not demonstrate major impact
in the general pediatric population, though the devices may be beneficial in
children with difficult intravenous access. A limitation is that the 2D display
prevents estimation of vessel depth.


PAPER 16
--------
Title:    Efficacy of Infrared Vein Visualization Versus Standard Technique for
          Peripheral Venous Cannulation in Infant and Toddler Populations:
          A Randomized Study
Authors:  (multiple authors)
Year:     2023
Journal:  Children (MDPI), 10(10), 1680
DOI:      (see link)
Link:     https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10605772/

Key Findings:
Randomized study examining efficacy of infrared vein visualization in infants and
toddlers. The vein finder group had significantly lower pain scores and shorter
crying durations, with improved behavioural states including longer durations in
deep sleep and reduced active alert states. However, success rate improvement was
not statistically significant across the general population studied.


================================================================================
SUMMARY OF IDENTIFIED RESEARCH GAPS
================================================================================

Based on this literature search, the following areas have LIMITED or NO published
research specifically using thermal/infrared cameras:

1. SKIN INTEGRITY ASSESSMENT VIA THERMAL IMAGING IN NEONATES
   - No studies found specifically using thermal cameras to detect or monitor
     medical adhesive-related skin injuries (MARSI) in neonates
   - MARSI is a well-documented problem (9.25-41.5% incidence in NICU), but
     thermal imaging has not been applied to this use case

2. DIAPER DERMATITIS DETECTION
   - No published studies found using thermal cameras for early detection of
     diaper dermatitis or skin breakdown in the diaper area of neonates
   - This represents an unexplored application area

3. SKIN MATURATION ASSESSMENT VIA THERMOGRAPHY
   - While premature infant thermoregulation is well studied with IRT, no
     studies specifically use thermal imaging to assess epidermal maturation
     or barrier function development over time

4. BREAST MILK TEMPERATURE MONITORING
   - Only consumer products (MilkChecker, Coroflo) exist; no published
     clinical research on using thermal cameras for breast milk temperature
     assessment during feeding

5. REAL-TIME THERMAL MONITORING DURING PHOTOTHERAPY
   - While the thermal effects of phototherapy are documented, no studies
     have proposed or tested a continuous thermal imaging surveillance system
     specifically for monitoring neonatal temperature during phototherapy

These gaps represent significant opportunities for NeoTherm innovation.

RELEVANCE TO NEOTHERM PROJECT
================================================================================

Key takeaways for NeoTherm development:

- PHOTOTHERAPY THERMAL RISK IS REAL: High-intensity LED phototherapy (>=60
  uW/cm2/nm) significantly increases body temperature. Incubator air temp
  should be reduced by 0.5-1.25C during phototherapy. Continuous thermal
  monitoring during phototherapy is an unmet clinical need.

- IRT ACCURACY IN INCUBATORS IS ACHIEVABLE: Hamada et al. (2022) achieved
  MAE <0.1C inside incubators with proper calibration methods, validating
  the technical feasibility of NeoTherm's approach.

- NON-CONTACT IS CRITICAL: Premature neonatal skin is insufficiently
  keratinized, making sensor attachment difficult and potentially injurious.
  Thermal cameras eliminate this problem entirely.

- SKIN CONDITION DETECTION IS AN UNTAPPED OPPORTUNITY: No published research
  applies thermal imaging to MARSI, diaper dermatitis, or skin maturation
  monitoring in neonates -- all potential NeoTherm differentiators.

- CPTD MONITORING FOR SEPSIS: The 2025 Zhang et al. paper validates
  automated core-peripheral temperature difference monitoring using deep
  learning + thermal cameras for early sepsis detection in NICU -- directly
  aligned with NeoTherm's vision.
