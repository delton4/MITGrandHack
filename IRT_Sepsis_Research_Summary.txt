SECTION 1: WHAT IS IRT AND HOW DOES IT DETECT SEPSIS?
================================================================================

--- THE PHYSICS ---

Every object above absolute zero emits infrared radiation proportional to its
surface temperature (Stefan-Boltzmann's law). The human body naturally emits
infrared in the 8–14 μm wavelength range.

An IRT camera is entirely passive — it emits nothing. It reads naturally
emitted heat from the body surface, pixel by pixel, and converts it into a
real-time pseudocolour temperature map.

There is NO ionising radiation. This is a fundamental safety advantage over
X-ray or CT, particularly for neonates.

--- THE PHYSIOLOGY: WHAT SEPSIS DOES TO TEMPERATURE ---

In sepsis, systemic infection triggers a cascade that disrupts blood
distribution:

  Step 1 — Inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β) cause central
           vasodilation, dropping systemic vascular resistance.

  Step 2 — To protect vital organs, the sympathetic nervous system triggers
           peripheral vasoconstriction (hands, feet, legs, skin).

  Step 3 — Reduced peripheral blood flow means less warm blood reaching
           extremities. The periphery cools while the core stays warm.

  Step 4 — A measurable Central-Peripheral Temperature Difference (CPTD)
           develops. This is what IRT detects.

Validated clinical threshold: >2°C difference sustained for ≥4 hours
= early marker for sepsis/septic shock.

--- WHAT IRT ACTUALLY MEASURES ---

  - Central-peripheral temperature difference (CPTD): degree of peripheral
    vasoconstriction and shock severity
  - Toe/fingertip temperature: distal perfusion (lowest in shock)
  - Thermal heterogeneity: uneven microvascular flow (mottling made objective)
  - Rate of temperature change over time: trajectory of deterioration
  - Spatial skin temperature gradient maps (e.g. leg thermal patterns)

--- WHY IRT DETECTS SEPSIS EARLIER THAN CLINICAL SIGNS ---

  Peripheral vasoconstriction → skin cooling  ← IRT detects HERE
           ↓
  Blood pressure starts falling
           ↓
  Tachycardia, altered mental status, reduced urine output
           ↓
  Clinical sepsis recognised by bedside team   ← Currently detected HERE

Vats et al. demonstrated IRT predicted septic shock up to 5 HOURS before
onset in paediatric ICU patients (AUROC 0.81 ± 0.06).

SECTION 2: EVIDENCE FROM THE 6 REVIEWED STUDIES
================================================================================

--- STUDY 1: Du et al. (2026) — Emergency Medicine International ---
Systematic Review, 11 studies, PRISMA-registered

Key findings:
  - Cannot make conclusive superiority claims vs. standard diagnostics
    (only 5 studies with direct comparisons)
  - IRT positioned as a viable adjunctive tool for early detection and
    severity stratification
  - Advantages over mottling score: objective, not skin-colour-dependent,
    non-invasive, no consumables, no radiation, no specialised training

Accuracy stats:
  - Vats et al. LSTM deep learning (paediatric ICU): AUROC 0.81 ± 0.06
    at 5h pre-shock
  - Nagori et al. ML on abdomen-thigh gradients: 75–77% accuracy
  - Luo et al. leg thermal heterogeneity: AUROC 0.866 for mortality
  - Luo et al. deep learning ResNet: 84% sensitivity, 91% specificity
  - Amson et al.: core-to-fingertip >7°C → Day-8 mortality aHR 3.2
  - Kazune et al.: anterior thigh thermal patterns predicted 28-day mortality
  - Hasanin et al.: toe ≤25.5°C at 12h → 70% NPV (inferior to lactate/CRT)
  - Ferraris et al.: fingertip IRT showed no prognostic value

Neonatal reference:
  Leante-Castellanos: persistent CPTD >2°C for ≥4h is a sensitive early
  diagnostic marker for LATE-ONSET NEONATAL SEPSIS.

--- STUDY 2: Hasanin et al. (2024) — BMC Anesthesiology ---
Prospective observational, n=56 post-operative septic shock patients

Key findings:
  - IRT explicitly INFERIOR to serum lactate and CRT for mortality prediction
  - Toe temperature at 12h: AUC 0.72, sensitivity 92%, specificity 47%
  - Lactate AUC: up to 0.81 at 12h; PPV up to 100% at >4.3 mmol/L
  - CRT AUC: up to 0.81 at 12h
  - Toe temperature negatively correlated with lactate (r = −0.24, p=0.002)
  - 73% mortality rate in cohort
  - Suggested as complement to existing tools, not replacement

--- STUDY 3: Stanley et al. (2024) — Frontiers in Medicine ---
Systematic Review, 30 studies from EDs and ICUs

Key findings:
  - Inconsistent results for adult septic shock; stronger evidence in paediatrics
  - IRT described as "non-contact, non-invasive, non-ionising"
  - High-quality diagnostic validation studies still required
  - Paediatric thermoregulatory immaturity = use with caution until more data
  - Acclimatisation period (10 min) needed in most studies
  - Machine learning combinations show greatest promise

Neonatal finding (NICU):
  - Barcat (2017): IRT used in NICU to study sleep onset in preterm neonates.
    Narrower CPTDs associated with shorter wakefulness episodes (vasodilation
    promoting sleep onset). Novel observation in preterm neonatal population.
  - Safety caution: "IRT should be used with caution in children until more
    data are available to assist interpretation"

--- STUDY 4: Lath et al. (2025) — International Journal of Emergency Medicine ---
Prospective observational, n=187 patients (104 sepsis, 83 septic shock), India

Key findings:
  - IRT gradients showed lower sensitivity and AUC than mottling/CRT data
  - Core-to-knee at 0h: AUC 0.621, sensitivity 31.7%, specificity 88.2%
  - Core-to-great-toe at 0h: AUC 0.603, sensitivity 65.9%, specificity 54.8%
  - 28-day mortality: no gradient reached significance (AUCs 0.5–0.6)
  - 7-day and 48-hour mortality: core-to-knee correlated significantly
  - Core-to-index finger correlated with: MAP (r=−0.286), lactate (r=0.281),
    SOFA (r=0.242), qSOFA (r=0.167), vasopressor requirement (p=0.020)
  - Advantage in dark-skinned populations where mottling score is unreliable
  - Concluded: good adjunct for assessing circulation, not standalone tool

--- STUDY 5: Ferraris et al. (2018) — PLOS ONE ---
Prospective observational, n=46 ICU septic shock patients, Lyon, France

Key findings:
  - NEGATIVE study: IRT did not predict ICU or 28-day mortality
  - IRT knee temperatures: no significant difference between survivors and
    non-survivors at H0 or H6
  - Mottling patients had lower knee temperatures (30.7°C vs 33.2°C, p=0.01)
    but mottling score itself did not predict 28-day mortality (p=0.75)
  - Concluded IRT "remains in medical and scientific research field"
  - Acknowledged as first negative study, balancing more optimistic literature

--- STUDY 6: Kazune et al. (2023) — Bioengineering ---
Prospective observational, n=81 ICU septic shock patients, Latvia

Key findings:
  - Skin temperature alone: no association with 28-day mortality (p=0.18)
  - Combined with mottling score: significant interaction (p=0.01)
    → survivors with mottling 2–3 had significantly higher skin temperatures
  - Big toe temperature: significantly associated with survival
    Survivors median 31.0°C vs non-survivors 26.0°C; OR 1.3 per °C (p=0.002)
  - 74.5–99.5% of skin temperature variation was between-subject variation
    → individual baseline differences dominate; absolute cut-offs are weak
  - SAFETY NOTE: Active dynamic thermography (applying cold stimulus) carries
    risk in hypoperfused patients. Passive IRT = no safety concerns.
  - Concluded: IRT adds objective, non-contact dimension to mottling score

SECTION 3: OVERALL EVALUATION OF IRT FOR SEPSIS
================================================================================

Q: IS IRT BETTER THAN STANDARD METHODS?
  No study demonstrates IRT is definitively superior to serum lactate, CRT,
  SOFA, or APACHE II. Two studies (Hasanin, Ferraris) explicitly show it is
  inferior or non-significant. Best framing: useful ADJUNCT, not replacement.

  Where IRT adds value over standard methods:
    - Dark-skinned patients (mottling score unreliable)
    - Resource-limited settings (lactate unavailable)
    - Infection control / non-contact requirements (pandemic settings)
    - Early warning: detects physiological changes hours before clinical signs
    - Continuous monitoring without disturbing the patient

Q: HOW ACCURATE IS IT?
  Heterogeneous — highly dependent on model and patient group:

  Best:     Paediatric ICU deep learning thermal video: AUC 0.81
            ResNet model for mortality: 84% sensitivity / 91% specificity
  Moderate: Toe temperature at 12h: AUC 0.72, NPV 70%
            Core-to-knee gradient for 7-day mortality: AUC 0.62
  Poor:     Core-to-toe for 28-day mortality: AUCs 0.5–0.6
            Fingertip IRT in COVID-19 sepsis: no predictive value

Q: IS IT REALISTIC TO OFFER IN HOSPITALS?
  Technically YES — devices (FLIR C2, E8, A600) are commercially available,
  portable, no consumables, ~£500–£3,000.

  Barriers to routine deployment:
    - Needs temperature-controlled room (~22°C)
    - 10-minute patient acclimatisation required
    - No validated clinical protocol currently exists
    - Manual image analysis without AI is time-consuming
    - No formal cost-effectiveness studies published

Q: IS IT WORTH USING vs. EMPIRICAL TREATMENT?
  No — does not replace empirical treatment decisions. Sepsis protocols
  (lactate, cultures, antibiotics, fluids) should not be delayed for IRT.

  Role is in:
    - Monitoring deterioration before overt shock
    - Risk stratification among admitted patients
    - Ruling out near-term mortality (toe >25.5°C → NPV ~70%)
    - Supplementing rather than replacing CRT and mottling score

SECTION 4: NEONATAL SEPSIS — THE CASE FOR IRT IN THE NICU
================================================================================

--- SCALE OF THE PROBLEM (US) ---

  Global:
    - ~1.3 million neonatal sepsis cases per year worldwide
    - ~203,000–225,000 neonatal deaths annually from sepsis
    - 3rd leading cause of neonatal death globally
    - Global incidence: 2,824 per 100,000 live births

  United States:
    - Neonates have the HIGHEST rate of ICU admissions for sepsis of any
      age group in the US
    - 36% of premature babies born before 28 weeks have ≥1 episode of
      bacteremia during initial hospitalisation
    - 33% of infants born <28 weeks will acquire sepsis during NICU stay
    - Sepsis hospital stays for neonates decreased 2016–2021, but
      in-hospital mortality rates INCREASED over the same period

--- MORTALITY (US) ---

  All septic infants (US):                    ~10%
  Infants with any comorbidity:               up to 30%
  Premature infants <28 weeks (bacteremia):   up to 50%
  Post-discharge mortality vs. non-sepsis:    RR 2.1

--- LONG-TERM OUTCOMES IN SURVIVORS ---

  Neurodevelopmental impairment:
    - 35% of culture-proven sepsis survivors vs. 17% non-sepsis group
    - Risk of cerebral palsy / neurosensory deficits: OR 3.18
      (95% CI 2.29–4.41)
    - Cerebral palsy: OR 1.4–1.7
    - Developmental delay: OR 1.3–1.6
    - Vision impairment: OR 1.3–2.2
    - Impaired head growth at 2 years corrected age

  Other major morbidities (preterm + sepsis):
    - Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD): 30.8%
    - Periventricular leukomalacia (PVL): 22.4%
    - Retinopathy of prematurity (ROP): 10.5%
    - Intraventricular haemorrhage (IVH): significantly elevated

  Mechanisms of brain injury:
    Systemic inflammation → blood-brain barrier disruption → microglial
    activation → white matter injury → cerebral palsy, intellectual
    disability, learning difficulties, hearing and vision loss

--- ECONOMIC BURDEN (US) ---

  Total US cost of treating severe sepsis in children (2016): $7.31 billion
    (up from $4.8B in 2005)
  Annual neonatal sepsis healthcare costs:                    $1.1 billion/yr
  Additional cost per late-onset sepsis episode (preterm):   ~$32,954
  Hospital cost per 1,000 births — current risk assessment:  $182,491
  Hospital cost per 1,000 births — enhanced observation:     $85,879
    → Smarter screening alone = 53% cost reduction
  Neonatal sepsis patients cost 2–3x more per admission than adult patients

--- THE ANTIBIOTIC OVERTREATMENT CRISIS ---

  - 50 to >100 term/late-preterm neonates receive empiric IV antibiotics
    for every 1 PROVEN case of early-onset sepsis
  - This is up to 250x higher than actual incidence of proven EOS
  - Consequences:
      · Antimicrobial resistance
      · Microbiome disruption → increased chronic disease risk
      · Unnecessary hospitalisation and painful procedures
      · Disrupted family bonding
      · Significant healthcare cost

  Smarter early detection tools like IRT could dramatically reduce this
  overtreatment burden.

--- WHY THE NICU IS THE IDEAL ENVIRONMENT FOR IRT ---

  1. High-risk population already identified: premature babies in NICU are
     captive, already monitored, and at 10–20x the sepsis risk of term babies

  2. Late-onset sepsis dominates (94.9% of all episodes occur after 72 hours,
     during NICU admission) — clear monitoring window

  3. No radiation, non-contact: ideal for fragile preterm infants; no added
     burden on the baby

  4. Diagnostic marker already validated: CPTD >2°C for ≥4h has clinical
     backing for late-onset neonatal sepsis detection (Leante-Castellanos)

  5. NICU environment solves IRT's main practical barriers:
      · Incubators are already temperature-controlled
      · Babies are immobile and undressed
      · No acclimatisation problem
      · Continuous monitoring is standard of care — IRT fits seamlessly

  6. Technology is already demonstrated:
      · Continuous NICU IRT monitoring shown with <0.55°C accuracy
      · Respiratory rate, skin temperature, and CPTD all measurable
        non-contact and continuously
      · IRT-incubator compatible systems exist

  7. Why neonatal IRT may outperform adult IRT:
      · High surface-area-to-volume ratio → skin temp more sensitive to
        perfusion changes
      · Immature thermoregulation → more pronounced peripheral cooling
        response to infection
      · Cannot communicate symptoms → objective monitoring is critical

--- THE CASE IN SUMMARY ---

  The consequences of missing neonatal sepsis are catastrophic: up to 50%
  mortality in the most premature infants, and 35% of survivors with
  permanent neurodevelopmental impairment. The current standard produces
  antibiotic overtreatment at 50–250x the rate of true sepsis.

  IRT offers a non-invasive, non-contact, non-ionising, continuous,
  real-time monitoring tool that detects the physiological signature of
  sepsis — peripheral vasoconstriction — hours before clinical deterioration
  becomes apparent. In the NICU, where the environment already meets IRT's
  technical requirements, the barrier to implementation is low and the
  potential clinical and economic benefit is substantial.

  Current evidence is insufficient to support IRT as a standalone diagnostic.
  The evidence base strongly supports IRT as a continuous adjunct monitoring
  tool in neonatal ICU — particularly when combined with AI-assisted
  analysis of CPTD trends over time.
